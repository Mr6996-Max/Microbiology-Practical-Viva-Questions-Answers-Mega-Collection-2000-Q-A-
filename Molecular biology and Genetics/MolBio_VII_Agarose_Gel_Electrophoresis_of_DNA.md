# Molecular Biology — Subtopic VII
## Agarose Gel Electrophoresis of DNA
### 50 Viva Questions with Detailed Answers

---

**1. What is agarose gel electrophoresis, and what is its primary purpose in molecular biology?**
Agarose gel electrophoresis is a technique used to separate, identify, and analyze nucleic acid molecules (DNA or RNA) based on their size and charge, by migrating them through a matrix of agarose gel under the influence of an applied electric field. It is one of the most fundamental and routinely used techniques in molecular biology, serving purposes such as verifying the size and integrity of DNA fragments after restriction enzyme digestion, confirming successful PCR amplification, checking the quality and size of extracted genomic or plasmid DNA, analyzing restriction fragment length polymorphisms (RFLPs), and preparing specific DNA fragments for further downstream applications (e.g., gel purification/extraction).

**2. What is agarose, and what is its origin and chemical nature?**
Agarose is a linear polysaccharide polymer derived from agar, which is itself extracted from the cell walls of certain red algae (rhodophytes) such as Gelidium and Gracilaria species. Chemically, agarose is composed of repeating units of a disaccharide (agarobiose) consisting of alternating D-galactose and 3,6-anhydro-L-galactose residues linked by alternating α-1,3 and β-1,4 glycosidic bonds. Upon heating in an aqueous buffer and subsequent cooling, agarose forms a porous gel matrix through non-covalent hydrogen bonding between the polysaccharide chains, creating a meshwork of interconnected pores through which nucleic acid molecules can migrate during electrophoresis.

**3. What is the principle of DNA migration in agarose gel electrophoresis?**
The principle is based on two fundamental properties of DNA: first, DNA is a negatively charged molecule at neutral pH due to the phosphate groups in its backbone (each nucleotide carries one negative charge at physiological pH), and second, when placed in an electric field, negatively charged molecules migrate toward the positively charged electrode (anode). The rate of migration through the agarose gel matrix is primarily determined by the size of the DNA molecule—smaller DNA fragments migrate through the pores of the gel matrix more easily and quickly than larger fragments, which are more impeded by the gel matrix, resulting in size-based separation over time, with smaller fragments migrating further from the wells (toward the anode) than larger fragments within a given run time.

**4. Why does DNA migrate toward the anode (positive electrode) and not toward the cathode?**
DNA migrates toward the anode because the phosphate groups along the DNA backbone carry a net negative charge at the neutral pH conditions typically used for electrophoresis (the pKa of the phosphate groups is around 1, so they are essentially fully deprotonated/negatively charged at pH 7–8). In an electric field, negatively charged species are attracted to and migrate toward the positively charged electrode (anode), which is typically the red electrode in standard electrophoresis setups; this is why the sample wells are loaded at the cathode (negative/black electrode) end of the gel, so that DNA migrates away from the wells and toward the far (anode/red) end during the run.

**5. What factors determine the rate of DNA migration through an agarose gel?**
The rate of DNA migration is influenced by: (1) DNA size — smaller fragments migrate faster than larger ones through the gel matrix (this is the primary separation principle); (2) agarose concentration — higher agarose concentrations produce smaller pore sizes, resulting in slower overall migration and better resolution of smaller fragments, while lower concentrations produce larger pores, better suited for larger fragment separation; (3) applied voltage — higher voltages increase migration speed but can reduce resolution (especially for larger fragments) and generate excessive heat; (4) buffer type and ionic strength — the buffer provides ions for current conduction, and its concentration affects the efficiency of charge conduction and heat generation; (5) DNA conformation — for a given molecular weight, the three-dimensional conformation of DNA (linear, relaxed circular/nicked, or supercoiled) affects its migration rate (supercoiled DNA migrates faster than linear DNA of the same size, which migrates faster than relaxed circular DNA).

**6. What is the role of the running buffer in agarose gel electrophoresis, and what buffers are commonly used?**
The running buffer serves to maintain a stable pH during electrophoresis (buffering capacity prevents pH changes that could affect DNA charge and integrity), provides ions to conduct the electrical current through the gel (without ions, current cannot flow and electrophoresis cannot occur), and maintains the ionic environment needed for DNA stability. Commonly used buffers include TAE (Tris-Acetate-EDTA, pH 8.3), which is the most widely used, offering good resolution and compatibility with downstream applications (e.g., gel extraction/cloning) due to lower salt content; TBE (Tris-Borate-EDTA, pH 8.3), which provides better buffering capacity and resolution for smaller fragments but the borate inhibits some downstream enzymes; and TPE (Tris-Phosphate-EDTA), less common, used in specific applications.

**7. What is the role of EDTA in the electrophoresis buffer?**
EDTA (Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid) is a chelating agent included in the electrophoresis buffer (as part of TAE or TBE) to chelate divalent metal ions, particularly Mg²⁺ and Ca²⁺. This is important because DNase enzymes (which can degrade DNA during sample preparation and handling) require divalent cations as cofactors for their enzymatic activity; by chelating these ions, EDTA inhibits DNase activity, thereby protecting DNA integrity during electrophoresis. EDTA also helps maintain a stable buffering environment and is present in DNA storage solutions (e.g., TE buffer) for the same DNA protection purpose.

**8. How do you prepare an agarose gel, and what are the key steps in the process?**
To prepare an agarose gel, the desired weight of agarose powder (calculated based on the required percentage, e.g., for a 1% gel in 100 mL buffer: weigh 1 g of agarose) is added to the appropriate volume of 1× electrophoresis buffer (e.g., 1× TAE) in a suitable flask/conical. The mixture is heated (typically in a microwave oven, in short bursts with swirling to ensure even heating) until the agarose dissolves completely and the solution becomes clear and transparent without any undissolved particles, being careful to avoid boiling over. The dissolved agarose is cooled to approximately 50–60°C (until the flask is comfortable to hold for a few seconds) before adding intercalating dye (e.g., ethidium bromide or safer alternatives), pouring into a gel casting tray fitted with a comb to create wells, and allowing to solidify at room temperature (typically 20–30 minutes) before removing the comb and submerging in the electrophoresis tank buffer.

**9. What percentage of agarose is typically used, and how does this choice relate to the size of DNA fragments being analyzed?**
Agarose gel percentage is chosen based on the expected size range of DNA fragments to be resolved: higher percentages (2–3%) produce smaller pore sizes and are used for resolving smaller DNA fragments (e.g., 100 bp to 1 kb range, such as in PCR products or after restriction digestion into small fragments), providing better resolution in the low molecular weight range. Lower percentages (0.5–0.8%) produce larger pores, suitable for resolving large DNA fragments (e.g., 5 kb to 50 kb range, such as genomic DNA fragments or large plasmids), though very large fragments may require specialized techniques like pulsed-field gel electrophoresis (PFGE). A standard 1% gel is broadly used for general purposes and provides reasonable resolution in the 0.5–10 kb range commonly encountered in routine molecular biology.

**10. What is ethidium bromide (EtBr), and what is its role in agarose gel electrophoresis?**
Ethidium bromide is a planar, aromatic, polycyclic compound that intercalates (inserts) between the base pairs of double-stranded DNA (and to a lesser extent single-stranded DNA and RNA) by sliding its flat ring structure between adjacent base pairs, which causes the DNA-EtBr complex to fluoresce intensely (approximately 20–25-fold increase in fluorescence) when exposed to ultraviolet (UV) light (excitation maximum ~302 nm; emission maximum ~590 nm, emitting orange-red light). It can be added to the molten agarose before pouring (pre-staining) or the gel can be stained in an EtBr solution after electrophoresis (post-staining), allowing visualization of DNA bands as bright orange-red bands against a dark background under UV transillumination.

**11. What are the safety concerns associated with ethidium bromide, and what safer alternatives are available?**
Ethidium bromide is a potent mutagen (intercalating between DNA base pairs, it can cause frameshift mutations during DNA replication) and is classified as a suspected carcinogen and possible teratogen, requiring careful handling (gloves, minimal direct contact, proper disposal of EtBr-containing waste according to hazardous waste regulations, and UV protection for eyes/skin during gel visualization). Safer fluorescent dye alternatives include SYBR Safe (Life Technologies), SYBR Green I, GelRed, GelGreen, and Nancy-520, which have significantly lower mutagenicity profiles (though no stain should be considered completely without risk), are often as sensitive as or more sensitive than EtBr, are compatible with most downstream applications, and can typically be visualized under blue light or UV with appropriate filter sets.

**12. What is a DNA ladder (molecular weight marker), and why is it always included in at least one lane of every gel?**
A DNA ladder (or molecular weight marker/size standard) is a mixture of DNA fragments of known, defined sizes (e.g., 100 bp, 200 bp, 300 bp… 1000 bp for a "100 bp ladder," or 250 bp, 500 bp, 750 bp, 1 kb, 2 kb… 10 kb for a "1 kb ladder"), which is loaded alongside the test samples in one or more lanes of every gel. It serves as an internal reference/standard that allows estimation of the sizes of unknown DNA fragments in adjacent sample lanes by comparing the position of each sample band relative to the bands of known sizes in the ladder lane, since all bands migrate in the same electric field and through the same gel matrix under identical conditions; without a size standard, no size information can be derived from the position of a band on the gel.

**13. What is a DNA loading dye, and what is its purpose?**
A DNA loading dye (also called gel loading buffer) is typically a mixture added to DNA samples before loading onto the gel, containing: a dense compound (usually glycerol or sucrose) to increase the density of the sample, causing it to sink to the bottom of the well rather than dispersing into the buffer upon loading; one or more tracking dyes (commonly bromophenol blue and/or xylene cyanol) that are visible to the naked eye and migrate through the gel at defined rates (approximately equivalent to certain size DNA fragments, depending on the agarose percentage), allowing monitoring of electrophoresis progress without exposing the gel to UV light; and usually EDTA to chelate divalent cations and protect the DNA.

**14. At approximately what size does bromophenol blue co-migrate in a 1% agarose gel, and why is this useful?**
Bromophenol blue co-migrates at approximately 300–500 bp (commonly cited as ~300 bp) in a standard 1% agarose TAE gel, though this value varies depending on the exact gel percentage and buffer conditions; xylene cyanol co-migrates at approximately 4–5 kb under similar conditions. These known migration positions are useful because they allow the experimenter to visually monitor the progress of electrophoresis in real time (without disturbing the gel) and to judge when to stop the run—for example, stopping when bromophenol blue has migrated approximately three-quarters of the way down the gel ensures that DNA fragments in the 300–500 bp range and above are well-resolved, while stopping earlier preserves the separation achieved for larger fragments.

**15. What voltage is typically used for routine agarose gel electrophoresis, and what are the consequences of using excessively high voltage?**
For routine agarose gel electrophoresis, voltages in the range of 80–120 V (for standard 7–10 cm gel systems) are commonly used, providing a reasonable balance between run time (typically 30–60 minutes for a standard gel) and resolution. Excessively high voltages (e.g., >150 V) can cause several problems: increased heat generation (Joule heating) due to the higher current passing through the gel, which can cause uneven melting or distortion of the gel, band smearing (particularly for larger DNA fragments), reduced resolution (especially in the high molecular weight range), and potential degradation of DNA; excessive heat can also cause the buffer to heat and even boil, further distorting results and potentially cracking the gel tray.

**16. Why are DNA samples heated or denatured with EDTA/formamide in some applications but not in routine DNA analysis?**
In routine DNA analysis (e.g., checking PCR products, restriction digests, or extracted DNA), double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) is run in its native, non-denatured state under non-denaturing conditions, where size-based separation under the applied electric field is sufficient for the analytical purpose. Denaturation (using formaldehyde or formamide) is specifically required for RNA gel electrophoresis (to prevent secondary structure formation in RNA, which would affect its migration based on conformation rather than size alone, complicating size estimation) and for certain DNA applications where it is important to separate by size regardless of complementary strand re-annealing (e.g., Southern blotting applications may involve denaturing conditions for transfer and hybridization).

**17. How would you prepare and load a DNA sample onto the gel without the sample spilling out of the well?**
The gel must be fully submerged in the running buffer (to a level just covering the gel surface) before loading, and the buffer level must not be too high (excessive buffer above the gel surface can cause the sample to float out of the wells). Samples are typically mixed with loading dye (which contains glycerol or sucrose to increase density) in a tube before loading, and loaded using a fine-tipped micropipette (or gel loading tips) held at the surface of the well mouth, carefully dispensing the sample slowly so that the dense sample sinks to the bottom of the well under gravity, without introducing air bubbles (which would cause the sample to be displaced upward, out of the well). Consistent, gentle pipetting is key.

**18. What is a "smear" on an agarose gel, and what might cause it in the context of a DNA sample lane?**
A smear (a diffuse, continuous background of fluorescent material extending from the well downward, rather than discrete bands) in a sample lane can indicate several problems: degradation of the DNA sample (nuclease contamination breaks the DNA into a heterogeneous population of fragments of many different sizes, rather than a discrete population), RNA contamination in a DNA sample (RNA migrates as a smear due to its small size and heterogeneity), excessive DNA loading (too much total DNA can produce a smear due to overloading of the lane), or poor-quality DNA extraction with significant protein/contaminant carryover that can cause aggregation and smearing. For a freshly isolated genomic DNA sample, a smear is the expected appearance (since genomic DNA is fragmented into many different sizes during extraction), while a smear of a PCR product or restriction digest should prompt investigation for degradation or other technical issues.

**19. What would "overloading" of DNA in a gel lane look like, and what are its consequences for interpretation?**
Overloading of a gel lane (adding too much total DNA) typically results in: extremely bright, thick bands that may be saturated (making precise size estimation difficult), distortion of the band (bowing upward or downward, or the center of the band migrating at a different rate than its edges), smearing of the band, and particularly for the wells and lane margins, a general "bleeding" of fluorescence that may obscure adjacent lanes or make weak bands in the same lane impossible to visualize against the bright background. As a consequence, overloaded lanes cannot be used for accurate band size estimation (because band migration may be altered), and it may be difficult to determine whether all bands are distinct, making quantitative comparison to other lanes unreliable.

**20. Why must the agarose gel be allowed to completely solidify before removing the comb and loading samples?**
The agarose gel must be completely solidified (which typically takes 20–30 minutes at room temperature, or can be accelerated by placing at 4°C) before removing the comb to ensure that the wells formed by the comb teeth are properly set and maintain their shape when the comb is removed; if the gel is not fully solidified and the comb is removed prematurely, the well walls may collapse or deform, resulting in wells that do not hold a defined shape, causing samples to spread into adjacent lanes, producing distorted bands and making quantification/size estimation impossible.

**21. How is the size of an unknown DNA band estimated from an agarose gel?**
The size of an unknown band is estimated by comparison to the known bands of the DNA ladder in an adjacent lane, using the semi-logarithmic relationship between DNA fragment size and migration distance (a plot of the logarithm of the base pair size of each ladder band versus the distance migrated from the well produces an approximately linear relationship, from which the size of an unknown band can be interpolated). In practice, this can be done by hand (measuring migration distances with a ruler and plotting/interpolating manually or using a regression line) or more commonly using gel imaging software that automatically calculates sizes by fitting a standard curve to the ladder bands and interpolating the positions of the unknown bands.

**22. Why is there a semi-logarithmic (log-linear) relationship between DNA fragment size and migration distance in agarose gel electrophoresis?**
The semi-logarithmic relationship arises from the nature of the sieving effect of the agarose gel matrix on DNA migration: as the size of a DNA fragment increases, the rate at which the increased molecular friction/resistance of the gel slows it down is not linear but rather proportional to the logarithm of its size (for linear double-stranded DNA within a certain size range), meaning that the distance migrated is inversely proportional to the log of the molecular size. Physically, this reflects the increasing difficulty of threading longer DNA molecules through the interconnected pores of the gel matrix — a molecule twice as long requires significantly more than twice the force to migrate the same distance, consistent with a power-law/logarithmic relationship.

**23. What is the difference in migration behavior between supercoiled (Form I), relaxed circular/nicked (Form II), and linear (Form III) DNA of the same molecular weight?**
For the same molecular weight, these three conformations of a circular DNA molecule (e.g., a plasmid) migrate differently through an agarose gel: supercoiled DNA (Form I), which is tightly coiled and compact due to the strain/twisting in the double helix, migrates fastest (furthest from the well) because its compact shape encounters less resistance as it passes through the gel matrix. Linear DNA (Form III), which is elongated but not coiled, migrates at an intermediate rate, typically corresponding to its "true" molecular weight when compared to a linear DNA ladder standard. Relaxed circular/nicked DNA (Form II), which is a large, open, floppy ring structure that is difficult to thread through the gel pores and that presents a large effective radius, migrates slowest (closest to the well) despite having the same molecular weight as Forms I and III.

**24. What is the implication of the different conformations of plasmid DNA (Forms I, II, III) co-migrating at different positions when running a plasmid preparation on a gel?**
When a plasmid preparation is run on a gel, you typically observe multiple bands corresponding to the different conformational forms (supercoiled, nicked circular, and potentially linear), which can be misinterpreted as different-sized molecules by someone unaware of this phenomenon if only a linear DNA ladder is used for comparison. The implication is that you cannot accurately estimate the "true" size of a plasmid based on the migration of a single band in a standard gel run compared to a linear ladder without knowing which conformational form that band represents; this is why restriction enzyme digestion to linearize the plasmid is often recommended before gel analysis when accurate size determination is needed, since the resulting linear fragment will migrate consistently with linear ladder standards.

**25. What are the typical components of a standard TAE (1×) buffer, and what are their concentrations?**
Standard 50× TAE stock buffer is typically composed of: Tris base at 2 M concentration, glacial acetic acid at 5.71% (v/v) or sufficient to adjust pH, and EDTA (disodium salt) at 50 mM (pH 8.0). When diluted to 1× working concentration, the final approximate concentrations are: Tris at 40 mM, acetic acid (as acetate) at ~20 mM, and EDTA at 1 mM, with a final pH of approximately 8.3. The 1× TAE working buffer is used for both casting the agarose gel and filling the electrophoresis tank, ensuring that the ionic conditions within the gel and the surrounding buffer are identical, which is important for consistent, uniform DNA migration.

**26. How would you troubleshoot a situation where no bands are visible on the gel after electrophoresis and staining?**
Several possible causes should be investigated in sequence: first, confirm that the gel was actually run (check that the power supply was on, set to the correct voltage, and connected with correct polarity—if the loading dye migrated out of the wells and toward the anode, current was running in the correct direction); second, confirm that samples were actually loaded (it is easy to forget to add loading dye, or to load incorrectly, causing samples to float out of the wells); third, check if the DNA concentration in the sample was sufficient to be detected (insufficient DNA below the detection threshold of EtBr/SYBR visualization—if so, concentrate the sample and re-run); fourth, confirm that the staining step was performed correctly (sufficient EtBr/stain concentration, proper exposure time for post-staining); and fifth, check the UV transilluminator/imaging system is functioning and set to the correct excitation wavelength.

**27. What does it mean if bands appear distorted or bent (smiling/frowning pattern) across the gel?**
A "smiling" pattern (where bands are higher/closer to the wells at the center of the gel and lower/further from the wells at the edges, or vice versa giving a "frowning" pattern) indicates uneven migration across the width of the gel, typically caused by uneven temperature distribution within the gel during electrophoresis—the center of the gel (which conducts heat away less efficiently than the edges) tends to become warmer than the edges during the run, and since higher temperatures increase DNA mobility, bands at the center migrate faster. This can be caused by running the gel at too high a voltage (generating excessive heat), using a buffer at too high an ionic strength (more current = more heat), or insufficient buffer volume (buffer heats up more quickly if the volume is small relative to the gel size). Running at lower voltages and in a cool environment helps prevent this artifact.

**28. What would cause bands to appear wavy or distorted within a single lane?**
Wavy or distorted bands within a single lane can be caused by uneven gel polymerization/solidification (producing areas of varying density within the gel, through which DNA migrates unevenly), air bubbles trapped within the gel during pouring (which locally disrupt the gel matrix and cause bands to deviate around them), particulate matter in the sample that partially blocks pores locally, or physical damage to the gel surface (e.g., scratches from pipette tips touching the gel during loading). These issues can generally be avoided by pouring gels carefully (tilting the flask to let the agar flow slowly without turbulence), removing air bubbles from the gel surface before solidification (using a flame or pipette tip to burst surface bubbles), and using careful loading technique.

**29. Why is it important to ensure that the agarose used for gel preparation does not contain undissolved particulates?**
Undissolved agarose particles within the gel create localized areas of varying pore size and density, causing inconsistent resistance to DNA migration through those regions, resulting in distorted, smeared, or inconsistent band patterns, particularly in lanes that pass through or near these particulates. Complete dissolution (achieved by careful, thorough heating and mixing of the agarose suspension before pouring) ensures a homogeneous gel matrix with uniform pore size distribution, which is essential for consistent, reproducible, and interpretable band migration throughout the gel.

**30. What is the purpose of running electrophoresis until the bromophenol blue dye reaches near the end of the gel, rather than stopping earlier?**
Running until bromophenol blue (which migrates at approximately 300 bp equivalent in a 1% gel) reaches near the end of the gel maximizes the resolution and separation between bands across the size range present in the sample, allowing smaller fragments to be resolved from each other and from larger ones over a greater distance of gel; stopping too early results in bands that are clustered close together (particularly for fragments in the lower size range), making it difficult to distinguish individual bands or accurately estimate their sizes. However, the gel should not be run so long that small fragments run off the end of the gel (if those fragments are of interest) or that the heat generated causes gel distortion.

**31. What is the difference between "pre-staining" and "post-staining" with ethidium bromide, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of each?**
Pre-staining involves adding ethidium bromide directly to the molten agarose before pouring the gel (and often also to the running buffer at the same concentration, ~0.5 µg/mL), so that EtBr is present throughout the gel during electrophoresis; this is convenient (no separate staining step) but may slightly retard DNA migration (EtBr intercalation adds mass and alters the charge of the DNA) and the EtBr in the buffer is more widely distributed (more waste to dispose). Post-staining involves running the gel without EtBr, then submerging it in an EtBr solution (0.5 µg/mL in water or buffer) for 15–30 minutes after electrophoresis, followed optionally by destaining in water; this avoids any potential effect of EtBr on migration during the run but requires an extra step, additional EtBr waste solution to handle, and longer total time.

**32. What is pulsed-field gel electrophoresis (PFGE), and for what application is it needed?**
Pulsed-field gel electrophoresis (PFGE) is a specialized form of electrophoresis in which the direction of the electric field alternates periodically between two or more angles, rather than maintaining a single constant direction as in standard electrophoresis. When the field direction changes, DNA molecules must reorient themselves in the new field direction before they can resume migration; the time required for this reorientation is dependent on the size of the molecule, such that very large DNA molecules (which take longer to reorient) are effectively separated from each other and from smaller molecules across a size range that cannot be resolved by standard electrophoresis (which cannot separate molecules above ~50 kb, as they migrate in a size-independent "reptation" mode). PFGE is used to separate very large DNA molecules such as intact chromosomes or large genomic fragments (up to several megabases), and is a gold standard method for epidemiological typing of bacterial strains (using whole-genome restriction fragment patterns).

**33. What is a "restriction map," and how does agarose gel electrophoresis contribute to its construction?**
A restriction map is a diagram showing the positions and distances between restriction enzyme cleavage sites along a DNA molecule (e.g., a plasmid or a cloned insert), determined experimentally. Agarose gel electrophoresis contributes by separating the fragments produced after digestion of the DNA with one or more restriction enzymes; by measuring the sizes of the resulting fragments (estimated from their migration relative to a size ladder), and by comparing the fragment size patterns from single and double (combination) digests, the order, orientation, and spacing of the restriction sites along the original molecule can be determined mathematically, allowing the restriction map to be constructed.

**34. A DNA sample is run on a 1% agarose gel alongside a 1 kb DNA ladder. The sample shows a single, bright band co-migrating with the 500 bp marker. What can and cannot be concluded from this result alone?**
What CAN be concluded: the DNA sample contains a dominant population of DNA molecules that migrate at approximately the same rate as the 500 bp linear marker, suggesting a size of approximately 500 bp for the predominant fragment(s) in the sample (assuming the DNA is linear double-stranded DNA under native conditions). What CANNOT be concluded: this alone does not confirm the exact identity or sequence of the DNA fragment (other fragments of the same size may exist), does not rule out the presence of lower-concentration bands not visible due to insufficient DNA loading, does not confirm purity (RNA or other contaminants at low levels may not be visible), and if the sample is a plasmid, the 500 bp apparent size might reflect the migration of a supercoiled form that does not represent the true plasmid size.

**35. Why would RNA contamination in a DNA sample appear as a faint smear at the bottom of the gel, and how can it be removed?**
RNA contamination in a DNA sample appears as a faint smear at the bottom of the gel (in the lower molecular weight region, below ~300 bp) because RNA molecules are typically much smaller than the DNA fragments of interest, are heterogeneous in size (mRNA of various lengths, rRNA subunits at 16S, 23S), are single-stranded (and may form secondary structures), and therefore migrate as a diffuse population of bands/smear rather than discrete, defined bands. RNA can be removed by treating the DNA sample with RNase A (a ribonuclease enzyme that degrades RNA without affecting DNA), incubating briefly, and then optionally re-purifying the DNA (e.g., by column purification or phenol-chloroform extraction followed by ethanol precipitation) to remove the RNase and RNA degradation products.

**36. What is the significance of having a "blank" lane (containing only loading buffer, no DNA) in a gel?**
A blank lane containing only loading buffer (loading dye in TE buffer or water, with no DNA sample) is run as a negative control to confirm that neither the loading buffer nor the agarose gel itself contains any fluorescent material that could be mistaken for a DNA band, and to provide a reference for background fluorescence level, helping to assess whether faint bands in sample lanes are genuine (above background) or artifactual. It also serves as a control for any potential buffer or gel contamination that could produce background bands.

**37. How does the ionic strength of the running buffer affect DNA migration and electrophoresis quality?**
The ionic strength of the running buffer affects the ability of ions in solution to carry current through the gel: at very low ionic strength (very dilute buffer), the current-carrying capacity is low, meaning a higher voltage must be applied to achieve an adequate current, which can generate excessive heat and cause uneven migration; at very high ionic strength (concentrated buffer), too much current flows even at moderate voltages, generating large amounts of heat (Joule heating) that can melt or distort the gel, cause band smearing, and degrade the DNA, and also results in the electric field gradient being largely dissipated within the buffer rather than efficiently applied across the gel. The standard 1× TAE or 0.5× TBE buffers represent an empirically optimized ionic strength balance that allows efficient current flow and DNA migration without excessive heat generation under standard voltage conditions.

**38. What is the purpose of a "submarine" electrophoresis setup (where the gel is submerged in buffer), compared to a "vertical" setup?**
In a submarine (horizontal) electrophoresis system, the agarose gel is completely submerged in running buffer and lies flat (horizontally), with samples loaded from the top into wells and DNA migrating horizontally; the buffer layer above and below/around the gel provides ionic continuity and helps dissipate heat. Horizontal submarine setups are universally used for DNA agarose gel electrophoresis because the gel is fully supported by the liquid buffer (preventing distortion or cracking as it might if held vertically by its own weight, particularly for soft, low-percentage gels), and the samples are easily loaded from above. Vertical gel electrophoresis systems (similar to polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, PAGE, setups) are generally used for protein separation (SDS-PAGE) or for very high-resolution DNA/RNA work (e.g., sequencing gels) rather than for routine DNA agarose gel electrophoresis.

**39. What is the minimum amount of DNA (in nanograms) detectable by ethidium bromide staining in a standard gel, and how does this compare to safer stains like SYBR Safe or GelRed?**
Ethidium bromide staining can typically detect approximately 1–10 ng of DNA per band under standard UV transillumination conditions, depending on the band sharpness, fragment size, and imaging system used; in practice, 10–100 ng total DNA per lane with defined bands is often recommended for reliable visualization. Alternative stains like SYBR Safe and GelRed are generally comparable in sensitivity to EtBr, with some formulations claiming sensitivity down to 1–5 ng per band, making them practical replacements in terms of detection capability while offering significantly improved safety profiles; some more sensitive variants or enhanced imaging protocols can push sensitivity below 1 ng per band.

**40. How would you use agarose gel electrophoresis to confirm a successful restriction enzyme digestion?**
To confirm successful restriction enzyme digestion, the digested DNA sample is run alongside an undigested (intact) control of the same DNA molecule and an appropriate size ladder. The undigested control should show a band (or bands if the DNA prep contains multiple conformational forms) corresponding to the original molecule, while the digested sample should show the expected number of bands at the predicted sizes (calculated from the known restriction map or predicted from an in silico digestion using software), and the complete absence of the original undigested band (or at most a very faint residual band if digestion is slightly incomplete). The sum of the sizes of all digestion fragments should approximately equal the total size of the original molecule, serving as an internal consistency check.

**41. What is gel extraction/gel purification, and how does agarose gel electrophoresis facilitate it?**
Gel extraction (or gel purification) is a technique used to isolate a specific DNA fragment of interest from a mixture (e.g., after restriction enzyme digestion or PCR) by running the mixture on an agarose gel, then excising the band of interest from the gel using a scalpel or razor blade under UV light, and subsequently recovering the DNA from the excised agarose slice using a commercial spin-column kit (which typically involves melting the agarose in a chaotropic salt solution, binding the DNA to a silica membrane, washing away agarose and other impurities, and eluting the purified DNA in a small volume of elution buffer or water). This approach achieves both size-based purification (the desired fragment is separated from other fragments by electrophoresis) and purification from enzymes, buffers, and salts used in the reaction.

**42. Why should UV exposure time be minimized during gel visualization and band excision for gel extraction?**
Prolonged or intense UV exposure (particularly at 302 nm, the typical excitation wavelength for EtBr) can cause thymine dimer formation and other photodamage (strand breaks, crosslinks) in the DNA being visualized, because UV light is highly energetic and is directly absorbed by the pyrimidine bases. This photodamage can reduce the efficiency of subsequent enzymatic manipulations (e.g., ligation, transformation, PCR) performed on gel-extracted DNA; therefore, UV exposure should be kept to the minimum time needed to visualize and mark/excise the band, and where possible, longer-wavelength UV (365 nm, which is less damaging but also produces weaker fluorescence) or safer blue-light transillumination (which does not cause DNA damage at all and is used with blue-light-compatible stains like SYBR Safe or GelGreen) should be used for gel extraction applications.

**43. What is the expected band pattern on an agarose gel after a PCR amplification of a 750 bp fragment, if the reaction worked correctly?**
A successful PCR amplification of a 750 bp fragment should produce a single, discrete, clearly defined band at the 750 bp position (co-migrating with or between the appropriate markers in the DNA ladder lane), representing the specific amplification product. There should be no additional non-specific bands (bands at other sizes) if the PCR was specific, and no visible "primer dimer" band at the very bottom of the gel (~50–100 bp, resulting from primer-primer self-amplification in the absence of template or with low template concentrations). The band should be bright enough to indicate sufficient amplification product, and its brightness should be roughly proportional to the total amount of PCR product in the lane.

**44. What does a "ladder-like" banding pattern (multiple evenly spaced bands) in a genomic DNA lane suggest about the sample?**
A ladder-like pattern of multiple evenly spaced bands in a genomic DNA sample (rather than a high-molecular-weight smear) typically indicates nucleosomal degradation of the DNA—a characteristic pattern of apoptotic (programmed cell death) DNA fragmentation, where the DNA is cleaved by caspase-activated DNase (CAD/ICAD) at the internucleosomal linker regions between nucleosomes, producing fragments that are multiples of the ~180–200 bp unit length of DNA wrapped around each nucleosome (~180 bp, ~360 bp, ~540 bp, ~720 bp, and so on), creating a "DNA ladder" appearance with bands spaced approximately 180–200 bp apart. This "DNA laddering" is a hallmark feature of apoptosis and is used as evidence for apoptotic cell death in experimental contexts.

**45. How would you analyze the quality of an extracted DNA sample by agarose gel, particularly to determine if it is suitable for downstream applications like PCR or restriction digestion?**
A high-quality DNA sample suitable for downstream applications should appear on the gel as a single, tight, high-molecular-weight band (for genomic DNA) or as sharp bands at the expected size (for plasmid DNA or PCR products), without: (1) a visible smear below the main band (which would indicate degradation), (2) faint RNA smear at the very bottom of the lane (indicating RNA contamination, which may interfere with some assays), (3) a band that remains in or near the well (which could indicate protein-DNA complexes or very large, sheared genomic fragments that haven't migrated properly, or the presence of contaminants impeding migration), or (4) multiple bands at unexpected sizes (suggesting contamination with other DNA species or incomplete digestion).

**46. What is the role of the EDTA within the DNA loading buffer?**
EDTA within the DNA loading buffer serves to chelate divalent cations (Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, and others) present in the DNA sample, thereby inhibiting the activity of any residual nucleases (DNases, which require divalent cation cofactors) that may be present in the sample (e.g., from incomplete inactivation of restriction enzymes or contamination during sample preparation), protecting the DNA from enzymatic degradation during the brief period between preparing the sample for loading and the start of electrophoresis. This is particularly important if samples are being held at room temperature for any period before loading.

**47. What is the difference between "analytical" and "preparative" agarose gel electrophoresis?**
Analytical agarose gel electrophoresis is performed to analyze/characterize a DNA sample (determine the size, number of bands, quality, and relative quantity of DNA fragments) using a relatively small amount of DNA, with the gel typically stained and visualized but not used for DNA recovery. Preparative agarose gel electrophoresis is performed specifically to isolate/purify a specific DNA fragment from a mixture for downstream use, typically using a larger amount of starting material (to maximize the yield of the recovered fragment), and the band of interest is excised and DNA is extracted/purified from the gel slice for subsequent enzymatic manipulations (ligation, sequencing, cloning, etc.).

**48. If two DNA fragments of 1.5 kb and 1.6 kb need to be resolved on an agarose gel, what steps would you take to optimize resolution between these closely sized fragments?**
To resolve closely sized fragments (1.5 kb and 1.6 kb, only ~100 bp difference), optimization steps include: (1) using a higher agarose percentage (e.g., 1.5–2%) to increase the sieving effect and provide better differential migration of similar-sized fragments; (2) running at a lower voltage for a longer time, which allows more time for differential migration to occur and avoids heat-related band spreading; (3) using a longer gel (more run distance available for separation); (4) extending the run time to maximize the distance each band travels (greater distance = proportionally larger absolute difference in position between similar-sized bands, making them easier to distinguish); and (5) using a buffer like TBE rather than TAE, as TBE provides superior resolution in this size range due to better buffering capacity over extended runs.

**49. What is the relationship between the concentration of a DNA sample and the intensity of the band on a gel, and how can a gel be used for rough quantification?**
Band intensity (brightness/fluorescence) on an EtBr-stained gel is roughly proportional to the total mass of DNA present in that band (since EtBr intercalation and fluorescence is proportional to the number of base pairs/mass of DNA), rather than the number of molecules. For rough quantification, the intensity of an unknown band can be visually (or with software) compared to the bands of a quantitative DNA ladder (where the manufacturer specifies the mass of DNA in each band at a standard loading, e.g., "100 ng total, with each band representing a specific fraction"), and by comparing brightness to the nearest mass-equivalent ladder band, an approximate estimate of the DNA concentration in the sample can be derived. This is only a rough, order-of-magnitude estimate; more precise quantification requires spectrophotometric (NanoDrop) or fluorometric (Qubit) methods.

**50. Summarize the complete procedure of agarose gel electrophoresis for DNA as an examiner would expect a candidate to present it, highlighting all key decision points.**
The complete procedure, as an examiner expects: (1) choose the appropriate agarose percentage based on the expected DNA fragment size range; (2) prepare the gel by dissolving agarose in 1× TAE (or TBE) by heating, cooling to ~55°C, adding EtBr or safer alternative stain if pre-staining, and pouring into a sealed casting tray with an appropriate comb; allow to solidify (~20–30 min); (3) remove the comb, place the gel in the electrophoresis tank, and fill with 1× running buffer (the same buffer used to prepare the gel) to just cover the gel surface; (4) prepare samples by mixing with DNA loading dye, and include a DNA size ladder; (5) load samples into the wells, ensuring the wells are oriented at the cathode (negative/black) end so that DNA migrates toward the anode; (6) run at an appropriate voltage (typically 80–120 V) until the tracking dye has migrated a sufficient distance; (7) visualize the gel on a UV transilluminator (or blue light box for safer stains), photograph the image; (8) measure band migration distances and estimate sizes by comparison to the ladder; (9) interpret results in the context of the experiment (expected vs. observed bands, quality assessment, size confirmation). Key decision points include: agarose percentage choice, buffer choice, stain choice (EtBr vs. safer alternatives), voltage (balancing speed vs. resolution), and run time (determined by tracking dye position and the size range needing resolution).
