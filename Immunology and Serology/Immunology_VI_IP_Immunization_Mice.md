## SECTION A: FUNDAMENTALS OF IMMUNIZATION & IMMUNE RESPONSE

---

**Q1. What is immunization and what is the fundamental immunological principle underlying it?**

Immunization is the process of introducing an antigen (immunogen) into a living organism to stimulate the adaptive immune system to mount a specific immune response, resulting in the development of immunological memory. The fundamental immunological principle is **clonal selection theory** (Burnet, 1957): each naive B or T lymphocyte bears a unique, somatically generated antigen receptor (BCR or TCR) that recognizes a specific epitope. Upon antigen encounter, lymphocytes bearing matching receptors are selectively activated, proliferate (clonal expansion), differentiate into effector cells (antibody-secreting plasma cells or cytotoxic/helper T cells), and generate long-lived memory cells. Upon re-exposure to the same antigen (secondary response), memory cells respond faster, more vigorously, and with higher affinity — forming the mechanistic basis of vaccination and experimental immunization protocols.

---

**Q2. What is the difference between active and passive immunization?**

**Active immunization** involves introducing an antigen (or attenuated/inactivated pathogen) into the host, stimulating the host's own immune system to generate a primary response including antibody production, T cell activation, and immunological memory. The response takes 7–14 days to develop but is long-lasting (months to decades). Active immunization is used in vaccines (e.g., tetanus toxoid, measles vaccine) and in experimental animal immunization for antibody production.

**Passive immunization** involves the direct transfer of pre-formed antibodies (immunoglobulins) or immune cells from an immune donor to a non-immune recipient, conferring immediate but temporary protection. No memory is generated because the recipient's own immune system was not activated. Duration corresponds to the half-life of the transferred antibodies (IgG half-life ~21 days in humans). Examples include: administration of tetanus antitoxin (anti-tetanus immunoglobulin) for post-exposure prophylaxis; transplacental transfer of maternal IgG to the fetus (natural passive immunity); administration of hyperimmune sera or monoclonal antibodies therapeutically. In research, passive transfer experiments (adoptive transfer of serum or cells) are used to study immune mechanisms.

---

**Q3. What are the cardinal features of the adaptive immune response that are exploited in immunization?**

The adaptive immune response has four cardinal features that make immunization effective:

1. **Specificity**: Each lymphocyte clone recognizes one specific epitope through its antigen receptor (BCR or TCR). The immune response generated by immunization is directed specifically against the immunizing antigen.

2. **Diversity**: The total lymphocyte repertoire can recognize ~10⁷–10¹⁸ different epitopes through somatic recombination of V(D)J gene segments — ensuring that virtually any antigen encountered will find matching lymphocyte clones to activate.

3. **Memory**: After the primary response, long-lived memory B and T cells persist for years to decades. Upon re-exposure (booster immunization or natural infection), memory cells respond within 1–3 days with a faster, stronger, and qualitatively superior (higher affinity, class-switched) response — the secondary (anamnestic) response.

4. **Self-tolerance**: The immune system is educated (in thymus for T cells, in bone marrow for B cells) to eliminate self-reactive clones, preventing autoimmune responses against host tissues. Immunization exploits the specificity and memory features while relying on self-tolerance to prevent unwanted autoreactivity.

---

**Q4. What is the difference between the primary and secondary immune response in the context of immunization?**

| Feature | Primary Response | Secondary Response |
|---|---|---|
| Trigger | First exposure to antigen | Re-exposure (booster dose) |
| Lag period (latency) | 7–14 days | 1–3 days |
| Peak antibody titer | Lower (10²–10³ units) | Higher (10⁴–10⁵ units, 10–100× primary) |
| Predominant Ig class | IgM (early), then IgG | IgG (predominantly); IgA, IgE |
| Antibody affinity | Lower (germline V region genes) | Higher (somatic hypermutation in germinal centers → affinity maturation) |
| Duration of response | Weeks to months | Months to years |
| Cells responsible | Naive B and T cells | Memory B and T cells (rapidly activated) |
| Germinal center reaction | Extensive (primary germinal center formation) | Rapid, enhanced (pre-existing GC memory) |

The difference underpins why booster doses are given after the initial immunization — the primary response primes the immune system; subsequent boosters recall the memory response, achieving high, sustained antibody titers required for protection or experimental use.

---

**Q5. What is an antigen versus an immunogen, and what properties make a molecule a good immunogen?**

**Antigen**: Any molecule capable of being specifically recognized by an antibody or T/B cell receptor. Antigens may or may not be able to stimulate an immune response on their own.

**Immunogen**: A molecule capable of both (a) being specifically recognized by the immune system AND (b) independently stimulating an immune response leading to antibody production and/or T cell activation. All immunogens are antigens, but not all antigens are immunogens.

**Properties of a good immunogen**:
1. **Foreignness**: The molecule must be recognized as non-self by the host immune system. Self-molecules are tolerized; highly conserved molecules (e.g., histones) are poorly immunogenic. The more genetically distant the antigen source from the host, the more immunogenic.
2. **Molecular weight**: Generally >10 kDa; large molecules have more epitopes and more surface area for immune cell recognition. Small molecules (<1 kDa, haptens) cannot stimulate immune responses alone.
3. **Chemical complexity**: Proteins are the most immunogenic (diverse amino acid composition, 3D structure, T cell epitopes in MHC context). Polysaccharides are moderately immunogenic (T-independent antigens). Lipids and nucleic acids are generally poorly immunogenic alone.
4. **Structural rigidity**: Rigid, stable molecules present epitopes consistently for immune recognition; highly flexible molecules may not maintain a single recognizable structure.
5. **Degradability**: Must be processable by antigen-presenting cells (APCs) — proteolytic degradation into peptides for MHC presentation is required for T cell help; completely non-degradable antigens are poor immunogens.
6. **Dose and route of administration**: Critical parameters — too little antigen → no response (below threshold); too much → tolerance; route determines which lymphoid compartment is primarily activated.

## SECTION B: INTRAPERITONEAL ROUTE — ANATOMY & IMMUNOLOGY

---

**Q6. What is the intraperitoneal (IP) route of injection and what anatomical structures are relevant to understanding this route?**

The **intraperitoneal (IP) route** refers to injection of a substance directly into the peritoneal cavity — the space within the abdomen enclosed by the peritoneum (a serosal membrane lining the abdominal wall and covering abdominal organs). Relevant anatomy in mice:

- **Peritoneal cavity**: A potential space between the parietal peritoneum (lining the abdominal wall) and visceral peritoneum (covering organs: intestines, liver, spleen, stomach, mesentery). In mice, the cavity contains a small amount of peritoneal fluid (~0.1–0.5 mL).
- **Peritoneal immune cells**: The peritoneal cavity is richly populated with immune cells: resident peritoneal macrophages (~30–40% of peritoneal cells in mice), B-1 B cells (a unique self-renewing B cell population especially abundant in the peritoneum of mice, distinct from conventional B-2 B cells), NK cells, mast cells, and T cells — making it an immunologically active compartment.
- **Omentum**: A fold of fatty tissue hanging from the stomach and transverse colon; rich in lymphoid structures called "milky spots" that serve as primary sites of immune cell aggregation and antigen sampling from the peritoneal cavity.
- **Mesenteric lymph nodes**: Drain the peritoneal cavity and intestinal lymphatics; major regional lymph nodes for peritoneal immune responses.
- **Systemic circulation**: Absorbed antigens from the peritoneal cavity reach systemic lymphoid organs (spleen, lymph nodes) via lymphatic drainage and portal circulation.

---

**Q7. What are the immunological advantages of the intraperitoneal route compared to other routes (subcutaneous, intramuscular, intradermal)?**

| Route | Primary lymphoid site activated | Key features |
|---|---|---|
| **Intraperitoneal (IP)** | Peritoneal macrophages, omentum milky spots, mesenteric LN, spleen | Strong systemic antibody response; high antigen uptake by peritoneal macrophages; suitable for large volume (up to 1 mL in mice); no special tissue access barriers |
| **Subcutaneous (SC)** | Local draining lymph nodes (inguinal, axillary) | Local response first; slow antigen release from depot; good for adjuvanted immunization; adjuvant granuloma formation at site |
| **Intramuscular (IM)** | Local draining lymph nodes | Used clinically; slower absorption; muscle damage from adjuvants; difficult in small mice |
| **Intradermal (ID)** | Skin-resident Langerhans cells and dermal DCs; local LN | Smallest volume (0.01–0.05 mL); highly immunogenic per unit antigen dose; used in tuberculin skin test; difficult in mice |
| **Intravenous (IV)** | Spleen, systemic | Immediate systemic distribution; no depot effect; risk of anaphylaxis; T-independent B cell activation |
| **Intranasal (IN)** | MALT (NALT, BALT), mucosal IgA response | Mucosal immunity; requires specialized formulations; variable uptake |

**IP advantages for mouse immunization**:
- Large volume capacity relative to body size (up to 1 mL) — allows administration of sufficient antigen + adjuvant.
- Excellent access to peritoneal macrophages — first-line APCs for IP antigens.
- Rapid systemic dissemination through lymphatic and portal circulation.
- Strong humoral immune response — high serum IgG titers.
- Technically simpler than IV injection in mice (lower risk of vascular damage).
- Particularly effective for ascites production (hybridoma-based monoclonal antibody production from peritoneal fluid).

---

**Q8. What is the role of peritoneal macrophages in processing intraperitoneally administered antigens?**

Peritoneal macrophages are the first immune cells to encounter an antigen administered intraperitoneally. They perform critical functions:

1. **Phagocytosis and antigen capture**: Resident peritoneal macrophages (large peritoneal macrophages, LPMs — characterized as Tim4⁺GATA6⁺ in mice) rapidly phagocytose particulate antigens, bacteria, and opsonized proteins within minutes of IP injection. For soluble proteins, macropinocytosis (fluid-phase uptake) and receptor-mediated endocytosis (via Fc receptors if opsonized, or scavenger receptors) are utilized.

2. **Antigen processing**: Internalized proteins are degraded by lysosomal proteases (cathepsins B, D, L, S) into 8–25 amino acid peptides in the endosomal-lysosomal compartment.

3. **MHC II presentation**: Peptides are loaded onto MHC class II molecules (H-2 in mice) in MIIC (MHC class II compartment) and transported to the cell surface for presentation to CD4⁺ T helper cells — initiating the adaptive immune response.

4. **Co-stimulation**: Activated macrophages upregulate co-stimulatory molecules (CD80/B7.1, CD86/B7.2) and secrete cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α, IL-12) — providing signals 2 and 3 for T cell activation (signal 1 = TCR-pMHC interaction; signal 2 = CD28-B7; signal 3 = cytokines directing T cell differentiation).

5. **Migration to lymphoid organs**: Some activated peritoneal macrophages (and dendritic cells recruited after initial activation) migrate via lymphatics to the mesenteric lymph nodes and spleen, carrying processed antigen to lymphoid organs where primary immune responses are organized.

---

**Q9. What is the omentum and why is it immunologically significant in the context of IP immunization?**

The **omentum** is a large fold of adipose and connective tissue attached to the stomach and transverse colon, hanging like an apron over the small intestine. It is immunologically significant because:

1. **Milky spots**: The omentum contains unique lymphoid aggregates called "milky spots" — clusters of macrophages, B cells (predominantly B-1 B cells), T cells, dendritic cells, and mast cells surrounded by vascular sinusoids. They are visible to the naked eye as white spots (hence "milky") and range from 0.5–2 mm in diameter.

2. **Antigen sampling hub**: Milky spots function as primary sites for sampling antigens from the peritoneal cavity. Specialized macrophages in milky spots phagocytose antigens entering the peritoneal space and present them to local B and T cells, initiating localized immune responses.

3. **B-1 B cell reservoir**: B-1 B cells in the omentum produce natural IgM antibodies (polyreactive, low affinity) that provide first-line defense against peritoneal pathogens; they are also important for T-independent antibody responses to polysaccharide antigens delivered IP.

4. **Immune surveillance**: The omentum acts as an immunological barrier that traps and destroys pathogens, cancer cells, and foreign particles that enter the peritoneal cavity — explaining its historical role as a "police of the abdomen" in surgical literature.

5. **Relevance to IP immunization**: After IP injection, antigens accumulate in omentum milky spots within minutes to hours, making the omentum one of the first lymphoid structures to process IP-administered antigens before systemic dissemination.

**Q10. How does the IP route specifically activate B-1 B cells, and what is the immunological significance of this activation?**

B-1 B cells are a unique self-renewing population of B lymphocytes predominantly found in the peritoneal and pleural cavities of mice (less abundant in humans). They differ from conventional follicular B-2 B cells in multiple ways:

**B-1 B cell characteristics**:
- Surface markers: CD19⁺ CD20⁺ IgM^hi IgD^lo CD23⁻ CD43⁺ B220^lo; B-1a subset additionally CD5⁺; B-1b subset CD5⁻.
- Self-renewing (unlike B-2 cells that are constantly renewed from bone marrow).
- Respond to T-independent antigens (TI-2 antigens: polysaccharides, LPS) without requiring T cell help.
- Produce natural antibodies (IgM, some IgA) with polyreactivity — can bind multiple unrelated antigens.

**IP immunization and B-1 activation**:
- Polysaccharide antigens, LPS, and certain bacterial components delivered IP preferentially activate peritoneal B-1 B cells (and marginal zone B cells after drainage to spleen) in a T-independent manner.
- These responses generate rapid IgM production (within 48–72 hours) without somatic hypermutation, affinity maturation, or long-term memory — providing immediate but non-specific protection.
- For protein antigens + adjuvant, conventional B-2 B cells are the primary responders (T-dependent response), activated in the mesenteric lymph nodes and spleen after antigen drainage.

**Experimental significance**: The peritoneal B-1 B cell population is why IP immunization with polysaccharide antigens generates robust IgM responses — useful for studying T-independent immune responses. For high-affinity IgG production (e.g., for monoclonal antibody production), protein antigens + adjuvant + multiple IP immunizations stimulate B-2 cell responses with germinal center reactions.

## SECTION C: EXPERIMENTAL PROTOCOL & TECHNIQUE

---

**Q11. What are the ethical and regulatory requirements for mouse immunization experiments?**

Mouse immunization experiments are governed by strict ethical and regulatory frameworks globally:

**Regulatory frameworks**:
- **Three Rs principle** (Russell and Burch, 1959): **Replacement** (use alternatives to animals where possible — in vitro methods, cell lines, computer models); **Reduction** (use minimum number of animals to achieve statistically valid results — proper power calculations); **Refinement** (minimize pain, distress, and suffering through improved techniques, humane endpoints, analgesia).
- **India**: Committee for the Purpose of Control and Supervision of Experiments on Animals (CPCSEA) under the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act 1960; institutional IAEC (Institutional Animal Ethics Committee) approval mandatory.
- **USA**: Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) approval; Animal Welfare Act (AWA); Guide for Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (NRC).
- **Europe**: Directive 2010/63/EU; project authorization required from national authority.
- **ARRIVE guidelines** (Animal Research Reporting of In Vivo Experiments): Reporting standards for published animal research.

**Specific requirements for immunization experiments**:
- IAEC/IACUC approval of the specific protocol including: antigen identity, adjuvant, route, dose, volume, frequency, number of animals, endpoint criteria.
- Qualified personnel performing injections (certified in laboratory animal handling and procedures).
- Daily monitoring of animals for signs of distress, weight loss, injection site reactions.
- Defined humane endpoints (criteria for early termination if animals show excessive suffering).
- Post-procedure care including analgesia if significant discomfort expected.

---

**Q12. What strain of mice is commonly used for immunization experiments and why?**

Several inbred mouse strains are used depending on experimental requirements:

**BALB/c (most common)**:
- Genetic background: H-2d MHC haplotype.
- Immune bias: Strong Th2 response (high IL-4, IL-5, IL-13; high IgG1, IgE production).
- Used for: Hybridoma production (BALB/c origin for standard NS0/Sp2/0 myeloma fusion partners); Th2-type immune response studies; allergy models.
- Highly inbred (>99% homozygous) → reproducible, consistent results between animals.

**C57BL/6 (C57 Black 6)**:
- Genetic background: H-2b MHC haplotype.
- Immune bias: Strong Th1 response (high IFN-γ, IL-2; high IgG2c production in C57BL/6).
- Used for: Knockout and transgenic models (most gene-targeted mice are on C57BL/6 background); Th1-type response studies; cancer immunology; metabolic studies.

**Swiss Webster / ICR (outbred)**:
- Outbred stock — genetic diversity; not inbred.
- Used for: General purpose studies; toxicology; situations where genetic diversity is preferred; cheaper to maintain.

**CBA, DBA/2, A/J**: Other inbred strains with specific MHC haplotypes used for particular immunological studies.

**Choice for immunization**:
- For polyclonal antibody production: BALB/c or C57BL/6 (both generate strong IgG responses).
- For hybridoma monoclonal antibody production: Must use the same strain as the myeloma partner (BALB/c with NS0 or Sp2/0 myeloma cells — same H-2d haplotype prevents immune rejection of the hybridoma implanted for ascites production).

**Q13. What are the standard antigen preparations used for immunization of mice, and how are they prepared?**

Antigen preparations vary by immunogen type and experimental goal:

**Protein antigens** (most common for antibody production):
- **Purified protein**: BSA, ovalbumin, keyhole limpet hemocyanin (KLH), cytokines, recombinant proteins — dissolved in PBS at 0.1–1 mg/mL; filter-sterilized (0.22 µm).
- **Hapten-carrier conjugates**: Small molecules (haptens, <1 kDa) conjugated to carrier proteins (KLH, BSA) using coupling reagents (glutaraldehyde, EDC/NHS chemistry) to render them immunogenic. KLH is preferred as carrier because it is highly immunogenic (large, complex, foreign to mice).
- **Whole cell preparations**: Bacterial cells (formalin-killed), tumor cell lysates, RBC preparations.

**Preparation for IP immunization with adjuvant**:
1. Dissolve protein antigen in sterile PBS at 2× desired final concentration (e.g., if final antigen dose = 100 µg in 0.5 mL, prepare 200 µg/mL = 0.1 mg/mL in PBS).
2. Prepare adjuvant (e.g., Freund's Complete Adjuvant) separately.
3. Emulsify: combine equal volumes of antigen solution and adjuvant using two syringes connected by a 3-way stopcock or Luer lock connector; push mixture back and forth vigorously (50–100 passes) until a stable water-in-oil emulsion forms.
4. Test emulsion stability: drop a small amount onto water surface — if the drop remains intact and does not disperse, the emulsion is stable.
5. Load into syringes; inject immediately (emulsions can separate if left standing).

**Dose range**: Typically 10–200 µg protein antigen per mouse per injection for IP immunization; optimal dose determined empirically for each antigen.

---

**Q14. What is an adjuvant and what are the most commonly used adjuvants in mouse immunization experiments?**

An **adjuvant** (from Latin *adjuvare* = to help) is a substance administered together with an antigen that enhances and/or modulates the magnitude, breadth, and quality of the immune response to that antigen. Adjuvants work by:
- Creating an antigen depot (prolonging antigen exposure).
- Activating innate immune cells (especially DCs, macrophages) via pattern recognition receptors (TLRs, NLRs, CLRs).
- Promoting inflammation (recruiting immune cells to injection site).
- Providing "danger signals" that overcome the immune system's threshold for activation.

**Commonly used adjuvants in mouse immunization**:

| Adjuvant | Composition | Mechanism | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Freund's Complete Adjuvant (FCA)** | Killed *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* + paraffin oil + mannide monooleate (emulsifier) | Depot effect; TLR2/4 activation by mycobacterial components; granuloma formation; strong Th1 induction | First (primary) immunization only; too inflammatory for repeated use |
| **Freund's Incomplete Adjuvant (FIA)** | Paraffin oil + mannide monooleate (no mycobacteria) | Depot effect only; milder inflammation | Booster immunizations (2nd, 3rd doses) |
| **Alum (Aluminum hydroxide/phosphate)** | Al(OH)₃ or AlPO₄ | Depot; NLRP3 inflammasome activation; Th2 bias; approved for human vaccines | Widely used; preferred when Th2 response desired |
| **MPL (Monophosphoryl Lipid A)** | Detoxified LPS | TLR4 agonist; Th1/Th2 balanced; licensed in human vaccines (AS04 with alum) | Less toxic than LPS; used in research and licensed vaccines |
| **CFA alternatives** | Titermax, RIBI (MPL+trehalose dicorynomycolate), ISCOMs | Various TLR agonists | Replacing CFA for animal welfare reasons |
| **Complete Sigma Adjuvant System** | Synthetic alternative to CFA | TLR2 agonist (muramyl dipeptide analog) | Animal welfare-friendly alternative |

**Q15. What is the difference between Freund's Complete Adjuvant (FCA) and Freund's Incomplete Adjuvant (FIA), and why is FCA used only for the primary immunization?**

**Freund's Complete Adjuvant (FCA)**:
- Composition: Heat-killed *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (or *M. butyricum*) in mineral oil + emulsifier (mannide monooleate, Arlacel A).
- Mechanism: (1) Oil creates a water-in-oil emulsion depot — antigen slowly released over weeks. (2) Mycobacterial components (muramyl dipeptide, lipoarabinomannan, mycolic acids) are potent TLR2 and TLR4 agonists, strongly activating dendritic cells and macrophages, promoting IL-12 secretion and robust Th1-type CD4⁺ T helper response. (3) Granuloma formation at injection site concentrates antigen-presenting cells.
- Effect: Produces the strongest, most sustained antibody response of any adjuvant; essential for inducing high-titer, high-affinity antibodies against weakly immunogenic antigens.

**Freund's Incomplete Adjuvant (FIA)**:
- Composition: Same mineral oil + emulsifier WITHOUT mycobacteria.
- Mechanism: Only the depot effect (slow antigen release); no TLR activation; milder inflammatory response.
- Effect: Less potent immune stimulation; lower but acceptable response for boosting.

**Why FCA only for primary immunization**:
- Repeated use of FCA (multiple injections) causes severe, progressive granulomatous inflammation, tissue necrosis at injection sites, adjuvant arthritis (polyarthritis resembling rheumatoid arthritis in mice — autoimmune-like condition triggered by mycobacterial epitopes cross-reactive with self-cartilage antigens), and significant chronic pain and morbidity.
- After the primary immunization primes the immune system with strong TLR activation, subsequent boosters use FIA (depot effect only) because the memory response is already established and does not require the full mycobacterial TLR stimulus to achieve high antibody titers.
- Animal welfare regulations in many countries (UK, EU) restrict or prohibit FCA use even for primary immunization, requiring alternatives (MPL, RIBI adjuvant system).

**Q16. Describe the complete step-by-step procedure for IP immunization of a mouse.**

**Materials**: 6–8 week old BALB/c mice; antigen (e.g., 100 µg BSA in 0.25 mL PBS); FCA (0.25 mL for primary immunization); 1 mL syringes; 23–25 gauge needles (1 inch); isoflurane anesthesia or physical restraint; alcohol swabs; animal handling gloves; cage card for documentation.

**Emulsion preparation**:
1. Load 0.25 mL antigen solution into one 1 mL syringe; 0.25 mL FCA into a second syringe.
2. Connect syringes via Luer lock coupler; pass the mixture back and forth vigorously 50–100 times until a thick, stable water-in-oil emulsion forms (drop test: stable on water surface).
3. Transfer emulsion to a single syringe; cap until use.

**Injection procedure**:
4. **Restrain the mouse**: For IP injection, manual restraint is usually sufficient. Scruff the mouse by grasping the loose skin over the back of the neck firmly between thumb and index finger; control the tail between the palm and ring/little finger; tilt the mouse to position the abdomen upward at ~30–45° angle with the head tilted slightly downward (gravity shifts organs away from the injection site).
5. **Site selection**: Lower right or left quadrant of the abdomen, lateral to the midline and caudal to the ribcage. Avoid the midline (linea alba — where the bladder, cecum, and uterus are closest to the surface); avoid the upper quadrant (liver).
6. **Disinfect**: Wipe the injection area with an alcohol swab; allow to dry.
7. **Injection**: Using a 23–25G × 1 inch needle at a 10–20° angle to the abdominal wall (nearly parallel to the skin surface — bevel up), insert the needle through the skin and abdominal wall into the peritoneal cavity. Keep the needle superficial enough to penetrate peritoneum but not deep enough to contact viscera.
8. **Aspiration check**: Gently pull back the plunger — if blood, urine, or intestinal contents are aspirated, the needle has entered a vessel, bladder, or intestine → withdraw, discard syringe, use fresh needle and new site.
9. **Injection**: If aspiration is negative (clear or no fluid), slowly inject 0.5 mL of emulsion; the animal may show momentary discomfort (normal).
10. **Withdrawal**: Withdraw the needle quickly; apply gentle pressure with dry gauze.
11. **Post-injection monitoring**: Return mouse to cage; observe for 5–10 minutes for adverse reactions (labored breathing, hunched posture, severe distress → immediate veterinary assessment).
12. **Documentation**: Record: mouse ID, weight, antigen, adjuvant, dose, volume, route, date, operator, any observations.

**Q17. What volume can be safely injected intraperitoneally in mice, and what factors determine the maximum safe volume?**

**Standard volumes for IP injection in mice**:
- **Routine maximum**: 1.0 mL per injection (total volume in peritoneal cavity).
- **Practical working volume**: 0.5–1.0 mL per injection for immunization.
- **Absolute maximum (exceptional circumstances)**: 2.0 mL (with caution; only for brief, single-dose studies).

**Factors determining maximum safe volume**:
1. **Body weight**: Larger mice tolerate larger volumes; scaled dosing: ~10 mL/kg body weight is the standard guideline. For a 25 g (0.025 kg) mouse: 10 mL/kg × 0.025 kg = 0.25 mL as conservative limit; up to 20 mL/kg (0.5 mL) is well tolerated for most studies; up to 40 mL/kg (1.0 mL) acceptable for single-dose studies.
2. **Tonicity of solution**: Hypertonic solutions cause pain and peritoneal irritation; always use isotonic (physiological, ~300 mOsm/kg) or near-isotonic solutions.
3. **pH**: Neutral pH (6.5–7.5) required; acidic or alkaline solutions cause peritoneal inflammation.
4. **Irritants**: Avoid particulates, solvents, preservatives; oil-adjuvant emulsions (0.5 mL) are routinely used.
5. **Frequency**: Repeated injections in the same site reduce tolerance; multiple injections (immunization series) should be spaced by ≥2 weeks; total volume per injection should not increase with successive doses.
6. **Animal condition**: Compromised or diseased animals tolerate smaller volumes; healthy, weight-appropriate animals are required.

**Q18. What complications can arise from intraperitoneal injection in mice, and how are they prevented or managed?**

**Complications and management**:

1. **Accidental intravisceral injection**:
   - Injection into intestinal lumen, cecum, or stomach — causes local inflammation; if adjuvant-containing, may cause peritonitis.
   - Prevention: Proper restraint (head down), correct needle angle (nearly parallel to skin), always aspirate before injecting, inject in lower lateral quadrant.
   - Detection: Aspiration of fecal material or brown/yellow fluid; animal shows sudden severe distress.
   - Management: Withdraw immediately; monitor animal closely; veterinary assessment; may require euthanasia.

2. **Bladder puncture**:
   - Urine aspirated on withdrawal test; injection into bladder wall.
   - Prevention: Ensure mouse has urinated before injection (gentle abdominal pressure); keep needle in lower lateral abdomen (bladder is medial and ventral).
   - Management: Usually minor; monitor for hematuria.

3. **Hemorrhage / vessel puncture**:
   - Blood aspiration; may cause internal bleeding.
   - Management: Apply pressure; monitor; veterinary assessment.

4. **Peritonitis**:
   - Caused by: accidental introduction of bacteria (poor aseptic technique); bowel puncture releasing intestinal contents; excessive irritant adjuvant volumes.
   - Prevention: Strict aseptic technique; proper volume limits; avoid FCA in sensitive locations; use alternatives to FCA.
   - Signs: Hunched posture, ruffled fur, abdominal distension, weight loss >20%, reduced activity within 24–48 hours.
   - Management: Veterinary assessment; antibiotics; or humane euthanasia per humane endpoint criteria.

5. **Injection into abdominal wall (subcutaneous)**:
   - Antigen delivered subcutaneously rather than IP; reduces efficacy of IP immunization.
   - Prevention: Confirm needle is through the full peritoneal wall (feel reduced resistance when needle enters peritoneal space); correct angle.

6. **Anaphylaxis**:
   - Rare; more common with subsequent immunizations (sensitization); immediate collapse, cyanosis, apnea.
   - Management: Epinephrine (0.1 mL of 1:1000 solution SC) immediately; supportive care.

**Q19. What is the immunization schedule for producing polyclonal antibodies in mice using the IP route?**

A standard IP immunization schedule for polyclonal antibody production:

**Schedule**:
| Week | Injection | Antigen Dose | Adjuvant | Volume |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Primary (1st immunization) | 50–100 µg protein | FCA (1:1 emulsion) | 0.5 mL IP |
| 2–3 | 1st Booster | 25–50 µg protein | FIA (1:1 emulsion) | 0.5 mL IP |
| 5–6 | 2nd Booster | 25–50 µg protein | FIA (1:1 emulsion) | 0.5 mL IP |
| 7–8 | Test bleed | — | — | 100–200 µL retroorbital or tail vein |
| 8 | Titer assessment | Measure antibody titer by ELISA | — | — |
| 9–10 | Final boost (if titer insufficient) | 25 µg protein | PBS only (no adjuvant) | 0.5 mL IP or IV |
| 10–11 | Terminal bleed / cardiac puncture | — | — | Maximum blood volume |

**Notes**:
- **Test bleed at Day 7–10 post-2nd booster**: Serum titer checked by ELISA; if titer >1:1000 (satisfactory), proceed to terminal bleed.
- **Final boost (antigen in PBS only, no adjuvant)**: Given 3–5 days before terminal bleed; stimulates rapid memory B cell activation and plasma cell differentiation, maximizing serum antibody titer at time of bleed.
- **Terminal bleed**: Cardiac puncture under deep anesthesia (then euthanasia) → 1–1.5 mL blood from a 25 g mouse. Serum separated by centrifugation; stored at −80°C.

**Q20. How is blood collected from mice for serum antibody titer assessment, and what volumes can be safely collected?**

**Blood collection methods in mice**:

| Method | Volume | Frequency | Anesthesia | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tail vein nick** | 0.05–0.2 mL | Weekly (with recovery) | Not required | Superficial nick of lateral tail vein; warm tail in warm water first to dilate vein |
| **Retroorbital (orbital sinus) bleed** | 0.1–0.3 mL | Every 2 weeks (alternating eyes) | Isoflurane required | Capillary tube into medial canthus; operator training required; animal welfare concern |
| **Saphenous vein** | 0.1–0.2 mL | Frequently | Not required | Less traumatic; preferred by welfare guidelines |
| **Submandibular (cheek) punch** | 0.1–0.3 mL | Every 2 weeks | Not required | Lancet used to puncture submandibular vein; quick; good welfare profile |
| **Cardiac puncture (terminal)** | 0.8–1.5 mL (full bleed) | Once (terminal) | Deep anesthesia | Performed under terminal anesthesia; maximum blood volume; followed by euthanasia |

**Safe blood collection volumes** (based on circulating blood volume ~72 mL/kg in mice):
- 25 g mouse → circulating blood volume ≈ 1.8 mL.
- **Single collection**: Maximum 7.5–10% of total blood volume = 0.13–0.18 mL safely; up to 15% (0.27 mL) acceptable with adequate recovery time.
- **Repeated collections** (every 2 weeks): Maximum 10% per collection.
- **Terminal bleed**: Up to 80% of total blood volume (1.4 mL) under terminal anesthesia.

**Serum preparation from collected blood**:
1. Allow blood to clot at room temperature for 30–60 minutes (or 37°C for 15 minutes).
2. Centrifuge at 2,000 × g for 10 minutes; collect serum (upper clear layer).
3. Store at −80°C in aliquots; avoid repeated freeze-thaw.

**Q21. How is the antibody titer of immunized mice determined by ELISA, and how do you interpret the results?**

**Indirect ELISA for antibody titer determination**:

**Principle**: Plate-bound antigen captures specific antibodies from the serum; bound antibodies detected by enzyme-labeled secondary anti-mouse IgG antibody; substrate produces colorimetric signal proportional to antibody concentration.

**Procedure**:
1. **Coat**: Add 100 µL of antigen solution (1–10 µg/mL in carbonate-bicarbonate buffer pH 9.6) per well of 96-well ELISA plate; incubate overnight at 4°C.
2. **Block**: Remove antigen; add 200 µL of blocking buffer (1–5% BSA or non-fat dry milk in PBS-Tween 0.05% — PBST); incubate 1–2 hours at RT.
3. **Serum dilutions**: Add 100 µL of serially diluted mouse serum (2-fold dilutions from 1:50 to 1:102,400) in blocking buffer; incubate 1–2 hours at RT; wash 3× with PBST.
4. **Secondary antibody**: Add 100 µL of HRP-conjugated anti-mouse IgG antibody (1:5,000–1:20,000 dilution); incubate 1 hour at RT; wash 5× with PBST.
5. **Substrate**: Add 100 µL TMB (3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine) substrate; incubate 10–15 minutes at RT in dark.
6. **Stop**: Add 50 µL of 2N H₂SO₄ stop solution; read OD at 450 nm (reference 630 nm).

**Titer interpretation**:
- **Endpoint titer**: The highest serum dilution that gives OD ≥ 2× (or 3×) the background (OD of negative control serum at the same dilution). Reported as the reciprocal of this dilution (e.g., titer = 1:6400 → 6400).
- **Positive response**: Titer >1:100 generally considered positive; >1:1000 good; >1:10,000 excellent for polyclonal sera.
- **Titer progression**: Compare pre-immunization (day 0), post-primary (day 14), post-booster (day 28) titers → should show stepwise increase, confirming successful immunization.

**Q22. What is somatic hypermutation and affinity maturation, and how do they relate to the booster immunization schedule?**

**Somatic hypermutation (SHM)** is the process by which point mutations are introduced into the variable (V) regions of immunoglobulin genes at a rate ~10⁶-fold higher than the background mutation rate, specifically in germinal centers (GCs) of secondary lymphoid organs. SHM is catalyzed by the enzyme Activation-Induced Cytidine Deaminase (AID), encoded by the *AICDA* gene, which deaminates cytosines in single-stranded DNA regions of V genes during transcription, initiating a mutagenic repair pathway.

**Affinity maturation** is the selection process in germinal centers whereby B cell clones with BCR mutations that increase antigen-binding affinity are preferentially selected for survival and differentiation, while low-affinity mutants die by apoptosis (neglect):
1. SHM generates diverse BCR variants in the GC dark zone (centroblasts).
2. GC light zone (centrocytes) express the mutated BCR; compete for limited antigen presented on follicular dendritic cells (FDCs).
3. High-affinity variants capture more antigen → receive stronger BCR signaling → receive T follicular helper (Tfh) cell survival signals (CD40L-CD40 + cytokines).
4. Low-affinity variants fail to compete → undergo Fas-FasL-mediated apoptosis.
5. Selected high-affinity clones cycle back to dark zone for further mutation → iterative affinity improvement over multiple rounds.

**Relevance to booster schedule**:
- Primary immunization initiates GC reactions in draining lymph nodes and spleen; SHM and affinity maturation begin during the primary response (peak GC reaction at Days 10–21).
- Booster injections re-activate memory B cells and promote new GC reactions with antigen-specific memory B cells as founders — these GC reactions undergo further rounds of SHM and selection, producing even higher-affinity antibodies with each successive boost.
- This is why antibodies from multiply-boosted animals have dramatically higher affinity than from singly immunized animals — the cumulative effect of iterative affinity maturation.

**Q23. What are the different immunoglobulin isotypes produced during mouse immunization and what determines class switching?**

In mice, the immunoglobulin isotypes (heavy chain classes) are: IgM, IgD, IgG1, IgG2a (IgG2c in C57BL/6), IgG2b, IgG3, IgA, and IgE. Class switch recombination (CSR) is the irreversible DNA recombination event in activated B cells that changes the heavy chain constant region gene from Cµ (IgM) to another isotype, while preserving the same V(D)J variable region (same antigen specificity, different effector function).

**Determination of class switching**:
CSR is directed by cytokines from T helper cells (and innate cells), AID, and the activation state of the B cell. Each cytokine combination directs switching to specific isotypes:

| Cytokine signal | Isotype switched to | T helper subset producing cytokine |
|---|---|---|
| IL-4 | IgG1 (in mice); IgE | Th2 cells |
| IL-4 + IL-21 | IgE (high IL-4) | Th2 cells |
| IFN-γ | IgG2a / IgG2c | Th1 cells |
| TGF-β | IgG2b, IgA | Treg / TGF-β-producing cells |
| IL-5 + TGF-β | IgA | Tfh cells in Peyer's patches |
| LPS (TI signal) | IgG3 (direct, T-independent) | — |

**Adjuvant effect on isotype**: 
- FCA/FIA → Th1 bias → promotes IgG2a production.
- Alum → Th2 bias → promotes IgG1 and IgE.
- This explains why adjuvant choice affects not just the magnitude but the isotype distribution of the antibody response.

**Q24. What is the role of T follicular helper (Tfh) cells in the germinal center reaction during mouse immunization?**

**T follicular helper (Tfh) cells** (CXCR5⁺ PD-1^hi ICOS⁺ Bcl-6⁺ CD4⁺ T cells) are a specialized CD4⁺ T helper subset that migrates into B cell follicles and germinal centers to provide help to antigen-specific B cells — essential for high-affinity, class-switched antibody responses.

**Tfh cell functions in germinal centers**:
1. **Cognate help**: Tfh cells express CD40L (CD154); binding to CD40 on GC B cells delivers a survival signal — prevents B cell apoptosis and promotes GC B cell proliferation.
2. **Cytokine provision**: Tfh cells secrete IL-21 (primary Tfh cytokine — promotes B cell differentiation, SHM, and class switching), IL-4 (class switching toward IgG1/IgE), IL-2 (general B cell survival), and CXCL13 (chemokine that maintains GC architecture).
3. **Antigen selection**: Tfh cells present peptide-MHC II complexes to GC B cells — B cells that capture more antigen (higher affinity BCR → more efficient antigen uptake and presentation) receive more Tfh help → positive selection basis of affinity maturation.
4. **Memory cell fate**: Tfh signals determine whether GC B cells exit as long-lived plasma cells (high sustained BCR signals → plasma cell fate, high Blimp-1) or memory B cells (lower BCR signals → memory B cell fate, high Bcl-6 in memory B precursors).

**Relevance to immunization**: The quality and quantity of Tfh cell generation determines the quality of the germinal center reaction and thus the affinity and titer of the antibody response. Adjuvants that activate DCs to produce IL-6, IL-21, and ICOS-L strongly promote Tfh differentiation — explaining why adjuvanted immunization generates far better antibody responses than antigen alone.

**Q25. What is the significance of the dose of antigen used in IP immunization, and what happens if too much or too little antigen is given?**

**Antigen dose response** follows a bell-shaped curve:

**Too little antigen (below threshold)**:
- Insufficient antigen to activate enough antigen-specific B and T cell clones for a productive immune response.
- Result: No detectable antibody response (immunological ignorance — antigen present but threshold not met).
- Threshold dose varies by antigen immunogenicity (10 ng to 100 µg depending on the antigen).

**Optimal dose**:
- Sufficient antigen to activate a productive primary response; generates memory B and T cells; high antibody titer after boosting.
- Typically 10–100 µg protein per mouse per IP injection with adjuvant.

**Too much antigen (supraoptimal dose)**:
- **High zone tolerance (clonal exhaustion)**: Extremely high antigen doses can exhaust antigen-specific lymphocytes — particularly T cells. Repeated activation without adequate rest leads to T cell apoptosis (activation-induced cell death, AICD) through Fas-FasL interactions.
- **B cell unresponsiveness**: Very high antigen concentrations can induce anergy or deletion of B cells (particularly for T-independent antigens like LPS at high doses).
- **Immune deviation**: High doses may shift the response toward regulatory T cell (Treg) generation, suppressing rather than amplifying the response.
- Result: Paradoxically reduced or absent antibody response despite higher antigen dose.

**Practical implication**: For most protein antigens, 50–100 µg per IP injection with adjuvant is empirically optimal. Novel antigens require dose-titration studies (comparing responses at 1, 10, 100, 500 µg) to identify the optimal dose for maximum antibody titer without tolerance induction.

**Q26. What is ascites production and how does IP immunization relate to monoclonal antibody production by the hybridoma-ascites method?**

**Ascites production** is a method for generating large quantities of monoclonal antibody (mAb) using the peritoneal cavity of a mouse as a bioreactor. It exploits the IP route specifically:

**Hybridoma-ascites method**:
1. **Immunization (IP)**: Mouse immunized IP with antigen (as described above) — generates antigen-specific B cells.
2. **Splenectomy**: Spleen removed; contains immunized B cells (splenic plasma blasts and memory B cells).
3. **Cell fusion**: Splenic B cells fused with myeloma cells (SP2/0, NS0 — non-antibody-secreting myeloma lines of BALB/c origin) using PEG (polyethylene glycol) or electrofusion.
4. **Hybridoma selection**: In HAT medium (hypoxanthine-aminopterin-thymidine) — unfused myeloma cells die (lack HGPRT enzyme), unfused B cells die (short-lived in culture); only fused hybridomas (immortal + antibody-secreting) survive.
5. **Screening**: Supernatants tested by ELISA for antigen-specific antibody; positive hybridomas subcloned (limiting dilution) to ensure monoclonality.
6. **Ascites production**: Hybridoma cells (10⁶–10⁷) injected IP into syngeneic, pristane-primed BALB/c mice. Hybridoma cells grow in peritoneal cavity as a tumor; accumulate ascites fluid (peritoneal effusion) containing concentrated mAb (1–15 mg/mL); collect by peritoneal tap every 3–5 days.

**Yield**: 5–20 mL ascites per mouse containing 5–20 mg mAb/mL = 25–400 mg mAb per mouse — far more than cell culture alone.

**Animal welfare concern**: Ascites production causes significant morbidity (abdominal distension, pain, respiratory difficulty); EU and many countries have moved to restricting this method — preferring cell culture bioreactors where possible. IP ascites is still used when very large quantities are needed and cell culture is not feasible.

**Q27. What is pristane (2,6,10,14-tetramethylpentadecane) and why is it administered before hybridoma injection for ascites production?**

**Pristane** (2,6,10,14-tetramethylpentadecane) is a branched, saturated alkane mineral oil (MW 268.5 g/mol) administered IP to mice 1–3 weeks before hybridoma injection for ascites production.

**Functions of pristane priming**:
1. **Peritoneal priming (inflammation induction)**: Pristane is a sterile, non-antigenic lipid that induces a sterile inflammatory response in the peritoneum — recruiting macrophages, monocytes, and other myeloid cells. This inflammatory microenvironment creates conditions permissive for hybridoma growth.
2. **Tumor take enhancement**: Hybridoma cells require a supportive microenvironment for growth as a peritoneal tumor. Pristane-induced inflammation provides growth factors (IL-6, M-CSF, VEGF) and reduces natural killer cell activity, increasing the rate of successful tumor establishment.
3. **Ascites fluid accumulation**: Pristane itself causes some peritoneal fluid accumulation (direct irritant effect on peritoneal mesothelium); subsequent hybridoma growth amplifies this accumulation dramatically.
4. **Dose**: 0.5 mL pristane IP, administered 1–14 days before hybridoma injection.

**Modern alternatives**: Because pristane-induced plasmacytomas can be a source of morbidity and the procedure raises welfare concerns, many laboratories now use cell culture bioreactors (hollow fiber systems, wave bioreactors) for mAb production — avoiding pristane and ascites production entirely. Regulatory guidelines (CCAC, FELASA) recommend in vitro production as the preferred method, with in vivo ascites only when scientifically justified.

**Q28. What is the difference between a T-dependent and T-independent antigen, and how does the IP route differentially activate responses to each type?**

**T-dependent (TD) antigens**:
- Typically protein antigens with multiple, diverse epitopes including peptide sequences that can be processed and presented by MHC II to CD4⁺ T helper cells.
- Require cognate T cell help (CD40L-CD40, IL-21, cytokines) for full B cell activation.
- Generate: germinal center reactions, somatic hypermutation, affinity maturation, isotype class switching (IgG, IgA, IgE production), and long-lived memory B cells and plasma cells.
- IP immunization with protein antigens (with adjuvant) → strong TD response; antigen processed by peritoneal macrophages and dendritic cells → presented to CD4⁺ T cells → Tfh cell differentiation → GC reactions in mesenteric LN and spleen.

**T-independent (TI) antigens**:
- *TI-1 antigens* (e.g., LPS, flagellin): Contain TLR agonist activity that directly activates B cells via both BCR and TLR — mitogenic at high concentrations; produce polyclonal IgM without antigen specificity at high doses.
- *TI-2 antigens* (e.g., polysaccharides, repetitive structures): Repetitive epitopes crosslink multiple BCRs simultaneously, providing strong BCR signal without T cell help; activate predominantly marginal zone B cells (spleen) and B-1 B cells (peritoneum).
- Generate: rapid IgM production (within 48–72 hours); limited class switching (some IgG3 in mice); no somatic hypermutation; no long-lived memory.
- IP immunization with TI antigens: directly activates peritoneal B-1 B cells (abundant IP) and drains to spleen marginal zone B cells → rapid IgM response without GC formation.

**Practical implication**: For antibody production experiments, protein antigens (TD) with adjuvant are used for high-affinity, class-switched (IgG) antibody responses. Polysaccharide antigens (TI-2) are used to study TI responses; polysaccharide-protein conjugates (e.g., pneumococcal conjugate vaccine) convert TI to TD antigens for improved immune responses in infants and immunocompromised individuals.

**Q29. What are the expected antibody response kinetics following IP immunization in terms of serum IgM and IgG levels?**

Following IP immunization with protein antigen + FCA, the serum antibody response follows a characteristic temporal pattern:

**Primary response (after first IP immunization, Day 0)**:
- **Day 0–3**: No detectable antigen-specific antibody (lag phase — clonal expansion and differentiation not yet complete).
- **Day 4–7**: First detectable IgM (produced rapidly by short-lived plasma cells differentiated from marginal zone B cells and B-1 cells, before GC formation).
- **Day 7–14**: IgM peaks; IgG begins to appear (class switching in early GC reactions); IgG gradually increases.
- **Day 14–21**: IgM declining; IgG rising (GC-derived long-lived plasma cells producing class-switched antibody); first IgG peak.
- **Day 21–28**: IgM near baseline; IgG plateau at moderate level; memory B cells and long-lived plasma cells established.

**Secondary response (after booster immunization, e.g., Day 28)**:
- **Day 0–3 post-booster**: Rapid IgG rise (memory B cells rapidly activated; no lag phase).
- **Day 5–10 post-booster**: IgG peaks at 10–100× primary response peak; predominantly high-affinity IgG (affinity maturation complete).
- IgM response is minimal in secondary response (memory cells are class-switched to IgG).
- **Post-booster plateau**: High IgG maintained for weeks to months from long-lived plasma cells in bone marrow.

**Third immunization (2nd booster)**: Further IgG titer increase; antibody affinity continues to increase; IgG plateau is higher and more sustained.

**Q30. How does the IP route of immunization compare with subcutaneous immunization for polyclonal antibody production in terms of immune response quality?**

| Parameter | IP Immunization | SC Immunization |
|---|---|---|
| Antibody titer (serum IgG) | High; equivalent to SC for most antigens | High; equivalent to IP |
| Response onset | Slightly faster (IP antigen rapidly absorbed systemically) | Slightly slower (depot at SC site; slower drainage) |
| Depot effect with adjuvant | Good (FCA forms depot IP; may dissipate faster than SC) | Excellent (FCA granuloma at SC site persists weeks) |
| Antigen presenting cells first encountered | Peritoneal macrophages, omentum DCs | Skin Langerhans cells, dermal DCs, local subcutaneous macrophages |
| Lymphoid organs primarily activated | Mesenteric LN, spleen | Inguinal/axillary/brachial LN, spleen |
| Volume capacity | Larger (0.5–1.0 mL) | Smaller (0.05–0.2 mL per site; multiple sites used) |

| Preferred use | Large volume adjuvant preparations; when peritoneal immune activation is desired; ascites production | Most standard antibody production protocols (preferred for animal welfare); FCA primary SC + FIA boost SC most common |

For most research purposes producing polyclonal antisera, multiple SC injections at different sites (dorsal, inguinal) are now preferred over IP for welfare reasons, as SC granulomas are more easily monitored and less likely to cause internal pathology. IP remains preferred for ascites production and certain antigen types where the peritoneal compartment provides an advantage.

**Q31. What is meant by "hapten-carrier conjugate immunization" and when is this approach used in IP immunization of mice?**

**Haptens** are small chemical molecules (MW <1 kDa) — drugs, pesticides, hormones, synthetic chemicals — that can bind to antibodies but cannot independently stimulate an immune response (they lack T cell epitopes and are too small for effective BCR crosslinking). To make haptens immunogenic, they must be chemically conjugated to a larger **carrier protein** that provides T cell epitopes. This is the hapten-carrier concept:

**Common carrier proteins**: KLH (keyhole limpet hemocyanin, MW ~8 MDa — highly immunogenic in mice); BSA (bovine serum albumin, MW 67 kDa); OVA (ovalbumin, 45 kDa); thyroglobulin; toxoids.

**Coupling chemistry**:
- **Glutaraldehyde crosslinking**: Reacts with primary amines (lysine residues) on both hapten and carrier; non-specific but simple.
- **EDC/NHS (carbodiimide) chemistry**: EDC activates carboxyl groups on hapten → NHS ester reacts with primary amines on carrier; more controlled.
- **Diazotization**: For aromatic amines (aniline-type haptens).
- **MBS (maleimidobenzoyl-N-hydroxysuccinimide ester)**: Heterobifunctional crosslinker for amine + thiol coupling.

**IP immunization with hapten-carrier conjugate**:
- Carrier protein provides T cell epitopes → CD4⁺ T helper cell activation → T cell help to hapten-specific B cells.
- Hapten-specific B cells recognize the hapten portion through their BCR → receive T cell help directed against the carrier → class-switched, high-affinity anti-hapten antibodies generated.
- Used to produce antibodies against drugs (anti-morphine, anti-digoxin antibodies for immunoassays), environmental pollutants (anti-atrazine, anti-PCB antibodies), hormones (anti-cortisol, anti-estradiol), and other small molecules.

**Q32. What are the different types of controls used in a mouse IP immunization experiment?**

Proper experimental controls are essential for interpreting immunization results:

**Negative controls**:
1. **Naive (unimmunized) animals**: Mice receiving no injection; establish baseline (pre-immune) serum antibody levels. Essential for calculating signal-to-noise ratio in ELISA.
2. **Adjuvant-only control**: Animals receiving adjuvant (FCA/FIA) + PBS (no antigen) IP; controls for non-specific adjuvant effects on serum immunoglobulin levels and for false-positive ELISA results from adjuvant-induced non-specific antibodies.
3. **Vehicle control**: Animals receiving PBS only (no antigen, no adjuvant); controls for injection-related stress effects on immune parameters.

**Positive controls**:
4. **Previously validated immunization protocol**: Animals immunized with a well-characterized antigen (e.g., BSA or OVA) using the exact same schedule, dose, and adjuvant — confirms that the immunization protocol itself is working (the immune system of the mice is functional, the adjuvant is active, the technique is correct).
5. **Standard serum**: A known-positive serum (from a previously immunized mouse) used in each ELISA run as an inter-assay calibrator; ensures ELISA performance is consistent between experiments.

**Isotype controls (in ELISA)**:
6. **Normal mouse IgG**: Used as isotype-matched negative control in indirect ELISA — at the same concentration as the primary antibody; confirms that the secondary anti-mouse IgG antibody does not produce false-positive signal in the absence of specific primary antibody.

**Q33. How does the immune response to IP immunization differ between young adult and aged mice?**

**Immunosenescence** — the age-related decline in immune function — significantly affects the response to immunization:

**Young adult mice (8–12 weeks, optimal age for immunization)**:
- Full complement of naive B and T cells.
- Intact thymic output of new naive T cells.
- Robust germinal center reactions with efficient Tfh generation.
- Strong somatic hypermutation and affinity maturation.
- Efficient class switching; high IgG production.
- Peak antibody titers with minimal inter-individual variability.

**Aged mice (>18–24 months)**:
- **Thymic involution**: Thymus atrophies with age → reduced naive T cell output; naive T cell pool depleted by antigen exposure over lifetime. Fewer antigen-specific naive T cells available to respond to novel antigens.
- **Reduced Tfh differentiation**: Aged mice generate fewer Tfh cells; weaker GC reactions; reduced SHM and affinity maturation.
- **Clonal exhaustion**: Lifetime antigen exposure leaves some specific T cell clones exhausted (high expression of inhibitory receptors: PD-1, LAG-3, TIM-3).
- **Inflammaging**: Chronic low-grade inflammation in aged mice → elevated baseline IL-6, TNF-α → chronically activated immune system → reduced additional activation capacity (paradox of inflammation without immunity).
- **Consequences for immunization**: Lower peak antibody titers; reduced antibody affinity; less durable antibody response; higher inter-individual variability.
- **Practical implication**: Immunization experiments should use mice of consistent age (6–10 weeks for most studies); avoid using old mice for antibody production unless studying age-related immune changes specifically.

**Q34. What is the carrier effect and the carrier-specific T cell help in hapten-carrier immunization?**

The **carrier effect** describes the phenomenon where:
1. A hapten conjugated to carrier A generates anti-hapten antibodies via T cell help from carrier-A-specific T cells.
2. If the same animal is then immunized with hapten conjugated to a different carrier (carrier B), the anti-hapten secondary response does NOT occur efficiently — despite the animal being primed to the hapten.
3. This is because the carrier B-specific T cells are naive (no memory), and there is insufficient T cell help for hapten-specific B cells even though hapten-specific memory B cells are present.

**Molecular explanation**:
- B cells specific for the hapten (via BCR recognizing hapten) internalize the hapten-carrier conjugate, process the carrier protein portion, and present carrier peptides on MHC II.
- T cells must be carrier-specific (recognize carrier-derived peptides presented on APC MHC II) to provide CD40L, IL-21, and other help to the hapten-specific B cells.
- If T cells were primed against carrier A but are now encountering hapten-carrier B, they cannot recognize carrier B peptides → no T cell help → poor secondary anti-hapten response.

**Practical implications for immunization**:
- **Always use the same carrier protein for priming and boosting** — booster injections must use the same hapten-carrier conjugate (or same carrier with different hapten) to exploit carrier-primed T cell memory.
- In clinical vaccine design (e.g., pneumococcal conjugate vaccine), consistent carrier protein (e.g., CRM197, a non-toxic diphtheria toxin mutant) is used across all doses.

**Q35. What are the signs of a successful immune response in immunized mice, and how would you monitor them?**

**Clinical monitoring (welfare and response assessment)**:

1. **Body weight**: Weigh animals weekly. Modest weight loss (5–10%) immediately post-injection is normal (stress/adjuvant effects); >20% weight loss = humane endpoint criteria → veterinary assessment/euthanasia.

2. **Injection site inspection**: Palpate and inspect the injection area (IP — abdomen) at each monitoring. Normal: slight abdominal distension immediately post-injection (resolves within hours); subcutaneous: small nodule at injection site (FCA granuloma — may persist for weeks; is normal). Abnormal: large, ulcerating abscess; extreme distension; signs of peritonitis (hunched posture, piloerection).

3. **General health parameters**: Coat condition (ruffled fur = distress), posture (hunched = pain), activity level, food/water consumption, breathing rate. Document any abnormalities.

**Immunological monitoring**:

4. **Serum antibody titer (ELISA)**: Primary readout; perform at 10–14 days after each immunization. Compare with pre-immune serum titer. Expected: rising titer with each successive immunization.

5. **Immunoglobulin isotype analysis**: Determine which Ig classes are produced (IgM, IgG1, IgG2a, IgG2b, IgG3) by isotype-specific ELISA — provides information on Th1/Th2 bias and class switching.

6. **Splenocyte proliferation assay (if cellular immunity also studied)**: Harvest spleen; culture splenocytes with antigen in vitro; measure proliferation by [³H]-thymidine incorporation or CFSE dilution by flow cytometry — confirms T cell memory response.

7. **Lymph node/spleen histology (if animals euthanized at specific time points)**: Germinal center formation visible by H&E staining (large, pale-staining aggregates of proliferating lymphocytes in B cell follicles of lymph nodes/spleen); confirmed by immunohistochemistry (GL-7, PNA markers for GC B cells).

**Q36. What is the role of dendritic cells (DCs) in the immune response to IP immunization?**

Dendritic cells are the most potent antigen-presenting cells and the critical initiators of adaptive immune responses to IP-administered antigens:

**In the peritoneal cavity**: Small peritoneal dendritic cells (CD11c^hi MHC II^hi) sample antigens from the peritoneal fluid. Within hours of IP antigen injection, DCs capture antigen via:
- Macropinocytosis (bulk fluid uptake).
- Receptor-mediated endocytosis (FcR if opsonized; lectin receptors for glycosylated antigens; DEC-205, mannose receptor).

**DC maturation**: Adjuvant components (TLR agonists in FCA) trigger innate danger signals → DC maturation:
- Upregulation of MHC II, CD80, CD86, CD40, CD70 (costimulatory molecules).
- Upregulation of CCR7 (chemokine receptor for CCL19, CCL21 → directs migration to T cell zones of lymph nodes).
- Production of cytokines: IL-12 (Th1 differentiation), IL-6 + TGF-β (Th17 differentiation), IL-4 environment supports Th2.
- Upregulation of cross-presentation pathway (MHC I loading of exogenous antigens → CD8⁺ T cell activation).

**Migration**: Mature DCs migrate from the peritoneum via lymphatics to mesenteric lymph nodes (and via portal circulation to spleen) where they present processed antigen to naive CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells — initiating primary adaptive immune responses.

**Plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs)**: Also recruited to inflamed peritoneum; produce large amounts of type I interferons (IFN-α/β) in response to viral or CpG DNA stimuli — these interferons broadly activate NK cells, macrophages, and conventional DCs.

**Q37. Explain the concept of original antigenic sin (OAS) and its relevance to multiple boosting in mouse immunization.**

**Original Antigenic Sin (OAS)** (Francis, 1960) describes the tendency of the immune system to preferentially recall memory B cell responses to a previously encountered version of an antigen, even when a new variant of the antigen is present. The "sin" is the immunological commitment to the original antigenic experience:

**Mechanism**:
1. Primary immunization generates memory B cells with BCRs specific for Epitope A (on antigen version 1).
2. Subsequent exposure to antigen version 2 (which shares Epitope A but also has new Epitope B): Memory B cells for Epitope A are rapidly activated (days vs. 2 weeks for naive B cells) and rapidly dominate the response.
3. Naive B cells specific for the new Epitope B are outcompeted — they encounter less antigen (memory B cells capture most of it), receive less T cell help (T cells are also biased toward recalled Epitope A), and mount a weaker primary response to Epitope B.
4. Result: The antibody response to the second antigen version is dominated by antibodies against the shared Epitope A, with poor or absent response to the new Epitope B.

**Relevance to mouse immunization**:
- If sequential boosters use slightly different antigen preparations (different batches, different conjugation ratios of hapten-carrier, or antigen from different sources), OAS may cause suboptimal targeting of the new epitopes.
- **Practical implication**: Use the same, consistent antigen preparation for all immunizations in a series; if intentionally broadening the response to multiple epitopes (as in multi-epitope vaccines), use antigen cocktails in the primary immunization rather than sequential exposures to variant antigens.
- OAS is a major concern in influenza vaccination design — sequential flu vaccines may elicit responses biased toward childhood strain epitopes rather than the current circulating strain's unique epitopes.

**Q38. How would you design an IP immunization experiment to specifically generate a Th1-type versus a Th2-type immune response in mice?**

**Generating Th1-dominant response (for studying cellular immunity, IgG2a production, macrophage activation)**:

1. **Adjuvant**: FCA (primary), FIA (booster) — mycobacterial TLR2/4 activation strongly induces IL-12 from DCs/macrophages → IL-12 drives Th1 differentiation via STAT4 → IFN-γ production → IgG2a class switching.
2. **Alternatively**: MPL (TLR4 agonist) + CpG ODN (TLR9 agonist) combination — strong Th1 adjuvant system.
3. **Antigen**: Protein antigen that routes through MHC II and generates strong CD4⁺ Th1 responses.
4. **Mouse strain**: C57BL/6 — inherently biased toward Th1 responses due to IL-12 production capacity.
5. **Readout**: High IgG2c (IgG2a in BALB/c), IFN-γ production by splenocytes upon antigen re-stimulation in vitro, strong delayed-type hypersensitivity (DTH) responses.

**Generating Th2-dominant response (for studying allergy, IgE, IgG1, helminth immunity)**:

1. **Adjuvant**: Alum (aluminum hydroxide) — promotes Th2 via NLRP3 inflammasome activation (IL-1β, IL-18) but also via unclear mechanisms; does NOT induce IL-12; results in IL-4 bias.
2. **Protocol**: Alum (2 mg) + antigen (50 µg) IP → strong IgG1 and IgE production.
3. **Mouse strain**: BALB/c — inherently biased toward Th2 responses (higher baseline IL-4 production).
4. **Alternatively**: Low-dose antigen (sub-optimal) without TLR agonists — tends toward Th2 default in absence of innate danger signals.
5. **Readout**: High IgG1, IgE, IL-4/IL-5/IL-13 production by splenocytes upon antigen re-stimulation, eosinophilia, mast cell activation.

**Q39. What is the significance of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) haplotype in mouse immunization responses?**

The **MHC** (encoded by the H-2 gene complex in mice) is the primary genetic determinant of immune response magnitude and quality to specific antigens:

**H-2 haplotype diversity**:
- BALB/c: H-2d (IA^d MHC II; K^d, D^d MHC I)
- C57BL/6: H-2b (IA^b MHC II; K^b, D^b MHC I)
- CBA: H-2k
- DBA/2: H-2d

**Significance for immunization**:

1. **Peptide binding specificity**: Each MHC II allele has a unique peptide-binding groove that accommodates peptides with specific anchor residues. An antigen may have T cell epitopes (peptides that bind H-2d MHC) that are absent in H-2b context → BALB/c might respond vigorously to antigen X while C57BL/6 might respond poorly, or vice versa.

2. **Immune response (Ir) genes**: Historical name for MHC-linked control of T cell-dependent antibody responses. "Non-responder" strains fail to mount a response to certain antigens due to absence of appropriate MHC II-antigen peptide binding. This is why some antigens are weakly immunogenic in one strain but strongly immunogenic in another.

3. **Hybridoma production**: The myeloma fusion partner must be syngeneic with the immunized mouse (same MHC haplotype) — mismatched hybridomas would be rejected when implanted for ascites production. This is why BALB/c mice are paired with BALB/c-derived NS0 or Sp2/0 myeloma cells.

4. **Experimental design**: If an antigen is known to be a poor immunogen in one strain, switch to a different strain or use more potent adjuvant rather than increasing antigen dose (which may induce tolerance).

**Q40. What are the humane endpoints in mouse immunization experiments and when should they be applied?**

**Humane endpoints** are pre-defined criteria that trigger early termination of an animal experiment (by euthanasia) when the animal's suffering exceeds what is scientifically justified. They are established at the ethics committee approval stage before the experiment begins.

**Standard humane endpoints for IP immunization experiments**:

1. **Body weight loss**: >20% loss from starting weight over any 72-hour period → immediate veterinary consultation and likely euthanasia. >15% sustained loss over 7 days without recovery → intervention.

2. **Severe injection site reactions**: Large (>2 cm) abscess, necrosis, fistula formation, or spreading infection at or near injection site → treatment with antibiotics or euthanasia.

3. **Signs of peritonitis**: Severe abdominal distension unrelated to normal IP fluid; extreme hunching; failure to ambulate; cold to touch; cyanosis → immediate euthanasia.

4. **Signs of anaphylaxis**: Acute collapse, respiratory distress, cyanosis immediately post-injection → emergency intervention (epinephrine) or euthanasia.

5. **Moribund state**: Inability to reach food and water; lateral recumbency; failure to respond to external stimuli → euthanasia.

6. **Severe adjuvant arthritis**: Pronounced swelling of joints, inability to ambulate, signs of chronic pain (persistent hunching, piloerection, reduced grooming, grimace scale score >2/4) → euthanasia or termination of adjuvant use.

7. **Tumor development** (in ascites production): Abdominal circumference > 3–4 cm increase from baseline, severe respiratory compromise from ascites → drain or euthanize.

**Mouse grimace scale**: A validated pain assessment tool based on facial expressions (orbital tightening, nose/cheek flattening, ear position, whisker orientation) scored 0–2 for each parameter; widely adopted for monitoring pain in immunization experiments.

**Q41. A mouse immunized IP with 100 µg of a recombinant protein in FCA shows no detectable antibody response after 4 weeks. What are the possible causes and solutions?**

**Possible causes and solutions**:

**1. Wrong growth phase/health of mouse**:
- Immunosuppressed (infection, disease, age >18 months) → ensure health status; use young (6–8 week) mice.
- Poor body condition → nutritional assessment; switch to healthy cohort.

**2. Antigen quality issues**:
- **Protein denatured**: Recombinant proteins expressed in E. coli may be in inclusion bodies or denatured — verify native structure by SDS-PAGE under non-denaturing conditions, circular dichroism, or bioassay.
- **Endotoxin contamination**: High endotoxin in recombinant protein (from E. coli production) can paradoxically suppress adaptive immune responses at certain concentrations by inducing regulatory cells; test by LAL assay; remove endotoxin by Polymyxin B column or Triton X-114 phase separation.
- **Aggregation**: Check for protein aggregates by dynamic light scattering; aggregates are sometimes tolerogenic.
- **Wrong protein**: Verify protein identity and purity by SDS-PAGE and Western blot; wrong protein → no specific antibody.

**3. Antigen too similar to self (low foreignness)**:
- Highly conserved proteins (e.g., histones, ubiquitin, actin) are tolerized in mice due to evolutionary conservation → try immunization in a more distant species; modify the antigen (conjugation to KLH); use more potent adjuvant.

**4. Adjuvant failure**:
- FCA emulsion not properly formed → repeat drop test; if emulsion breaks on water → redo; FCA must form stable water-in-oil emulsion.
- FCA expired, improperly stored → check date; mycobacteria must be active.
- Dose delivered SC rather than IP (technique error) → verify correct anatomical placement; use radiographic verification if uncertain.

**5. Too few animals (statistical)**:
- Normal inter-animal variation; test more animals (n ≥ 5); exclude outliers using Grubbs' test.

**6. ELISA failure (false negative)**:
- Antibody present but not detected: wrong secondary antibody (anti-human IgG instead of anti-mouse IgG); incorrect antigen coating; blocking too stringent; low antigen coating concentration → revalidate ELISA; use known positive serum control.

**7. MHC non-responsiveness**:
- Antigen lacks peptides binding to the mouse's MHC II haplotype → switch to a different mouse strain; conjugate antigen to carrier protein (KLH provides additional T cell epitopes).

**Q42. What is the Jerne plaque assay and how is it used to enumerate antibody-secreting cells in immunized mice?**

The **Jerne plaque assay** (hemolytic plaque assay, Jerne and Nordin, 1963) is a cell-level assay that detects individual antibody-secreting cells (ASCs) by their ability to lyse antigen-coated red blood cells (RBCs) in the presence of complement:

**Principle**:
1. Spleen cells from immunized mice (containing antigen-specific plasma cells/ASCs) are mixed with sheep red blood cells (SRBCs) — the target antigen if anti-SRBC immunization was performed — in agarose gel.
2. Each antibody-secreting cell secretes antibodies that diffuse outward and bind to surrounding SRBCs.
3. Upon addition of complement, the antibody-coated SRBCs are lysed by the complement cascade → a clear "plaque" (zone of hemolysis) surrounds each ASC.
4. Count plaques under dissecting microscope → each plaque = one ASC.

**Direct plaques**: Detect IgM-secreting cells (IgM efficiently activates complement; appears in early primary response).

**Indirect plaques**: Detect IgG-secreting cells — requires additional anti-mouse IgG antibody added before complement (bridges IgG on RBCs to complement activation); detected in animals boosted to promote class switching.

**Applications**: Quantifying the number of antigen-specific ASCs in spleen, mesenteric lymph nodes, or bone marrow at different time points after IP immunization; comparing ASC numbers with different adjuvants, doses, or routes; studying the kinetics of the B cell response (numbers of ASCs peak at different times for IgM vs. IgG — directly measuring at the cellular level the kinetics described by serum antibody titers).

**Q43. What is the ELISPOT assay and how does it differ from the Jerne plaque assay in assessing B cell responses after IP immunization?**

**ELISPOT (Enzyme-Linked Immunospot Assay)** is a modern, sensitive method for detecting and enumerating individual cytokine-secreting T cells or antibody-secreting B cells (ASCs) at the single-cell level:

**Principle for B cell ELISPOT (Antibody ELISPOT)**:
1. PVDF (polyvinylidene fluoride) membrane-bottomed 96-well plate coated with antigen (the target of interest).
2. Spleen cells from immunized mice added to wells; incubate 4–6 hours (ASCs are short-lived; use immediately) or culture overnight.
3. Each ASC secretes antibodies that are captured locally on the antigen-coated membrane immediately surrounding the cell.
4. After washing, add enzyme (HRP or AP)-labeled anti-mouse IgG (or IgM) antibody.
5. Add chromogenic substrate (NBT/BCIP for AP or AEC for HRP) → each ASC produces a discrete colored "spot" on the membrane corresponding to the local antibody secretion.
6. Count spots using automated plate reader (ELISPOT reader/image analyzer) → each spot = one ASC.

**Comparison with Jerne plaque assay**:
| Feature | Jerne Plaque Assay | ELISPOT |
|---|---|---|
| Detection principle | Complement-mediated hemolysis | Enzyme-chromogenic |
| Target cells | IgM-ASCs (direct); IgG-ASCs (indirect) | IgG, IgM, IgA, or cytokine-secreting cells (isotype-specific) |
| Antigen types | RBC-bound antigens only | Any antigen that can coat the membrane |
| Specificity | Requires antigen-coated RBCs | More versatile; any protein/peptide |
| Sensitivity | Detects ~100 ASCs/10⁶ cells | Detects 1–50 ASCs/10⁶ cells (more sensitive) |
| Quantification | Manual counting under microscope | Automated; objective |
| T cell detection | No | Yes (cytokine ELISPOT: IFN-γ, IL-4, IL-17 for Th1, Th2, Th17) |
| Throughput | Low | High (96-well format) |

ELISPOT has largely replaced the Jerne plaque assay in modern immunology research due to its versatility, sensitivity, and ability to simultaneously assess both B and T cell responses.

**Q44. What is the difference between serum and plasma, and which is preferred for antibody titer assessment after immunization?**

**Serum**: The liquid component remaining after blood has clotted and the clot (fibrin matrix + platelets + RBCs) is removed by centrifugation. Serum LACKS clotting factors (fibrinogen, factors V, VIII, etc.) that were consumed in clotting. Contains all antibodies (Igs), complement proteins, albumin, and other plasma proteins except clotting factors.

**Plasma**: The liquid component of un-clotted blood, obtained by adding anticoagulant (EDTA, heparin, citrate) before centrifugation. Plasma CONTAINS all clotting factors including fibrinogen. Contains all antibodies and proteins.

**Preferred for antibody titer assessment: SERUM**

Reasons:
1. **No anticoagulant interference**: Anticoagulants in plasma (EDTA chelates metals; heparin is a polyanion) can interfere with ELISA by chelating ELISA plate-bound cations, disrupting antigen coating, or non-specifically inhibiting antibody binding.
2. **Cleaner matrix**: Absence of fibrinogen prevents fibrin clot formation during ELISA incubation (fibrinogen can polymerize in the plate wells, trapping antibodies non-specifically → false positives or variable results).
3. **Standard practice**: Immunization titer monitoring is universally reported in serum; consistency with literature values requires serum use.
4. **Complement**: For some functional assays (complement fixation, opsonization), fresh serum containing active complement is required (complement activity is partially destroyed by EDTA in plasma and by heparin at high concentrations).

**Collection**: Allow blood to clot (room temperature, 30–60 min); centrifuge (2,000 × g, 10 min); collect upper clear serum layer; store at −20°C or −80°C in aliquots.

**Q45. How do you calculate the minimum number of mice required for an IP immunization experiment and why is this important?**

**Sample size calculation** (statistical power analysis) is both an ethical requirement (Reduction principle of 3Rs) and a scientific necessity:

**Key parameters for power calculation**:
- **Effect size (d)**: The magnitude of difference expected between groups (e.g., difference in mean antibody titer between immunized and control groups, normalized by standard deviation).
- **Significance level (α)**: Probability of false positive (Type I error); conventionally α = 0.05.
- **Power (1-β)**: Probability of detecting a true effect; conventionally 80% (β = 0.2) or 90%.
- **Variability (SD)**: Estimated from pilot data or literature; immune responses in inbred mice have modest variability (CV ~15–30% for ELISA titers).

**Formula (for two-group comparison)**:

**n = 2 × [(z_α/2 + z_β)²  × σ²] / d²**

Where z_α/2 = 1.96 (α=0.05, two-tailed); z_β = 0.84 (80% power); σ = expected SD; d = expected mean difference.

**Practical example**: If expected difference in log₁₀ titer = 1.5 (one log), SD = 0.7 (log scale), α = 0.05, power = 80%:
n = 2 × [(1.96 + 0.84)² × 0.7²] / 1.5² = 2 × [7.84 × 0.49] / 2.25 = 2 × 3.84 / 2.25 = **3.4 ≈ 4 mice per group**

**Why important**:
- **Ethical**: Institutional ethics committees (IAEC/IACUC) require power calculations; using more animals than necessary is unethical (violates Reduction principle).
- **Scientific validity**: Too few animals → underpowered study → false negative results (miss real effects); too many → waste of animals and resources.
- **Regulatory**: Regulatory bodies require statistical justification for animal numbers in grant applications and ethical approvals.
- **Inbred mice**: Using inbred mice (BALB/c, C57BL/6) reduces variability compared to outbred stocks, allowing smaller group sizes while maintaining statistical power.

**Q46. What is polyclonal versus monoclonal antibody and what are the respective advantages of each for research and clinical applications?**

**Polyclonal antibodies (pAbs)**:
- Produced by multiple B cell clones, each specific for a different epitope on the antigen.
- Generated by animal immunization (mouse, rabbit, goat, sheep) + bleeding; contain a mixture of antibodies with different epitope specificities, affinities, and isotypes.

| Feature | Polyclonal Ab | Monoclonal Ab |
|---|---|---|
| Epitope specificity | Multiple epitopes on antigen | Single epitope |
| Batch consistency | Variable between animals/bleeds | Consistent (cell line) |
| Production | Simple (immunize → bleed) | Complex (hybridoma/recombinant) |
| Affinity | Variable (mixture of affinities) | Defined, single affinity |
| Cross-reactivity | Higher (binds related antigens) | Lower (single epitope specificity) |
| Isotype | Mixed (IgG, IgM, IgA) | Single defined isotype |
| Applications | Western blot, IHC, ELISA (robust; tolerates denatured antigen) | ELISA capture, flow cytometry, therapeutics, diagnostics requiring specificity |
| Clinical therapeutics | Not used (undefined specificity) | Widely used (Herceptin, Rituximab, Adalimumab, etc.) |
| Cost | Low | High (initial development) |

**Advantages of pAbs**: Recognize multiple epitopes → more robust signal in Western blot (multiple binding events even if protein partially denatured); faster to produce; higher total antibody titer achievable; useful when antigen identity is uncertain.

**Advantages of mAbs**: Absolute specificity for single epitope → essential for clinical diagnostics (no cross-reaction with similar antigens); unlimited supply from immortal cell line; defined affinity and isotype → reproducible quantification; therapeutic use (targeted therapy, checkpoint inhibitors, cancer immunotherapy).

**Q47. What is the lymph node response to IP immunization and how is it different from the splenic response?**

**Mesenteric lymph node (MLN) response**:
- Primary draining lymph nodes for IP antigens are the mesenteric lymph nodes (a chain of ~4–5 lymph nodes associated with the mesentery draining the small intestine and peritoneal cavity).
- IP-injected antigens drain via peritoneal lymphatics → mesenteric lymphatics → MLN.
- In the MLN: antigen is presented by locally resident DCs and macrophages to naive T cells in the paracortex → T cell activation → follicular migration → germinal center formation in cortical follicles → B cell activation and antibody production.
- MLN GCs form within 5–7 days of IP immunization.
- The MLN has a particular bias toward IgA production (due to gut-associated mucosal signals — RALDH2 enzyme in gut DCs imprints gut-homing receptors and IgA class switch signals on B cells migrating through the MLN).

**Splenic response**:
- Antigen reaches spleen via portal circulation and systemic lymphatics.
- In the spleen: marginal zone macrophages and DCs in the marginal zone capture blood-borne antigens → present in white pulp (T cell zone = periarteriolar lymphoid sheath, PALS; B cell zone = follicles).
- Marginal zone B cells (specialized for TI antigens) respond rapidly; conventional follicular B-2 cells form GCs in primary follicles (which become secondary follicles with GCs by Day 7–10).
- Spleen is the major site for systemic IgG production; particularly important for IP immunization.
- Spleen also activates NK cells and cytotoxic T cells in response to systemic antigen spread.

**Comparison**: The MLN primarily drives local (mesenteric/peritoneal) and mucosal immunity; the spleen drives systemic humoral (IgG) immunity. Both are activated by IP immunization, making IP one of the most effective routes for generating systemic antibody responses.

**Q48. What is opsonization and how does it relate to the antibodies produced by IP immunization?**

**Opsonization** is the process by which antibodies (and complement) coat the surface of a microorganism or foreign particle, making it more readily recognized and phagocytosed by macrophages and neutrophils. The term comes from the Greek "opsonin" meaning "to prepare for eating."

**Mechanism**:
1. Antigen-specific IgG (produced after IP immunization) binds to surface antigens on bacteria or foreign particles through its Fab (antigen-binding) regions.
2. The Fc (constant) region of the bound IgG protrudes outward, recognized by Fc receptors (FcγR) on macrophages and neutrophils.
3. FcγR binding triggers phagocytosis → microorganism engulfed, killed by reactive oxygen species and lysosomal enzymes within the phagolysosome.
4. IgG can also activate the classical complement pathway (IgG1, IgG3 in humans; IgG2a in mice → binds C1q → C3b deposition on pathogen surface → phagocytosis via complement receptor CR1, CR3).

**Relevance to IP immunization**:
- High-titer, high-affinity IgG produced after IP immunization (with FCA/FIA adjuvant, multiple boosters) is particularly effective at opsonization because:
  - High affinity → stable binding to pathogen surface.
  - High titer → abundant opsonins available in serum.
  - IgG2a isotype (Th1-biased response from FCA) has high affinity for FcγRIV (activating FcR in mice) → efficient macrophage activation.
- Passively transferred immune serum (containing opsonizing antibodies from IP-immunized mice) can protect naive mice against bacterial challenge — classical experiment demonstrating protective immunity through opsonizing antibodies.

**Q49. What is the peritoneal lavage technique and what can it reveal about the immune response to IP immunization?**

**Peritoneal lavage** (peritoneal wash) is a technique to collect cells and fluid from the peritoneal cavity for analysis of the local immune response:

**Procedure**:
1. Euthanize mouse by CO₂ asphyxiation or cervical dislocation.
2. Pin mouse on dissection board; wipe abdomen with 70% ethanol.
3. Carefully make a small incision in the skin over the abdomen; expose but do not open the peritoneal membrane.
4. Inject 5–10 mL of cold sterile PBS (with 3% fetal bovine serum or 2 mM EDTA to prevent cell clumping) through the peritoneal wall using a 23G needle.
5. Gently massage the abdomen for 30–60 seconds to detach adherent cells.
6. Make a small incision in the peritoneum; insert a Pasteur pipette; aspirate the lavage fluid.
7. Transfer to a 15 mL centrifuge tube; centrifuge (300 × g, 5 min, 4°C); pellet = peritoneal cells; supernatant = peritoneal fluid.

**Analysis of peritoneal cells after IP immunization**:
- **Differential cell count**: Cytospin preparation stained with Giemsa or May-Grünwald/Giemsa; count macrophages, lymphocytes, neutrophils, eosinophils, mast cells.
- **Flow cytometry**: Multi-parameter immunophenotyping — B-1 B cells (CD19⁺ CD23⁻ CD43⁺ B220^lo CD5⁺/−), B-2 B cells, T cells (CD4⁺, CD8⁺), NK cells (NK1.1⁺), DCs (CD11c^hi MHC II^hi), macrophages (F4/80⁺ CD11b⁺).
- **Expected changes after IP immunization**: Neutrophil influx within hours (acute inflammatory response to FCA); monocyte/macrophage increase by 24–48 hours; lymphocyte increase by Day 5–7 (antigen-specific lymphocyte recruitment); by Day 14–21, composition returns toward baseline but with increased B-1 B cell numbers and activated macrophages.
- **Peritoneal fluid antibody**: Measure IgM and IgG in the peritoneal supernatant — local antibody production at the site of IP immunization.

**Q50. Design a complete IP immunization experiment in mice to produce high-titer polyclonal antiserum against a novel recombinant bacterial protein for use in Western blotting and ELISA. Include all considerations from animal selection to final serum characterization.**

**Complete experimental design**:

---

**Step 1: Pre-experiment planning**

- **Ethical approval**: Submit IAEC protocol specifying: BALB/c mice (6–8 weeks, female), n=5 per group (4 immunized + 1 adjuvant control + 1 naive control = 6 animals total); antigen, adjuvant, dose, volume, schedule, endpoints, blood collection method, humane endpoints.
- **Antigen characterization**: Verify recombinant protein purity (>90%) by SDS-PAGE; confirm identity by Western blot with anti-His-tag or mass spectrometry; measure concentration by BCA assay; check for endotoxin (LAL assay; <1 EU/µg acceptable); check for aggregation by DLS or analytical SEC.
- **Statistical justification**: Based on expected coefficient of variation (CV 20–30% for ELISA titers) and desired effect size, n=4–5 per group provides 80% power at α=0.05.

---

**Step 2: Animal preparation**

- Animals: 5 × female BALB/c, 6–8 weeks old, 18–22 g; acclimatize for 7 days before experiment.
- Ear-tag or tail-mark for individual identification; record baseline body weight.
- Collect pre-immune blood (Day −3): 100 µL submandibular bleed; process serum; store at −80°C (negative control for ELISA).

---

**Step 3: Immunization schedule**

| Day | Event | Antigen | Adjuvant | Volume | Route |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Primary immunization | 100 µg protein in 0.25 mL PBS | 0.25 mL FCA (1:1 emulsion) | 0.5 mL | IP |
| 14 | 1st Booster | 50 µg protein in 0.25 mL PBS | 0.25 mL FIA (1:1 emulsion) | 0.5 mL | IP |
| 28 | 2nd Booster | 50 µg protein in 0.25 mL PBS | 0.25 mL FIA (1:1 emulsion) | 0.5 mL | IP |
| 35–38 | Test bleed | — | — | 100 µL | Submandibular |
| 39 | ELISA titer check | If titer ≥1:5000 → proceed to final bleed | — | — | — |
| 42 | 3rd Booster (if needed) | 25 µg protein in PBS (no adjuvant) | None | 0.5 mL | IP or IV |
| 45 | Terminal bleed | — | — | 1.0–1.5 mL | Cardiac puncture under terminal anesthesia |

---

**Step 4: Monitoring**

- Weigh animals weekly; record.
- Inspect IP injection sites daily for 72 hours post-injection.
- Apply grimace scale score at 24 and 48 hours post-injection.
- Apply humane endpoints: euthanize if weight loss >20%, peritonitis signs, or grimace score >2 sustained >24 hours.

---

**Step 5: Blood collection and serum preparation**

- Terminal cardiac puncture under isoflurane anesthesia (2–3%); collect into 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tube.
- Allow clotting (30 min, RT); centrifuge 2,000 × g × 10 min; collect serum.
- Pool serum from all 4 immunized mice (if titers similar); aliquot 50 µL per tube; store −80°C.

---

**Step 6: Serum characterization**

1. **ELISA titer**: Indirect ELISA with recombinant protein-coated plates; 2-fold serial dilutions from 1:100 to 1:102,400; determine endpoint titer. Target: ≥1:10,000.

2. **Isotype analysis**: Isotype-specific ELISA (anti-mouse IgG1, IgG2a, IgG2b, IgG3, IgM secondary antibodies); determine Th1/Th2 profile of response.

3. **Western blot specificity**: Run bacterial cell lysate or recombinant protein on SDS-PAGE; transfer to PVDF membrane; probe with 1:1,000 diluted antiserum (anti-mouse HRP secondary); confirm band at correct MW.

4. **Cross-reactivity assessment**: Western blot against related proteins or whole bacterial lysate; confirm no non-specific bands that would interfere with experimental use.

5. **ELISA validation**: Determine linear range, sensitivity (LOD, LOQ), coefficient of variation (CV inter-assay and intra-assay), and specificity using unrelated proteins as negative controls.

6. **Storage**: Aliquots at −80°C; avoid freeze-thaw; add BSA (0.1%) and sodium azide (0.02%) as stabilizer/preservative for long-term storage.
