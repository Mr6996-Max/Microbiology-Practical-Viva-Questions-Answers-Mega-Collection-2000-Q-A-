### SECTION A: BASIC PRINCIPLES & BACKGROUND (Q1–Q10)

---

**Q1. What is Kinema? Describe its origin, cultural significance, and nutritional importance.**

Kinema is a traditional fermented soybean food originating from the Eastern Himalayan regions of Nepal, Sikkim, Darjeeling, and parts of Bhutan and Northeast India. It is prepared by fermenting cooked whole soybeans, predominantly by *Bacillus subtilis*, resulting in a sticky, slightly mucilaginous, strongly flavored product with a characteristic pungent aroma. Culturally, it holds deep significance as a protein-rich food for communities where meat consumption is limited or seasonal, and it is consumed as a condiment, curry ingredient, or soup base. Nutritionally, kinema is a rich source of protein (18–20% dry weight), essential amino acids (particularly lysine and methionine), B vitamins (especially riboflavin and niacin), and minerals (calcium, iron, phosphorus). Fermentation enhances the bioavailability of nutrients by degrading anti-nutritional factors (trypsin inhibitors, phytates, tannins) present in raw soybeans, making kinema superior to unfermented soybeans in protein digestibility.

---

**Q2. What is the primary fermenting microorganism in kinema and why is it uniquely suited for this fermentation?**

The primary fermenting microorganism in kinema is *Bacillus subtilis* (and closely related species such as *B. subtilis* var. *natto*, also involved in Japanese natto fermentation). *B. subtilis* is uniquely suited because:
1. It is a thermophilic-tolerant, spore-forming bacterium that survives the initial cooking of soybeans and germinates when conditions become favorable.
2. It secretes high levels of protease (especially subtilisin/alkaline serine protease), amylase, and lipase, enabling extensive hydrolysis of soy protein to peptides and free amino acids, contributing to flavor and improved digestibility.
3. It grows rapidly at 37–45°C under the warm, semi-anaerobic conditions inside wrapped leaf bundles, outcompeting other microorganisms.
4. It produces poly-γ-glutamic acid (PGA), responsible for the characteristic sticky/slimy texture of kinema (as in natto).
5. It is a GRAS organism, safe for human consumption, and its endospores provide natural preservation.

---

**Q3. What biochemical changes occur in soybeans during kinema fermentation? Discuss proteolysis, lipolysis, and changes in anti-nutritional factors.**

**Proteolysis:** *B. subtilis* secretes potent alkaline proteases (subtilisin) and neutral proteases that hydrolyze soy storage proteins (glycinin 11S, β-conglycinin 7S) into peptides and free amino acids. Total free amino acid content increases 3–5-fold during fermentation, contributing to flavor (umami from glutamic acid) and improved digestibility. Non-protein nitrogen (NPN) increases from ~5% to 40–50% of total nitrogen.

**Lipolysis:** Lipases secreted by *B. subtilis* hydrolyze soybean triglycerides to free fatty acids and glycerol. Linoleic acid and other polyunsaturated fatty acids are released, and some undergo further oxidation, contributing to the characteristic strong aroma of kinema.

**Anti-nutritional factors:** Trypsin inhibitors (Kunitz and Bowman-Birk inhibitors) are heat-inactivated during the initial cooking step and further degraded by bacterial proteases during fermentation. Phytates (phytic acid) are hydrolyzed by phytases produced by *B. subtilis*, improving iron, zinc, and calcium bioavailability. Tannins and oligosaccharides (raffinose, stachyose — responsible for flatulence) are also degraded.

**Q4. Compare kinema with other Bacillus-fermented soybean foods worldwide. What are the similarities and differences?**

| Primary organism | *B. subtilis* | *B. subtilis* var. *natto* | *B. subtilis* | *B. subtilis* |

All these products share *B. subtilis* as the primary fermenter, alkaline pH development, sticky texture (PGA), and strong aroma. Kinema is distinguished by its use of local leaves as the wrapping/inoculation material and the multi-day fermentation at ambient tropical/subtropical temperatures.

**Q5. What is poly-γ-glutamic acid (PGA) and what is its role in kinema fermentation?**

Poly-γ-glutamic acid (PGA) is a naturally occurring, water-soluble, biodegradable biopolymer produced by *B. subtilis* and related species, consisting of D- and L-glutamic acid units linked by γ-peptide bonds (between the γ-carboxyl group and the α-amino group). In kinema fermentation, PGA is secreted into the fermentation matrix and is primarily responsible for the characteristic sticky, mucilaginous, thread-pulling texture of kinema — similar to the stringiness of Japanese natto. PGA synthesis is encoded by the *pgs* operon (*pgsB, pgsC, pgsA, pgsE* genes) and is regulated by carbon source, nitrogen availability, and temperature. Beyond texture, PGA acts as a humectant (moisture retainer), contributes to the binding of flavor compounds, and may have probiotic or prebiotic effects in the gut. PGA is also studied for biotechnological applications in drug delivery, bioremediation, and food preservation.

**Q6. What is the role of the wrapping leaves (banana leaves, fern leaves) in kinema fermentation? Can kinema be made without them?**

The wrapping leaves (banana leaves, fig leaves, or local fern leaves such as *Diplazium* spp.) serve multiple critical roles in kinema fermentation:
1. **Source of inoculum:** The leaves harbor indigenous *B. subtilis* spores on their surfaces, which serve as the natural inoculum for traditional kinema. This is why fermentation initiates without the addition of a starter culture in traditional practice.
2. **Microenvironment creation:** Leaves create a warm, humid, semi-anaerobic microenvironment that favors *B. subtilis* growth while inhibiting obligate aerobes.
3. **Moisture retention:** Leaves prevent excessive drying of the fermenting soybeans.
4. **Flavor contribution:** Volatile compounds from the leaves contribute subtle flavor notes to the final product.
5. **Anti-contamination:** Certain leaf extracts may have mild antimicrobial activity against undesirable bacteria.

Kinema can be made without leaves using pure *B. subtilis* starter cultures in controlled fermenters; this approach is used in standardized laboratory and industrial preparations and produces more consistent results, though the product may lack the complex flavor notes derived from leaf-associated microorganisms.

**Q7. Explain the biochemical basis of the alkaline pH development during kinema fermentation. Why does pH rise?**

During kinema fermentation, pH rises from the initial acidic/neutral pH of cooked soybeans (~6.0–6.5) to an alkaline pH of 7.5–8.5 by the end of fermentation (48–72 h). This alkalinization occurs due to:
1. **Proteolysis:** Degradation of soy proteins releases free amino acids, particularly basic amino acids (lysine, arginine, histidine) which are positively charged at physiological pH and consume protons, raising pH.
2. **Deamination:** Some amino acids undergo deamination, releasing NH₃, which reacts with water to form NH₄OH — a weak base that directly raises pH.
3. **Consumption of organic acids:** Any lactic or acetic acid initially present is rapidly consumed by *B. subtilis* metabolism.
4. **Urease activity:** Hydrolysis of urea (naturally present in soybeans) by bacterial urease: Urea + H₂O → 2NH₃ + CO₂; the ammonia raises pH.
The alkaline pH is critical for product safety (inhibits most acid-sensitive pathogens) and also optimizes the activity of *B. subtilis* alkaline proteases (optimal pH 8.0–10.0), creating a positive feedback loop that amplifies proteolytic activity as pH rises.

**Q8. What are the anti-nutritional factors in soybeans and how does kinema fermentation address them?**

Soybeans contain several anti-nutritional factors (ANFs) that limit their nutritional value when consumed raw or improperly processed:

| Anti-nutritional factor | Effect | How kinema addresses it |
|---|---|---|
| Trypsin inhibitors (Kunitz, Bowman-Birk) | Inhibit protein digestion | Inactivated by boiling; further degraded by *B. subtilis* proteases |
| Phytic acid (phytate) | Chelates minerals (Fe, Zn, Ca), reducing bioavailability | Hydrolyzed by *B. subtilis* phytase to inositol + free phosphate |
| Tannins | Reduce protein digestibility | Degraded by *B. subtilis* tannase |
| Oligosaccharides (raffinose, stachyose) | Cause flatulence (non-digestible) | Hydrolyzed by α-galactosidase of *B. subtilis* |
| Lectins (hemagglutinins) | Agglutinate red blood cells, reduce nutrient absorption | Inactivated by heat during boiling |
| Saponins | Bitter taste, haemolytic | Partially hydrolyzed during fermentation |
| Goitrogens | Interfere with thyroid function | Reduced during cooking and fermentation |

The net result is that kinema has significantly higher in vitro protein digestibility (~85–90%) compared to cooked but unfermented soybeans (~70–75%).

**Q9. What are the key enzymes produced by Bacillus subtilis during kinema fermentation and their roles?**

*Bacillus subtilis* produces an extensive enzymatic arsenal during kinema fermentation:

1. **Alkaline serine protease (subtilisin, subtilisin Carlsberg):** Major protease responsible for extensive soy protein hydrolysis; optimal pH 8.0–10.5, temperature 50–60°C. Encoded by *aprE* gene.
2. **Neutral metalloprotease:** Active at pH 6.0–8.0; contributes to mid-fermentation proteolysis. Encoded by *nprE* gene.
3. **α-Amylase:** Hydrolyzes soy starch/oligosaccharides to maltose and glucose; contributes to substrate for bacterial growth.
4. **Lipase:** Hydrolyzes triglycerides to free fatty acids and glycerol, contributing to aroma development.
5. **Phytase:** Hydrolyzes phytic acid, releasing inorganic phosphate and improving mineral bioavailability.
6. **Urease:** Converts urea to NH₃ + CO₂, contributing to alkalinization.
7. **Catalase/superoxide dismutase:** Protect the bacterium against oxidative stress during aerobic growth phases.
8. **β-Glucanase/Cellulase:** Minor activity on plant cell wall polysaccharides in soybean hull.
9. **Lecithinase (phospholipase):** Degrades soybean phospholipids.

The collective action of these enzymes transforms raw cooked soybeans into the nutritionally enhanced, flavored product characteristic of kinema.

**Q10. Discuss the microbial succession during kinema fermentation. Is Bacillus subtilis the only organism involved?**

While *B. subtilis* dominates kinema fermentation, microbial succession occurs in distinct phases:

**Initial phase (0–6 h):** Diverse microflora from the leaf surface and environment, including coliforms, lactic acid bacteria (LAB), wild yeasts, and moulds, may be transiently present. However, the high cooking temperature (100°C) kills most vegetative bacteria initially, selecting for heat-resistant *B. subtilis* spores.

**Active fermentation phase (6–48 h):** *B. subtilis* spores germinate in the warm, moist environment, rapidly proliferating and dominating the fermentation. pH rises to 7.5–8.5, further inhibiting most other bacteria. Yeast (*Candida*, *Saccharomyces*) may be present in small numbers.

**Late fermentation/storage:** If fermentation continues beyond 72 h or storage is prolonged, contaminating organisms may grow. *Micrococcus*, *Staphylococcus* (non-pathogenic cohalides), and yeasts may be found. Moulds (*Aspergillus*, *Mucor*) may appear on the surface under aerobic storage conditions.

**Other Bacillus species:** Besides *B. subtilis*, *B. licheniformis*, *B. pumilus*, and *B. amyloliquefaciens* have also been isolated from kinema and may contribute to flavor development and enzyme production. Modern molecular studies (16S rRNA gene sequencing, metagenomics) have confirmed *B. subtilis sensu lato* as the dominant species constituting >80% of the microbiome during peak fermentation.

### SECTION B: PROCEDURE & METHODOLOGY (Q11–Q25)

---

**Q11. Describe the complete step-by-step procedure for preparation of kinema in the laboratory.**

**Materials:** Soybeans, distilled water, banana/fern leaves, incubator (40°C), muslin cloth.

**Procedure:**
1. **Cleaning and soaking:** Sort soybeans to remove damaged seeds and foreign matter. Wash thoroughly with tap water. Soak in excess water for 12–18 hours at room temperature to allow adequate hydration (beans should absorb 2× their dry weight in water).
2. **Dehulling (optional):** Soaking loosens the seed coat; rubbing soaked beans between palms and washing removes hull fragments. Dehulling improves texture and reduces tannin content but is not always done in traditional preparation.
3. **Cooking:** Boil dehulled/whole soybeans in excess water for 3–4 hours (or pressure cook at 15 psi for 30–45 min) until the beans are fully tender and can be easily crushed between fingers. Drain excess water.
4. **Cooling and draining:** Drain cooked beans on a wire mesh or spread on a clean surface; allow to cool to ~40°C (not completely to room temperature, as warm beans support faster spore germination).
5. **Wrapping:** Transfer warm cooked beans onto cleaned banana leaves (or fern leaves). Wrap tightly into packets (~200–300 g per packet); secure with string or bamboo splints.
6. **Incubation:** Place wrapped packets in a warm, humid incubation chamber at 37–40°C for 36–72 hours. Maintain high humidity (>85%) to prevent drying. In traditional practice, packets are kept near a hearth or in a warm corner.
7. **Monitoring:** After 24 h, open one packet to check for development of white mycelium-like growth (*B. subtilis* colony appearance), characteristic ammonia/pungent smell, stickiness, and pH (should be 7.5–8.0).
8. **Completion:** After 48–72 h when the beans are sticky, well-fermented, and pH 8.0–8.5, fermentation is complete.
9. **Post-fermentation:** Product is sun-dried (traditional) or used fresh. For laboratory analysis: measure pH, moisture, protein content, free amino acids, and perform microbial counts.

---

**Q12. Why are soybeans soaked for 12–18 hours before cooking? What happens at the cellular level during soaking?**

Soaking is critical for:
1. **Rehydration:** Dry soybeans have ~10% moisture; soaking increases moisture to ~60–65%, softening the seed coat and cotyledons for easier cooking.
2. **Leaching of anti-nutritional factors:** Water-soluble oligosaccharides (raffinose, stachyose), some saponins, and tannins leach into the soaking water, reducing their content in the final product. The soaking water should be discarded.
3. **Enzyme activation:** Soaking activates endogenous seed enzymes (lipases, proteases, phytases) that begin to modify seed composition.
4. **Uniform cooking:** Fully hydrated beans cook uniformly, preventing the outer layers from overcooking while the interior remains hard.
5. **Softening of cell walls:** Water penetration begins to disrupt cell wall integrity, facilitating later bacterial protease access during fermentation.

At the cellular level: water diffuses across the seed coat via osmosis into the starchy cotyledons, cell walls swell, protein bodies (aleurone grains containing storage proteins glycinin and β-conglycinin) begin to hydrate, and lipid bodies (oleosomes) redistribute. The seed's moisture uptake follows a sigmoidal hydration curve, with the rate determined by seed coat permeability and temperature.

---

**Q13. What is the purpose of cooking/boiling soybeans before fermentation? What would happen if raw soybeans were used?**

Cooking serves multiple essential purposes:
1. **Inactivation of heat-labile ANFs:** Trypsin inhibitors, lectins (hemagglutinins), and lipoxygenase (responsible for beany flavor) are all heat-denatured at 100°C, making the beans nutritionally safe.
2. **Protein denaturation:** Native soybean proteins (glycinin 11S, β-conglycinin 7S) are tightly folded; heat denaturation unfolds them, exposing peptide bonds to bacterial protease attack — essential for efficient proteolysis during fermentation.
3. **Sterilization:** Boiling kills vegetative bacteria, yeasts, and moulds on the bean surface, while heat-resistant *B. subtilis* endospores survive and serve as the primary inoculum.
4. **Starch gelatinization:** Starch granules swell and gelatinize, making carbohydrates more accessible to amylases.
5. **Softening texture:** Fully cooked beans have the soft texture needed for the sticky, cohesive consistency of kinema.

If raw soybeans were used: trypsin inhibitors would remain active, severely limiting protein digestibility; the hard seed coat would prevent enzyme penetration; non-*Bacillus* bacteria would compete effectively; and fermentation would be much slower and less complete.

**Q14. How would you determine the optimum fermentation time for kinema production in a laboratory setting?**

To determine optimum fermentation time, set up kinema preparations as described in Q11 and sample at regular intervals (0, 12, 24, 36, 48, 60, 72, 96 h). At each time point, measure:
1. **pH:** Should rise from ~6.5 to 8.0–8.5; plateau indicates completion.
2. **Free amino nitrogen (FAN):** Measured by ninhydrin or OPA (o-phthalaldehyde) assay; FAN rises with increasing proteolysis.
3. **Degree of hydrolysis (DH%):** Measured by OPA method: DH% = (FAN at time t / Total amino nitrogen after complete hydrolysis) × 100.
4. **Texture (stickiness):** Assessed subjectively (pulling test — degree of stringiness) or instrumentally (texture profile analysis with a Texture Analyser).
5. **Microbial count:** Viable plate count on nutrient agar to track *B. subtilis* population (should peak at 24–48 h and remain high).
6. **Sensory evaluation:** Panel assessment of aroma (ammonia, pungency), taste, and texture.
7. **Moisture content and weight loss.**

Optimum fermentation time is when pH is 7.5–8.5, FAN is maximized, stickiness is acceptable, and sensory characteristics are optimal — typically 48–72 h at 37–40°C.

**Q15. How do you measure free amino nitrogen (FAN) in kinema? Describe the ninhydrin method.**

**Principle:** Ninhydrin (2,2-dihydroxyindane-1,3-dione) reacts with free amino acids and primary amines (but not intact proteins) to form Ruhemann's purple (diketohydrindylidene-diketohydrindamine) at 570 nm. Proline and hydroxyproline (secondary amines) give a yellow product at 440 nm. The reaction requires heating (100°C) in an alkaline environment.

**Procedure:**
1. Extract kinema with 5% trichloroacetic acid (TCA) to precipitate proteins; centrifuge.
2. Take 1 mL supernatant (diluted appropriately).
3. Add 1 mL ninhydrin reagent (ninhydrin in ethylene glycol monoethyl ether + sodium acetate buffer pH 5.5, with KCN as reducing agent).
4. Heat in boiling water bath for 15 min; cool.
5. Add 5 mL 60% ethanol; vortex.
6. Read absorbance at 570 nm (Ruhemann's purple).
7. Compare against glycine standard curve (0–1 mg/mL).

Results are expressed as mg glycine equivalents per g dry weight of kinema. An increase in FAN reflects the degree of proteolysis achieved during fermentation.

**Q16. How is the degree of proteolysis (degree of hydrolysis, DH%) measured in kinema? What is the OPA method?**

**Degree of Hydrolysis (DH%)** = (Number of peptide bonds cleaved / Total peptide bonds present) × 100

**OPA (o-phthalaldehyde) method:**
The OPA reagent reacts with primary amines (α-NH₂ groups of amino acids and N-terminal amines of peptides) in the presence of 2-mercaptoethanol to form a fluorescent/chromogenic isoindole compound, measured at 340 nm (absorbance) or 455 nm (fluorescence).

**Procedure:**
1. Prepare OPA reagent: dissolve OPA (40 mg) in methanol (1 mL), add 2.5% sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS, 25 mL), 0.1 M sodium tetraborate buffer pH 9.3 (50 mL), and 2-mercaptoethanol (100 μL); make to 50 mL with distilled water.
2. Mix 200 μL diluted sample with 1.5 mL OPA reagent; incubate 2 min at RT.
3. Read absorbance at 340 nm.
4. Use serine as standard (1 serine = 1 NH₂ group); construct standard curve.
5. DH% = [(FAN sample − FAN initial) / (FAN total − FAN initial)] × 100
   where FAN total = FAN after complete acid hydrolysis (6N HCl, 110°C, 24 h).

**Q17. How would you enumerate Bacillus subtilis from kinema? Describe selective plating methods.**

**Sample preparation:** Weigh 10 g kinema, add 90 mL sterile peptone water (0.1%), and homogenize for 2 min in a stomacher or blender. Prepare serial dilutions (10⁻¹ to 10⁻⁷).

**Selective plating methods:**

1. **Heat shock enrichment:** Heat diluted sample at 80°C for 10 min to kill non-spore-forming bacteria; only *Bacillus* spores survive. Plate heat-shocked sample on nutrient agar (NA); incubate at 37°C for 24–48 h. White, rough, irregular colonies with a matt surface are characteristic of *B. subtilis*.

2. **Tryptose blood agar base with polymyxin (TBAB-P):** Polymyxin B inhibits Gram-negative bacteria; *B. subtilis* colonies appear as flat, irregular, rough, cream-to-white colonies.

3. **MYP agar (Mannitol-Yolk-Polymyxin):** Used primarily for *B. cereus* but aids differentiation of Bacillus spp.

4. **Confirmation:** Pick colonies; perform Gram staining (Gram-positive rods with endospores), catalase test (positive), oxidase test (negative for *B. subtilis*), motility (positive), lecithinase test, Voges-Proskauer (VP) test (positive — acetoin production), and 16S rRNA gene sequencing for definitive identification.

Results expressed as log₁₀ CFU/g of kinema.

**Q18. What media would you use to detect contamination with Bacillus cereus in kinema? Why is this important?**

*B. cereus* is an important food safety concern in kinema because it is a spore-forming, heat-resistant organism that can produce emetic toxin (cereulide — a heat-stable dodecadepsipeptide) and diarrheal toxins (enterotoxins: HBL, NHE, CytK). Since *B. cereus* is closely related to *B. subtilis* and grows under similar conditions, it may contaminate kinema and produce toxins during fermentation or storage.

**Selective media for B. cereus:**
1. **MYP (Mannitol-Yolk-Polymyxin) agar:** *B. cereus* produces lecithinase (pink precipitate around colonies) and does NOT ferment mannitol (colonies remain red/pink on red background). Polymyxin B suppresses most other bacteria.
2. **PEMBA (Polymyxin-Pyruvate-Egg yolk-Mannitol-Bromothymol Blue Agar):** Similar principle; *B. cereus* colonies appear turquoise/blue with egg yolk precipitation halo.
3. **Chromogenic BCM agar:** *B. cereus* forms characteristic colored colonies (blue/green) due to specific enzyme activity.

**Significance:** Indian and Nepali food safety standards stipulate *B. cereus* counts <10³ CFU/g in fermented foods. High counts (>10⁵ CFU/g) pose a risk of food poisoning (vomiting onset 1–6 h for emetic type, diarrhea 6–24 h for diarrheal type). Proper hygiene, controlled fermentation temperature, and short fermentation time minimize *B. cereus* risk in kinema.

**Q19. How is pH monitored during kinema fermentation? What does a pH profile during fermentation look like?**

**pH monitoring:**
- Use a calibrated pH meter with a penetration electrode or flat-surface pH electrode for direct measurement on the kinema surface/interior.
- Alternatively, prepare a 1:10 (w/v) homogenate of kinema in distilled water, allow to equilibrate for 15–20 min, and measure pH with a standard glass electrode pH meter.
- pH indicator strips can be used for rapid, approximate measurement.

**Typical pH profile:**
- 0 h (freshly cooked soybeans): pH 6.0–6.5
- 12 h: pH 6.5–7.0 (minimal change; early bacterial establishment)
- 24 h: pH 7.0–7.5 (active proteolysis, ammonia release begins)
- 36 h: pH 7.5–8.0 (exponential *B. subtilis* growth, peak protease secretion)
- 48 h: pH 8.0–8.5 (maximum alkalinization)
- 72 h: pH 8.2–8.8 (stable or slight further rise)

The characteristic rapid rise in pH from ~12–24 h onwards is a reliable indicator of *B. subtilis* activity. If pH fails to rise, fermentation may not be proceeding correctly (check inoculum viability, temperature, and moisture).

**Q20. How would you assess moisture content and water activity (aW) of kinema? Why are these parameters important?**

**Moisture content:**
- **Gravimetric method (oven drying):** Weigh sample (W₁), dry at 105°C for 24 h in a hot-air oven, reweigh (W₂).
- Moisture (%) = [(W₁ − W₂)/W₁] × 100
- Fresh kinema moisture: ~55–65%; dried kinema: ~8–12%.

**Water activity (aW):**
- Measured with a water activity meter (chilled mirror dew-point hygrometer, e.g., AquaLab) or using calibrated salt solutions in a sealed chamber.
- aW = (relative humidity at equilibrium / 100) = p/p₀ (vapor pressure of sample / vapor pressure of pure water).
- Fresh kinema: aW ~0.95–0.98.
- Sun-dried kinema: aW ~0.60–0.70.

**Importance:**
- Fresh kinema (aW >0.95) supports rapid microbial growth, limiting shelf life to 2–3 days at room temperature.
- Drying to aW <0.70 inhibits most bacteria and moulds, extending shelf life to months.
- aW below 0.85 prevents *S. aureus* growth; below 0.70 prevents most bacterial growth.
- Water activity affects texture, enzymatic activity (proteolysis continues slowly even after primary fermentation), and consumer acceptability.

**Q21. How would you perform a proximate analysis (nutritional composition) of kinema? List all parameters and methods.**

Proximate analysis determines the major nutritional components:

1. **Moisture:** Hot-air oven at 105°C, 24 h (gravimetric). Alternatively, Karl Fischer titration for accurate measurement.
2. **Crude protein:** Kjeldahl method — digest in concentrated H₂SO₄ (+ catalyst), distill released NH₃ into boric acid, titrate with HCl. Crude protein = Total nitrogen × 6.25 (conversion factor for most legumes).
3. **Crude fat (ether extract):** Soxhlet extraction using diethyl ether or petroleum ether for 8–16 h; dry and weigh residue.
4. **Crude fiber:** Successive digestion with 1.25% H₂SO₄ and 1.25% NaOH; ash the residue; fiber = weight loss on ashing.
5. **Ash:** Muffle furnace at 550°C for 5–6 h; residue = total mineral content.
6. **Carbohydrate (by difference):** Carbohydrate (%) = 100 − (moisture + protein + fat + fiber + ash).
7. **Energy:** Calculated: Energy (kcal) = 4 × protein (g) + 4 × carbohydrate (g) + 9 × fat (g).
8. **Additional analyses:** Calcium (AAS), iron (AAS), Vitamin B₂ (fluorimetry), total phenolics (Folin-Ciocalteu), phytate (AOAC method).

Fresh kinema: ~20% protein, 8–10% fat, 15–20% carbohydrate (dry weight basis), with improved bioavailability compared to unfermented soybean.

**Q22. How is total protein estimated in kinema by the Kjeldahl method? What are the sources of error?**

**Principle:** Organic nitrogen in the sample is converted to ammonium sulfate by digestion with concentrated H₂SO₄ in the presence of catalysts (K₂SO₄ + CuSO₄ or Se catalyst). The NH₃ released by NaOH treatment is distilled into boric acid and titrated with standard HCl.

**Procedure:**
1. **Digestion:** Weight 0.5–1 g sample into Kjeldahl flask; add 10 mL conc. H₂SO₄ + Kjeldahl catalyst tablet; heat in digestion block at 420°C until clear green-blue solution.
2. **Distillation:** Add 40% NaOH; steam distil NH₃ into 4% boric acid + mixed indicator (methyl red + bromocresol green).
3. **Titration:** Titrate boric acid-NH₃ mixture with 0.1 N HCl until color changes from green to pink.
4. **Calculation:**
   - mg N = (Volume HCl × Normality × 14) / 1000 × 1000
   - Protein (%) = (mg N / mg sample) × 6.25 × 100

**Sources of error:**
- Incomplete digestion (non-protein nitrogen-containing compounds underestimated; non-digestible matrices overestimated).
- Nitrogen conversion factor 6.25 assumes 16% N in protein; soy protein factor is more accurately 5.71 (due to higher lysine content).
- Volatile nitrogen loss during digestion (if temperature is too high initially).
- In kinema, high free amino acid content may cause slight overestimation of protein due to inclusion of non-protein nitrogen in the Kjeldahl digest.
- Contamination from reagents.

**Q23. Describe how you would prepare a standardized Bacillus subtilis starter culture for controlled kinema production.**

**Starter culture preparation:**
1. **Selection:** Isolate *B. subtilis* from well-fermented kinema by serial dilution and plating on NA; select colonies with characteristic morphology and confirm by Gram stain, endospore staining, biochemical tests (catalase+, oxidase−, VP+, motility+), and 16S rRNA sequencing.
2. **Cryopreservation:** Maintain master stock in 20% glycerol at −80°C.
3. **Working culture:** Transfer from master stock to NA slants; incubate 37°C, 24 h.
4. **Spore preparation:** Inoculate into Schaeffer's Sporulation Medium (SSM); incubate 37°C, 5–7 days with aeration until >80% sporulation (confirmed by phase-contrast microscopy and malachite green spore stain). Centrifuge at 5,000 rpm, wash spore pellet 3× with sterile distilled water, resuspend to 10⁸–10⁹ spores/mL.
5. **Quality control:** Check spore viability by heat shock (80°C, 15 min) + plating; check purity by Gram stain; check protease activity on skim milk agar (clear zones around colonies).
6. **Inoculation:** Add 1–2 mL of spore suspension per 100 g cooked soybeans (spraying or mixing), then wrap and incubate.
7. **Documentation:** Record batch number, spore count, protease activity, and fermentation performance for each starter batch.

**Q24. What is the role of salt in kinema? When and why is it sometimes added after fermentation?**

Salt (NaCl) is not traditionally added during kinema fermentation — it is added after fermentation is complete (post-fermentation salting) or at the time of cooking for consumption. The reasons for adding salt post-fermentation rather than before:

**Why not added before/during fermentation:**
1. Salt inhibits *B. subtilis* growth at concentrations above 5–8% (w/v), reducing fermentation rate and yield.
2. *B. subtilis* proteases are inhibited by high ionic strength.
3. Salt reduces water activity, slowing microbial activity.

**Why added after fermentation:**
1. **Preservation:** Salt lowers aW, extending shelf life by inhibiting spoilage microorganisms.
2. **Flavor:** Salt enhances the savory (umami) flavor of kinema, balancing its ammonia-derived pungency.
3. **Texture:** Salt can modify texture by partially dehydrating the product.

In some preparations, dry sun-salted kinema (with ~5–10% NaCl) has a shelf life of several months at room temperature. In laboratory-prepared kinema for immediate analysis, salt is omitted to avoid interference with analytical measurements (particularly protein and moisture analysis).

**Q25. What quality control parameters would you check in freshly prepared kinema before declaring it fit for consumption?**

**Microbiological quality control:**
- Total viable count (TVC) on nutrient agar: acceptable <10⁶ CFU/g for a fermented product
- *B. cereus* count on MYP agar: <10³ CFU/g
- Coliforms (E. coli) on MacConkey agar: absent in 1 g
- *Staphylococcus aureus* on Baird-Parker agar: <10² CFU/g
- Yeast and mould count on potato dextrose agar: <10³ CFU/g
- Absence of Salmonella and Listeria (enrichment methods per ISO standards)

**Physico-chemical quality control:**
- pH: 7.5–8.5 (alkaline pH confirms *B. subtilis* fermentation)
- Moisture content: 55–65% (fresh), <12% (dried)
- Protein content: >18% (dry weight basis)
- Free amino nitrogen: indicative of degree of proteolysis
- Free fatty acid value: indicator of rancidity (should be <5% in fresh product)

**Sensory quality control:**
- Appearance: White to creamy-white mold-like growth on bean surface; no black/green mold
- Aroma: Characteristic pungent, ammonia-like fermented smell; no putrid or fecal odor
- Texture: Sticky, mucilaginous; beans should hold together when pulled

**Q26. If 500 g of dry soybeans are used for kinema preparation and each gram of soybean protein has a nitrogen content of 16%, calculate the expected crude protein content using the Kjeldahl method after fermentation, given that soybeans contain 40% crude protein on dry weight basis.**

**Crude protein in raw soybeans:**
- 500 g soybeans × 40% protein = 200 g protein
- Nitrogen in 200 g protein = 200 × 16/100 = 32 g nitrogen
- Kjeldahl crude protein = 32 g N × 6.25 = 200 g (consistent with the assumption)

**After fermentation:**
Fermentation does not create new protein but redistributes existing protein into peptides and amino acids. However, water loss during fermentation (evaporation) concentrates the product:
- If 500 g dry soybeans absorb ~1000 mL water during soaking/cooking (final wet weight ~1400 g), and fermentation causes ~10% weight loss (evaporation + CO₂), final weight = ~1260 g.
- Protein mass remains ~200 g (assuming no protein lost in drip/wash water).
- Protein % (wet basis) = 200/1260 × 100 = **15.9% wet weight basis**.
- On dry weight basis (65% moisture in fresh kinema → 35% dry matter): Protein = 200/(1260 × 0.35) × 100 = **45.4% dry weight basis**.

Note: Fermentation may slightly increase apparent protein content on dry weight basis due to net water loss, even though absolute protein mass is conserved.

**Q27. A kinema sample shows FAN (free amino nitrogen) increasing from 0.5 mg/g at 0 h to 8.2 mg/g at 48 h. If the total amino nitrogen (after complete acid hydrolysis) is 22 mg/g, calculate the degree of hydrolysis (DH%) at 48 h.**

**DH% formula:**
DH% = [(FAN at time t − FAN initial) / (FAN total − FAN initial)] × 100

**Calculation:**
DH% = [(8.2 − 0.5) / (22 − 0.5)] × 100
= [7.7 / 21.5] × 100
= **35.8%**

This means approximately 35.8% of all peptide bonds in the soybean proteins have been cleaved by 48 h of fermentation. A DH% of 30–40% at 48–72 h is typical for well-fermented kinema. Higher DH% correlates with increased free amino acid content, improved digestibility, and stronger flavor. DH% above 50% may indicate over-fermentation and excess ammonia production (unacceptably pungent product).

**Q28. If 10 g of kinema is homogenized in 90 mL peptone water and serially diluted to 10⁻⁶, and plating 0.1 mL of the 10⁻⁵ dilution on nutrient agar after heat shock gives 235 colonies, calculate the Bacillus count per gram of kinema.**

**Q29. Calculate the theoretical increase in free amino acids if 18 g of soy protein per 100 g dry kinema is completely hydrolyzed to constituent amino acids. Assume average molecular weight of an amino acid residue is 110 Da and average peptide length before fermentation is 300 residues.**

**Number of moles of protein (polypeptide chains) initially:**
- Assume average molecular weight of intact soy protein = 300 residues × 110 Da = 33,000 Da = 33 kDa
- Moles of protein in 18 g = 18 / 33,000 g/mol = 5.45 × 10⁻⁴ mol

**Moles of amino acid residues (total peptide bonds + 1 per chain):**
- Total residues = (18 g) / (110 g/mol) = 0.1636 mol of amino acid residues
- Total peptide bonds = 0.1636 − 5.45 × 10⁻⁴ ≈ 0.163 mol (approximately = total residues since chains are long)

**After complete hydrolysis:**
- All 0.163 mol residues become free amino acids
- Mass of free amino acids = 0.163 mol × 127 g/mol (average MW of free amino acid including water addition per peptide bond) ≈ 0.163 × 127 ≈ **20.7 g free amino acids per 100 g dry kinema**

This shows that complete proteolysis converts 18 g protein to approximately 20.7 g free amino acids — the apparent increase (about 15%) is due to the water molecules added during hydrolysis of each peptide bond (H₂O addition per bond cleavage: MW 18 Da per bond).

**Q30. A researcher prepares kinema at three fermentation temperatures: 30°C, 37°C, and 45°C. At 72 h, pH values are 7.1, 8.3, and 7.4 respectively, and FAN values are 3.2, 8.5, and 4.8 mg/g. What conclusion can you draw?**

|---|---|---|---|
| 30°C | 7.1 | 3.2 | Suboptimal; slow growth, poor proteolysis |
| 37°C | 8.3 | 8.5 | Optimal; maximum protease activity, best fermentation |
| 45°C | 7.4 | 4.8 | Above optimum; some protease denaturation, reduced growth |

**Q31. Calculate the protein digestibility corrected amino acid score (PDCAAS) concept for kinema protein. Why is PDCAAS a better measure than crude protein content?**

**PDCAAS concept:**
PDCAAS = Amino Acid Score (AAS) × True Digestibility (TD)
- AAS = (mg of limiting amino acid per g test protein) / (mg of same amino acid per g reference protein [FAO/WHO pattern])
- TD = fraction of dietary protein absorbed in the small intestine (measured in rat assays or in vitro digestion)

**For kinema (illustrative calculation):**
- Kinema protein contains methionine + cysteine (sulfur amino acids, SAA) as the first limiting amino acids: ~22 mg SAA/g protein
- FAO/WHO reference for SAA (adults): 25 mg/g protein
- AAS = 22/25 = 0.88
- True digestibility of fermented soy protein ≈ 0.90 (improved over raw soy 0.78 due to protease action)
- PDCAAS = 0.88 × 0.90 = **0.79**

PDCAAS is better than crude protein (%) because:
1. It accounts for amino acid quality (balance of essential amino acids).
2. It corrects for actual protein digestibility (not all dietary protein is absorbed).
3. It is capped at 1.00 (proteins with PDCAAS >1.00 are truncated to 1.00) to prevent overclaiming.
4. It reflects the protein's ability to meet human amino acid requirements, not just total nitrogen content.

**Q34. How would you determine the phytate content of kinema and calculate the molar ratio of phytate:zinc? Why is this ratio important?**

**Phytate estimation (AOAC method/Wade method):**
1. Extract phytate from sample with 2.4% HCl (2 h, stirring).
2. Add FeCl₃ solution to precipitate iron-phytate complex; centrifuge.
3. Wash precipitate, dissolve in NaOH.
4. Determine iron by atomic absorption spectrophotometry (AAS) or colorimetrically (thiocyanate method).
5. Calculate phytate from Fe content (Fe:phytate molar ratio = 4:1 in iron-phytate complex).
6. Alternatively, use HPLC with UV detection at 500 nm using Wade reagent (ferric ammonium sulfate + sulfosalicylic acid).

**Molar ratio calculation (illustrative):**
- Phytate in kinema: 3.5 mg/g dry weight = 3.5/660 mmol/g = 5.3 μmol/g (MW of phytic acid = 660 g/mol)
- Zinc in kinema: 2.8 mg/g dry weight = 2.8/65.4 μmol/g = 42.8 μmol/g
- Phytate:Zinc molar ratio = 5.3/42.8 = **0.12**

**Significance:** A phytate:zinc molar ratio <0.5 indicates good zinc bioavailability; ratios >15 predict severe zinc deficiency. Kinema with extensive phytate hydrolysis by *B. subtilis* phytase (reducing phytate by 50–70% vs. raw soybeans) has excellent zinc bioavailability.

**Q35. Design a simple experiment to compare the protein digestibility of raw soybeans, cooked soybeans, and kinema. What statistical analysis would you apply?**

**Experimental design:**
- **Treatments (n=3 each, triplicate measurements):**
  1. T1: Raw soybeans (ground to flour)
  2. T2: Cooked soybeans (boiled 3 h, ground)
  3. T3: Kinema (48 h fermented, ground)

- **In vitro protein digestibility (IVPD):**
  1. Suspend each sample (10 mg protein equivalent) in 10 mL phosphate buffer pH 8.0.
  2. Add pepsin (pH 1.5, 37°C, 60 min), then pancreatin + bile salts (pH 7.5, 37°C, 4 h) — two-step in vitro digestion (Hsu et al. or INFOGEST protocol).
  3. Centrifuge; measure protein in supernatant (Bradford or Lowry method).
  4. IVPD (%) = (Protein in supernatant / Total protein) × 100.
  5. **In vivo (rat bioassay):** Net protein utilization (NPU) or protein efficiency ratio (PER) using Wistar rats fed each diet for 28 days.

- **Statistical analysis:** One-way ANOVA with Tukey's post-hoc test for multiple comparisons (p<0.05 significance). Report means ± SD. Expected results: IVPD — T3 (kinema) > T2 (cooked) > T1 (raw), with significance between all groups.

### SECTION D: TROUBLESHOOTING & PROBLEM SOLVING (Q36–Q43)

**Q36. You prepared kinema but it does not develop the characteristic sticky texture after 48 hours. What could be the reasons and how would you troubleshoot?**

Lack of stickiness indicates insufficient PGA (poly-γ-glutamic acid) production, which requires active *B. subtilis* fermentation. Troubleshooting:

1. **Insufficient moisture:** If the wrapped beans dried out, *B. subtilis* growth is suppressed. Check: unwrap a packet; if beans are dry, re-wrap with addition of a few drops of sterile water or wet muslin.
2. **Low inoculum:** Insufficient *B. subtilis* spores on the wrapping leaves (old, dried leaves have fewer viable spores). Remedy: Use fresh leaves or add a pure culture starter.
3. **Temperature too low:** Below 30°C, *B. subtilis* growth is very slow. Confirm incubator temperature; increase to 37–40°C.
4. **Undercooked soybeans:** Hard beans are not permeable to bacterial enzymes. Verify cooking adequacy (beans should crush easily between thumb and forefinger).
5. **PGA inhibition:** Some trace metals (particularly high copper or iron from process water) may inhibit PGA synthetase. Use distilled or filtered water for soaking and cooking.
6. **Wrong Bacillus strain:** Not all *B. subtilis* strains produce high PGA levels; if using a laboratory culture, verify the strain produces PGA (test on glutamate-containing medium for PGA production).
7. **pH too low:** If pH <6.5 at 48 h, *B. subtilis* growth may be inhibited; check by pH meter and allow additional fermentation time.

**Q37. The kinema you prepared has a very strong, unpleasant ammonia smell, much stronger than typical kinema. What has gone wrong?**

Excessive ammonia odor indicates over-fermentation or contamination with highly urease-active or proteolytic organisms. Possible causes:

1. **Over-fermentation:** Fermenting beyond 72 h at optimal temperature leads to excessive amino acid deamination, releasing NH₃ beyond what contributes to flavor.
   - Remedy: Reduce fermentation time (check at 36–48 h and stop when pH reaches 7.5–8.0 and texture is acceptable).
2. **Temperature too high:** At >45°C, certain thermophilic *Bacillus* strains with higher urease/protease activity may dominate, producing excessive ammonia.
   - Remedy: Control fermentation temperature to 37–40°C.
3. **Contamination with Bacillus cereus or other ammonia-producing bacteria:** Some *B. cereus* strains are highly proteolytic. Check by plating on MYP agar.
4. **Excess water:** Very wet beans support faster growth and higher ammonia production.
   - Remedy: Drain cooked beans more thoroughly before wrapping.
5. **Poor quality soybeans:** High protein content from certain soybean varieties may result in more ammonia from extensive proteolysis.

Remediation: Sun-drying with salt addition post-fermentation helps to dissipate ammonia and stabilize the product. Starting fermentation again from scratch with better temperature control is the definitive solution.

**Q38. After preparing kinema, you observe green and black mold growth on the surface. Is this normal? How would you prevent it?**

Green and black mold growth is NOT a feature of properly fermented kinema. *B. subtilis* colonies appear white, powdery-surfaced, and wrinkled — they can superficially resemble mold but are bacterial colonies. Green (e.g., *Penicillium*, *Aspergillus flavus*) or black (e.g., *Aspergillus niger*, *Aspergillus carbonarius*) growth indicates fungal contamination, which is a quality defect and potential safety hazard (particularly *Aspergillus flavus* which produces aflatoxins).

**Causes:**
1. Prolonged aerobic storage of kinema after fermentation.
2. Insufficient pH rise (allowing mold growth alongside bacteria).
3. Contaminated packaging material.
4. High moisture content (aW >0.85) during storage.

**Prevention:**
1. Store fresh kinema at 4°C immediately after fermentation; consume within 2–3 days.
2. Sun-dry kinema to reduce moisture content (aW <0.70) before storage.
3. Salt the product post-fermentation.
4. Use clean, sterilized wrapping materials.
5. Ensure proper pH alkalinization (pH >7.5) during fermentation — high pH inhibits most fungi.
6. Vacuum-pack or modified atmosphere packaging (CO₂/N₂) for extended shelf life.

**Q39. How would you investigate the source of Bacillus subtilis in traditional kinema making? Design a study to characterize the microflora of wrapping leaves.**

**Objective:** Identify and characterize *B. subtilis* strains on wrapping leaves used in traditional kinema preparation.

**Sample collection:** Collect fresh banana leaves and fern leaves (*Diplazium* spp.) from different geographical locations (3–5 villages, 3–5 different plants per location, different seasons).

**Microbial enumeration:**

**Q40. A student measured the pH of kinema at 48 h and got a reading of 5.8. What does this indicate and what corrective actions would you suggest?**

A pH of 5.8 at 48 h indicates that typical *B. subtilis* alkaline fermentation has NOT occurred. This suggests:

1. **Failed *B. subtilis* fermentation:** Dominant microorganism may be lactic acid bacteria (LAB) instead of *B. subtilis*, producing lactic acid and keeping pH low (LAB typically maintain pH 4.5–5.5). This can occur if: leaves were contaminated with LAB; inoculation temperature allowed LAB to establish before *B. subtilis* could dominate; or the *B. subtilis* inoculum was non-viable.

2. **Undercooked soybeans:** If beans were not fully cooked, their interior remains acidic (soybeans contain organic acids).

3. **pH meter malfunction:** Calibrate pH meter with fresh buffer standards (pH 4.0 and pH 7.0 buffer sachets).

**Corrective actions:**
- Check pH meter calibration; repeat measurement in 1:10 homogenate.
- Perform microscopic examination: if rods are short and occur in chains, LAB contamination is likely (vs. *B. subtilis*: long rods, sporulating, motile).
- Plate on NA (heat-shocked) and MRS agar (for LAB) to determine dominant microorganism.
- Discard the batch; prepare fresh kinema with confirmed *B. subtilis* starter culture, ensuring adequate cooking temperature and clean wrapping materials.
- For future preparations, inoculate with 1–2% *B. subtilis* spore suspension sprayed on cooked beans before wrapping.

**Q41. How would you test the protease activity of Bacillus subtilis isolated from kinema? Describe both qualitative and quantitative methods.**

**Qualitative method (skim milk agar/casein agar):**
1. Prepare skim milk agar: dissolve 10 g skim milk powder + 15 g agar in 1 L distilled water; autoclave separately and mix.
2. Pour plates; allow to solidify.
3. Spot inoculate or place disc with isolated *B. subtilis* colony.
4. Incubate at 37°C, 24–48 h.
5. **Positive protease:** Clear (transparent) zone (halo) around growth = casein hydrolysis. Measure zone diameter.

**Quantitative method (azocasein assay):**
1. Prepare enzyme extract: culture *B. subtilis* in BHI broth, 37°C, 24 h; centrifuge (5,000 rpm, 10 min); use cell-free supernatant as enzyme source.
2. Mix 100 μL enzyme extract + 900 μL 0.5% azocasein (dye-labelled casein) in 50 mM Tris-HCl buffer, pH 8.0.
3. Incubate 30 min at 40°C (optimal for *B. subtilis* neutral protease) or 55°C (for alkaline protease).
4. Stop with 10% TCA (200 μL); vortex; centrifuge.
5. Transfer supernatant to tube; add equal volume 1 N NaOH; read OD₄₄₀.
6. One unit of protease activity = amount of enzyme causing ΔOD₄₄₀ of 0.001/min under standard conditions.
7. Express as units/mL culture supernatant.

**Q42. During kinema fermentation, you observe liquid collecting at the bottom of the wrapping leaves. What does this indicate, and is it desirable?**

The liquid collecting at the bottom of kinema wrapping is exudate (soybean juice/broth) containing soluble proteins, peptides, free amino acids, sugars, and PGA secreted during fermentation. This is generally undesirable for the following reasons:

1. **Nutrient loss:** Valuable amino acids and peptides leach out of the product, reducing nutritional quality and yield.
2. **Texture impact:** Excessive moisture loss from the beans can make the product less cohesive/sticky.
3. **Microbial growth in exudate:** The liquid-filled space may support growth of aerobic bacteria or fungi, creating a contamination risk.
4. **Yield loss:** Total solid yield is reduced.

However, small amounts of exudate are normal and indicate active fermentation (protein hydrolysis releases water-soluble peptides). In some traditional preparations, this exudate is collected and used as a soy sauce-like condiment.

**Causes of excessive exudate:**
- Too much water remaining on beans after draining (inadequate draining after cooking).
- Very high temperature causing faster liquefaction of proteins.

**Prevention:** Drain cooked soybeans more thoroughly before wrapping; use porous wrapping material that allows some moisture escape while maintaining humidity.

**Q43. A student found that kinema prepared using banana leaves fermented well, but kinema prepared using plastic wrap did not develop proper fermentation. Explain this observation.**

This observation demonstrates the critical role of wrapping leaf material as the inoculum source and microenvironment creator:

**Banana leaves (successful fermentation):**
- Carry indigenous *B. subtilis* spores on their surface (natural inoculum).
- Porous structure allows limited gas exchange (CO₂ out, minimal O₂ in), creating semi-anaerobic microenvironment favoring *B. subtilis*.
- Release phenolic compounds and natural extracts that may inhibit competing bacteria.
- Provide moisture regulation through controlled transpiration.

**Plastic wrap (failed fermentation):**
- Completely impermeable and sterile — no indigenous *B. subtilis* inoculum.
- Creates anaerobic environment; if any fermenting bacteria are absent, no fermentation occurs.
- No selective advantage for *B. subtilis*; any contaminating organisms that do enter (during wrapping process) may grow instead.
- May create excessive moisture condensation (anaerobic, very wet conditions) that promotes *Clostridium* or other anaerobic bacteria.

**Conclusion and remedy:** Plastic wrap can be used ONLY if a pure *B. subtilis* starter culture is sprayed on the cooked soybeans before wrapping. For traditional kinema, fresh biological wrapping materials (banana, fig, or fern leaves) are essential, or a defined *B. subtilis* inoculum must supplement the substrate.

**Q44. Discuss the potential health benefits of kinema consumption beyond basic nutrition. What bioactive compounds are produced during fermentation?**

Kinema fermentation generates several bioactive compounds with demonstrated health benefits:

1. **Nattokinase (fibrinolytic enzyme):** *B. subtilis* in kinema produces a serine protease with fibrinolytic (clot-dissolving) activity, similar to the nattokinase of Japanese natto. This enzyme degrades fibrin clots and may reduce cardiovascular disease risk (thrombosis prevention).

2. **γ-Polyglutamic acid (PGA):** Functions as a prebiotic and may stimulate beneficial gut microbiota; also has antioxidant and immunomodulatory properties.

3. **Isoflavone transformation:** Soybean isoflavones (daidzin, genistin — glycoside forms with poor bioavailability) are hydrolyzed by *B. subtilis* β-glucosidase to aglycone forms (daidzein, genistein) which have greater estrogenic, antioxidant, and anticarcinogenic activity.

4. **Bioactive peptides:** Proteolysis generates peptides with ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme) inhibitory activity (antihypertensive), antioxidant activity (DPPH radical scavenging), and immunostimulatory activity.

5. **Vitamin K₂ (menaquinone-7, MK-7):** *B. subtilis* synthesizes vitamin K₂, important for bone metabolism and cardiovascular health (activates osteocalcin and matrix Gla protein).

6. **Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs):** Fermentation produces butyrate and other SCFAs that promote intestinal health and epithelial integrity.

**Q45. What is the significance of Bacillus subtilis spore formation in the context of kinema safety and shelf life? Describe the sporulation process.**

**Sporulation significance:**
Endospores of *B. subtilis* confer exceptional resistance to heat (survive 100°C for 30+ min), desiccation, UV radiation, chemicals, and extreme pH, making them natural preservation agents. In kinema, spores ensure:
1. Survival of the fermenting organism through the initial cooking step (spores survive boiling).
2. Long-term viability of dried kinema (spores remain viable for years in dried products).
3. Safety concern: Unlike most bacteria, *B. subtilis* spores can survive in the finished product and may germinate under favorable conditions during storage if aW rises.

**Sporulation process (Stages 0–VII):**
- **Stage 0:** Vegetative cell; nutrient depletion triggers Spo0A phosphorylation.
- **Stage I:** Chromosome replication and asymmetric cell division begins.
- **Stage II:** Asymmetric septum formation creating forespore and mother cell compartments.
- **Stage III:** Engulfment of forespore by mother cell (forespore surrounded by double membrane).
- **Stage IV:** Cortex synthesis (spore-specific peptidoglycan layer between membranes).
- **Stage V:** Coat protein deposition (inner and outer protein coat layers).
- **Stage VI:** Maturation; DPA (dipicolinic acid) accumulation, dehydration of core, heat resistance acquisition.
- **Stage VII:** Mother cell lysis; release of mature endospore.

Duration: 6–8 hours at 37°C. Key regulatory protein: Spo0A (master regulator), σF (forespore), σE/σG/σK (mother cell sigma factors).

**Q47. What role does kinema play in sustainable food systems and food security in the Himalayan region? Discuss socioeconomic aspects.**

Kinema plays a multifaceted role in sustainable food systems of the Eastern Himalayan region:

**Nutritional security:** As a protein-rich, affordable food (cost of soybeans is low and production requires no specialized equipment), kinema provides essential amino acids, minerals, and vitamins to populations where animal protein is expensive or culturally restricted (vegetarian communities, Buddhist populations).

**Economic sustainability:** Kinema production is a cottage industry, predominantly managed by women, generating additional household income during winter months when agricultural work is limited. Soybean cultivation itself is well-suited to the region's climate and improves soil nitrogen through biological nitrogen fixation.

**Food preservation:** Traditional kinema production (sun-drying, salting) enables long-term preservation of soybeans without refrigeration — critical in remote mountain communities with limited access to cold chains.

**Cultural preservation:** Kinema is part of culinary heritage; its continued consumption and production maintains traditional knowledge of fermentation and local biodiversity (species of wrapping leaves).

**Challenges:** Lack of standardization leads to variable product quality and shelf life; limited commercial-scale production prevents market expansion; food safety concerns (microbial contamination) need to be addressed for urban/export markets. Standardization using defined starter cultures and hygienic processing could expand kinema's market reach while preserving its traditional character.

**Q49. Discuss the molecular mechanisms by which Bacillus subtilis alkaline protease (subtilisin) acts on soy proteins. What is the catalytic mechanism?**

**Substrate proteins:** Soy storage proteins — glycinin (11S, MW ~360 kDa, hexameric structure) and β-conglycinin (7S, MW ~150–200 kDa, trimeric structure) — are the primary targets of subtilisin during kinema fermentation.

**Catalytic mechanism of subtilisin (serine protease):**
1. **Catalytic triad:** Asp32-His64-Ser221 (in subtilisin BPN'; numbering varies by isoform). Serine (Ser221) acts as the nucleophile, histidine (His64) as the general base, and aspartate (Asp32) orients and stabilizes His64.
2. **Step 1 (Acylation):** His64 abstracts a proton from Ser221-OH, activating it as a nucleophile. Ser221 attacks the carbonyl carbon of the scissile peptide bond in the substrate, forming a tetrahedral intermediate, stabilized by the oxyanion hole (backbone NH of Ser221 and Asn155).
3. **Step 2:** The tetrahedral intermediate collapses; C-N bond breaks; the leaving amino group accepts a proton from His64; acyl-enzyme intermediate is formed.
4. **Step 3 (Deacylation):** Water molecule attacks the acyl-enzyme; His64 acts as general base; second tetrahedral intermediate forms; product carboxylate is released; enzyme is regenerated.

**Substrate specificity:** Subtilisin has broad specificity; prefers bulky hydrophobic residues at P1 position (leucine, phenylalanine, tyrosine), consistent with the hydrophobic interior of soy protein globules. Alkaline optimum (pH 8.0–10.5) of *B. subtilis* protease is ideally matched to the rising pH of kinema fermentation.

**Rationale:** Kinema contains nattokinase (fibrinolytic) and VK₂ (bone health) but is underutilized due to perishability, strong aroma, and variable composition. Converting kinema into a standardized, odour-reduced, bioactive peptide powder would expand its health application.
