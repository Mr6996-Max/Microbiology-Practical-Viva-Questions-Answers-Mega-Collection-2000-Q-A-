### PART A: Basic Principles & Background (Q1–Q12)

---

**Q1. Define wine and describe its basic composition.**

Wine is an alcoholic beverage produced by the complete or partial fermentation of fresh grape juice (must) or other fruit juices by yeast, primarily *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*. Its basic composition: (1) *Ethanol* — 8–15% v/v (11–12% typical table wine); primary fermentation product; determines body, warmth, and preservation. (2) *Water* — 80–85%; solvent for all other components. (3) *Sugars* — residual glucose and fructose (0–220 g/L depending on wine style; dry wine <4 g/L; sweet wine up to 220 g/L); sucrose in grape juice is hydrolyzed by yeast invertase before fermentation. (4) *Organic acids* — tartaric acid (2–4 g/L; unique to grapes, not metabolized by yeast), malic acid (0–4 g/L; partially degraded by malolactic fermentation), citric acid (0.1–0.5 g/L), acetic acid (0.2–0.8 g/L; volatile acidity indicator of spoilage if excessive), lactic acid (1–3 g/L after MLF), succinic acid (0.5–1.5 g/L; yeast metabolite). (5) *Glycerol* — 5–15 g/L; yeast by-product; contributes sweetness and viscosity/mouthfeel. (6) *Phenolic compounds* — anthocyanins (red wine color), flavonoids (tannins; bitterness and astringency), stilbenes (resveratrol — health-relevant). (7) *Volatile aroma compounds* — esters (isoamyl acetate — banana note), higher alcohols (fusel oils), terpenes (from grape variety), aldehydes, lactones. (8) *Minerals* — potassium (predominant), calcium, magnesium. (9) *SO₂ (sulfur dioxide)* — added as preservative and antioxidant; 20–150 mg/L total; free SO₂ must be ≤30–35 mg/L (EU legal limit). pH of wine: 3.0–3.8.

**Q2. What are the biochemical reactions that occur during grape juice fermentation to produce wine?**

Wine fermentation involves a sequence of biochemical reactions: (1) *Sucrose hydrolysis* — grape juice sucrose (if present) is hydrolyzed by yeast invertase (β-fructosidase) to glucose + fructose; most fermentable sugar in grape must is already present as free glucose and fructose in approximately equal proportions (fructose slightly predominates in ripe grapes). (2) *Glycolysis (EMP pathway)* — glucose and fructose → 2 pyruvate + 2 ATP + 2 NADH per hexose; the rate of glycolysis determines fermentation rate. (3) *Fermentation* — pyruvate → acetaldehyde (by PDC, TPP cofactor) → ethanol (by ADH, NADH oxidized to NAD⁺). (4) *Glycerol production* — under early fermentation conditions (osmotic stress from high sugar; redox balancing), a fraction of dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) is reduced to glycerol-3-phosphate by NADH-dependent glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GPD1/GPD2), then dephosphorylated to glycerol; glycerol production is 4–8% of ethanol production (by mass). (5) *Organic acid production* — succinic acid is produced from α-ketoglutarate via reductive TCA; acetic acid is produced from acetaldehyde by aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH); citric acid from TCA overflow. (6) *Ester formation* — alcohol acetyltransferases (Atf1, Atf2) catalyze: ethanol + acetyl-CoA → ethyl acetate + CoA; isoamyl alcohol + acetyl-CoA → isoamyl acetate (banana ester, key wine aroma compound). (7) *Higher alcohol (fusel oil) formation* — from amino acid catabolism via the Ehrlich pathway: amino acid → α-keto acid (transamination) → aldehyde (decarboxylation) → higher alcohol (reduction by ADH).

**Q3. What is the role of sulfur dioxide (SO₂) in wine making and what forms does it exist in?**

Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) is the most important preservative and antioxidant in winemaking, present in multiple forms: (1) *Molecular SO₂ (free SO₂)* — the antimicrobial and antioxidant form; exists as dissolved gas H₂SO₃ (sulfurous acid) at very low pH; at wine pH (3.2–3.8), the equilibrium between molecular SO₂, bisulfite (HSO₃⁻), and sulfite (SO₃²⁻) strongly favors bisulfite. At pH 3.2, approximately 8% of free SO₂ is molecular; at pH 3.8, approximately 3% molecular. The molecular SO₂ fraction is the active antimicrobial agent; effective levels require 0.5–0.8 mg/L molecular SO₂. (2) *Free SO₂* — total of molecular SO₂ + bisulfite in solution; not bound to wine components. (3) *Bound SO₂* — bisulfite forms addition compounds (bisulfite adducts) with carbonyl compounds: acetaldehyde + HSO₃⁻ → 1-hydroxyethanesulfonate (the major bound form; aldehyde-bisulfite adduct; this binding is reversible and keeps SO₂ unavailable for antimicrobial action but acts as an acetaldehyde reservoir). Also binds to glucose, pyruvate, α-ketoglutarate. (4) *Total SO₂* = free + bound. Roles: antimicrobial (inhibits wild yeasts, bacteria, *Brettanomyces*), antioxidant (reduces O₂ and protects phenolics from oxidative browning), anti-browning (inhibits polyphenol oxidase — PPO enzyme in grape). Added as potassium metabisulfite (K₂S₂O₅) — releases 2 SO₂ per molecule — or as SO₂ gas from pressurized cylinders.

**Q4. What is malolactic fermentation (MLF) and when is it desirable in winemaking?**

Malolactic fermentation (MLF) is the biological deacidification of wine through the enzymatic conversion of L-malic acid (sharp, harsh diprotic acid, pKa 3.46 and 5.10) to L-lactic acid (softer monocarboxylic acid, pKa 3.86) + CO₂, catalyzed by malolactic enzyme (malic enzyme; requires NAD⁺ and Mn²⁺) in lactic acid bacteria (LAB), primarily *Oenococcus oeni* (formerly *Leuconostoc oenos*), the dominant MLF organism, and secondary species including *Lactobacillus plantarum* and *Pediococcus damnosus*. Net reaction: **HOOCCH(OH)CH₂COOH → CH₃CH(OH)COOH + CO₂** (L-malic acid → L-lactic acid + CO₂). Effects on wine: (1) *pH increase* — conversion of diprotic malic to monocarboxylic lactic acid reduces titratable acidity by 1–3 g/L (expressed as tartaric acid equivalents), raising pH by 0.1–0.3 units. (2) *Flavor changes* — removes harsh green apple/tart character (malic acid); adds creamy, buttery notes from diacetyl (2,3-butanedione; a by-product of α-acetolactate decarboxylation in OAB at residual amounts). (3) *Microbial stability* — malic acid is a substrate for spoilage organisms; its removal increases wine stability. Desirability: strongly desirable in red wines (softens tannins and astringency); desirable in high-acid white wines (Chardonnay, cool-climate Riesling); undesirable in aromatic whites (Sauvignon Blanc, Riesling, Gewürztraminer) where sharp acidity is a quality attribute and fresh fruit aromas would be masked.

**Q5. Describe the microbial succession during spontaneous wine fermentation.**

Spontaneous (uninoculated) wine fermentation from freshly crushed grape must proceeds through a predictable microbial succession driven by the changing environment (increasing ethanol, decreasing sugar, falling pH): (1) *Initial phase (0–2 days; ethanol <3% v/v)* — dominated by apiculate yeasts: *Hanseniaspora uvarum* (Kloeckera apiculata, anamorph) and *H. guilliermondii*; these are abundant on grape skin surfaces (10⁴–10⁶ CFU/mL) and initiate fermentation rapidly; they produce significant acetic acid (volatile acidity), ethyl acetate, and other esters at this stage. Also present: *Metschnikowia pulcherrima*, *Starmerella bacillaris*, *Candida* species. (2) *Middle phase (2–5 days; 3–7% ethanol)* — *Hanseniaspora* numbers decline as ethanol increases past their tolerance (~5–6% v/v); *Torulaspora delbrueckii* and *Lachancea thermotolerans* become prominent; contribute positive aroma compounds and lactic acid respectively. (3) *Dominant phase (5–10 days; 7–15% ethanol)* — *S. cerevisiae* outcompetes all other species due to its superior ethanol tolerance (12–15% v/v) and ability to produce killer toxins (K1, K2 type) that kill sensitive strains. *S. cerevisiae* completes sugar fermentation and dominates the final population (10⁶–10⁸ CFU/mL). (4) *Post-fermentation* — lactic acid bacteria (*Oenococcus oeni*) emerge after yeast die-off to conduct MLF (if SO₂ is low enough and pH is not too low). This succession is exploited by inoculated fermentation (adding commercial *S. cerevisiae* starter culture) to suppress unwanted wild yeasts and ensure reproducible fermentation.

**Q6. What are tannins in wine and why are they important?**

Tannins are polyphenolic compounds with the ability to precipitate proteins (including salivary proteins, causing the sensation of astringency when they bind to mouth proteins). In wine, tannins originate from: (1) *Grape tannins (condensed tannins / proanthocyanidins)* — polymers of flavan-3-ol monomers (catechin, epicatechin, epicatechin gallate) located predominantly in grape seeds (seed tannins — harsh, astringent), skins (skin tannins — softer, contribute to color stability by co-pigmenting with anthocyanins), and grape stems (stem tannins — herbaceous, green). (2) *Oak tannins (hydrolysable tannins — gallotannins and ellagitannins)* — extracted from oak barrels or oak chips/staves during maturation; ellagitannins (castalagin, vescalagin) from Quercus robur and Q. petraea; add complexity, vanilla and coconut notes (from oak lactones), and improve wine longevity through controlled oxidation. (3) *Tannin functions in wine:* preserve wine through antioxidant activity and antimicrobial properties; contribute structure, body, and ageability (tannins polymerize over time → softer, silkier texture in aged red wine); stabilize color (anthocyanin-tannin condensation products are more stable than free anthocyanins); affect fining (bentonite, egg albumin, casein proteins precipitate excess tannins). Tannin measurement: Folin-Ciocalteu method (total polyphenols as gallic acid equivalents); BSA precipitation method; HPLC for individual tannin classes.

**Q7. What is the role of potassium metabisulfite (K₂S₂O₅) in laboratory wine preparation and how is it used?**

Potassium metabisulfite (KMS; K₂S₂O₅; MW = 222.3 g/mol) is the most common form of SO₂ used in amateur and laboratory winemaking. When dissolved in aqueous solution (must or wine): K₂S₂O₅ + H₂O → 2 KHSO₃ (potassium bisulfite) → releases SO₂ in acidic conditions. Each gram of K₂S₂O₅ releases approximately 0.57 g SO₂ (theoretical: 2 × 64.06/222.3 = 0.576 g SO₂ per g KMS). Uses in laboratory wine preparation: (1) *Must treatment before fermentation* — 50–75 mg SO₂ per liter of must (prepared from KMS; add 88–130 mg KMS per liter) is added to crushed grape must 12–24 hours before inoculation with *S. cerevisiae* starter; this selectively inhibits wild yeasts and bacteria (most sensitive at pH <3.5 with molecular SO₂ > 0.5 mg/L) while allowing the more SO₂-tolerant *S. cerevisiae* starter to dominate; also inhibits polyphenol oxidase (PPO) to prevent must browning. (2) *Equipment sterilization* — 1–2% KMS solution is used to sanitize fermentation vessels, airlocks, siphon tubing, and bottles before use (swirl or spray to coat surfaces; drain; no rinsing needed at low concentration — residual SO₂ is wine-safe at these concentrations). (3) *Post-fermentation preservation* — after fermentation, free SO₂ is replenished (depleted during fermentation by binding to acetaldehyde) by adding 25–50 mg/L SO₂ as KMS at each racking/bottling step.

**Q8. What is the difference between dry wine, off-dry, semi-sweet, and sweet wine in terms of residual sugar?**

Wine sweetness categories are defined by residual sugar (RS) concentration — the amount of unfermented sugar remaining after fermentation: (1) *Bone dry* — RS < 1 g/L; all fermentable sugar consumed; common in premium still table wines. (2) *Dry* — RS < 4 g/L (EU definition: <4 g/L; OIV: ≤4 g/L if total acidity is within 2 g/L of RS, or ≤9 g/L); the residual sweetness is below the human perception threshold (~4 g/L), so dry wines taste non-sweet. Most red wines and many white wines. (3) *Medium dry / Off-dry* — RS 4–12 g/L; slight sweetness detectable; e.g., most commercial German Riesling Kabinett, Pinot Grigio. (4) *Medium sweet / Demi-sec* — RS 12–45 g/L; noticeable sweetness; e.g., Mosel Spätlese, Vouvray demi-sec. (5) *Sweet* — RS 45–120 g/L; dessert wines; e.g., Sauternes, late harvest wines, Auslese. (6) *Very sweet (liquoreux)* — RS > 120 g/L; e.g., Trockenbeerenauslese (TBA), ice wine (Eiswein), Pedro Ximénez sherry. Residual sugar is controlled by: stopping fermentation early (SO₂ addition, cooling, centrifugation to remove yeast); using a low-alcohol-tolerance yeast that ceases fermentation before all sugar is consumed; adding sterile grape juice (Süssreserve) to finished dry wine; or starting with extremely high sugar concentrations (botrytized or dried grapes) that exceed yeast osmotolerance.

**Q9. What is pectin and why is pectinase enzyme important in winemaking?**

Pectin is a complex polysaccharide (galacturonic acid polymer with methylester groups and neutral sugar side chains) that forms part of the primary cell wall and middle lamella of plant cells, holding cells together. In grapes, pectin makes the must viscous and hazy. Pectinase (pectinolytic enzyme complex) — comprising polygalacturonase (PG), pectin lyase (PL), pectin methylesterase (PME), and pectin methylgalacturonase — depolymerizes pectin by cleaving glycosidic bonds and demethylating ester groups. Importance in winemaking: (1) *Juice clarification* — breaking down pectin reduces must viscosity and allows rapid sedimentation of grape solids (pulp, skin fragments); results in cleaner juice that ferments more cleanly and produces wines with less off-flavors from skin contact. (2) *Juice yield improvement* — pectinase degrades cell walls, releasing more juice from grape pulp (yield improvement of 10–20% in commercial operations). (3) *Color extraction* — for red wines, pectinase treatment of crushed red grapes facilitates release of anthocyanins from skin vacuoles into the must (the pectin matrix in the skin traps color). (4) *Filterability* — pectin forms gel-like structures that clog filters; pectinase treatment dramatically improves wine filterability post-fermentation. Commercial pectinase preparations (e.g., Rapidase, Vinozym, Pectinex) from *Aspergillus niger* are added at 2–5 g/100 kg of grapes.

**Q10. What is the role of yeast nutrients and vitamins in wine fermentation and what happens if they are deficient?**

Yeast require macro-nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, sulfur) and micro-nutrients (vitamins, minerals) for healthy fermentation: (1) *Yeast assimilable nitrogen (YAN)* — the sum of free amino acids (FAN) and ammonium ion (NH₄⁺) available for yeast growth and enzyme synthesis; optimal YAN for complete fermentation: 200–350 mg N/L. YAN deficiency (<150 mg N/L) causes: slow or stuck fermentation; production of H₂S (hydrogen sulfide — rotten egg smell) from sulfur amino acid metabolism as yeast scavenge sulfur; production of heavy sulfurous off-flavors. Correction: add diammonium phosphate (DAP; 200–500 mg/L; provides NH₄⁺ and phosphate) or commercial yeast nutrient blends (FermaidK, GoFerm — contain YAN + vitamins + minerals). (2) *Thiamine (Vitamin B₁)* — essential cofactor for pyruvate decarboxylase (PDC; TPP = thiamine pyrophosphate); deficiency impairs PDC activity, slowing ethanol production and favoring pyruvate accumulation → more SO₂ binding (pyruvate binds SO₂). Add thiamine (0.5–1 mg/L). (3) *Biotin* — required for fatty acid synthesis (acetyl-CoA carboxylase); deficiency reduces membrane lipid synthesis → poor ethanol tolerance. (4) *Pantothenic acid* — required for CoA synthesis. (5) *Zinc* — ADH cofactor; deficiency reduces fermentation rate. (6) *Sterols and unsaturated fatty acids (UFAs)* — required for membrane integrity under anaerobic conditions (yeast cannot synthesize sterols without O₂); limited O₂ addition or rehydration of dried yeast in the presence of sterols (GoFerm Protect) addresses this.

**Q11. What is Brix (°Brix) and how is it used in winemaking?**

Brix (°Brix) is a unit of measurement representing the percentage of dissolved soluble solids (predominantly sugars) in a liquid solution, measured by refractometry or hydrometry at 20°C. One degree Brix = 1 gram of sucrose per 100 grams of solution (i.e., 1% w/w sucrose equivalent). In winemaking: (1) *Grape ripeness assessment* — °Brix is measured at harvest to estimate sugar content and predict potential alcohol; ripe wine grapes are typically harvested at 20–25°Brix; premium/late-harvest grapes at 26–35°Brix. *Rule of thumb:* Potential alcohol (% v/v) ≈ Brix × 0.55–0.59 (depending on conversion efficiency and yeast strain; more precisely calculated using: potential alcohol = Brix × 0.576 in European practice). (2) *Fermentation monitoring* — Brix drops as sugar is consumed; monitoring Brix decline (using a hydrometer or refractometer daily) tracks fermentation progress; dry wine is complete when Brix reaches −1 to −2 (below zero because ethanol has lower density than water/sucrose, depressing the refractometer or hydrometer reading). (3) *Specific gravity (SG) correlation* — SG (or density) relates to Brix by approximate formula: °Brix ≈ (SG − 1) × 258 for dilute sucrose solutions; more precise conversion tables are used in practice. A refractometer reads °Brix directly from refractive index; a hydrometer (saccharometer) reads SG from which °Brix is derived.

**Q12. What are the major off-flavors and spoilage conditions encountered in wine fermentation and their causes?**

Common wine off-flavors and their biochemical causes: (1) *H₂S (hydrogen sulfide) — "rotten egg"* — produced by yeast from elemental sulfur (residual from vineyard fungicide sprays), from sulfate reduction (sulfate → sulfite → sulfide via sulfite reductase), or from degradation of sulfur amino acids (cysteine, methionine) under nitrogen deficiency. Remedy: adequate YAN; SO₂-reduced spraying; bentonite fining; Cu²⁺ treatment (CuSO₄ precipitates Cu₂S). (2) *Acetic acid (volatile acidity; VA) — "vinegar"* — produced in excess by *Acetobacter* and *Gluconobacter* (acetic acid bacteria, AAB) when wine is exposed to oxygen; also by *Hanseniaspora* and *Brettanomyces*. Legal limit: <1.2 g/L (red wine). (3) *Ethyl acetate — "nail polish remover"* — produced from ethanol + acetate by yeast (Atf1, Atf2 enzymes) and AAB; associated with stuck fermentation or spoilage. (4) *4-Ethylphenol and 4-ethylguaiacol ("barnyard", "horse sweat", "band-aid")* — produced by *Brettanomyces bruxellensis* from hydroxycinnamic acid precursors in red wine post-fermentation; the most feared spoilage organism in premium red wine production. (5) *Geraniol degradation ("mousy" taint)* — rare; from *Dekkera* or LAB. (6) *Lactic acid bacteria spoilage ("ropiness", "mousiness", excess diacetyl)* — from uncontrolled *Lactobacillus*, *Pediococcus* growth; excessive diacetyl (>0.4 mg/L is offensive). (7) *Oxidative browning* — from phenolic oxidation; prevented by SO₂ and oxygen exclusion.

**Q13. What raw materials are used for laboratory wine preparation and how are they selected?**

Laboratory wine can be prepared from: (1) *Fresh grapes* — most authentic; ripe table or wine grapes at 20–24°Brix; crush by hand or with a manual wine press; ferment as white must (juice only, after pressing skins) or red must (skin-contact fermentation). (2) *Grape juice (commercial 100% juice)* — convenient for laboratory use; no sulfite or preservatives; adjust to desired Brix with added glucose/sucrose if below 20°Brix; adjust pH to 3.0–3.5 with tartaric acid if too high. (3) *Other fruit juices* — apple juice (cider), mango juice, pineapple juice, strawberry juice; country wine preparation; adjust sugar to 20–22°Brix using sucrose (chaptalisation). (4) *Reconstituted grape juice concentrate* — commercially available concentrated grape juice diluted to 20°Brix; consistent composition; suitable for reproducible laboratory experiments. Selection criteria: (a) Sugar content (20–24°Brix for table wine; measure with refractometer); (b) pH (3.0–3.5; adjust if necessary); (c) Absence of chemical preservatives (sorbate, benzoate — these inhibit fermentation; check label); (d) Natural yeast population (spontaneous fermentation is possible from fresh grapes; killed by KMS treatment if using inoculated fermentation); (e) Freedom from mold contamination (moldy grapes produce off-flavors and excess acetic acid).

---

**Q14. Describe the complete laboratory protocol for white wine preparation from grape juice.**

Step-by-step white wine preparation: (1) *Juice preparation* — measure 500 mL–1 L commercial grape juice or freshly pressed white grape juice; check Brix (refractometer); if <20°Brix, add sucrose to adjust [sucrose required (g) = volume (L) × (desired Brix − actual Brix) × 10]. Measure pH; adjust to pH 3.2–3.4 with tartaric acid (2–5 g/L) if too high. (2) *SO₂ treatment* — add KMS at 50 mg SO₂ per liter [KMS amount (g/L) = 50 mg SO₂/L × (1 g KMS / 570 mg SO₂) = 0.088 g KMS/L]; stir; cover; allow 12–24 hours for wild yeast/bacteria to be inhibited. (3) *Yeast inoculation* — rehydrate active dry wine yeast (*S. cerevisiae* wine strain e.g., EC1118, LALVIN 71B, or equivalent) in warm water (37–40°C, 10× yeast weight in water, 15–20 minutes); inoculate into must at 0.2–0.3 g/L rate; stir gently. (4) *Nutrient addition* — add DAP (diammonium phosphate; 200 mg/L) and thiamine (0.5 mg/L) to ensure adequate YAN and vitamin supply. (5) *Fermentation vessel* — transfer to a glass fermentation vessel (5 L demijohn or 1 L Erlenmeyer) fitted with a fermentation lock (water trap); fill to 80% capacity. (6) *Fermentation* — incubate at 18–22°C (white wine); monitor Brix daily; stir gently every 24 hours. Fermentation complete when Brix stable at −1 to −2 for 3 consecutive days (7–14 days total). (7) *Racking* — siphon wine off the lees (dead yeast sediment) into a clean vessel; add 25 mg/L SO₂ (KMS); seal. (8) *Clarification* — allow to settle at 4–10°C; optional fining with bentonite (1 g/L) to remove proteins; optional filtration through 1 μm filter pad. (9) *Bottling* — bottle under inert gas (CO₂ or N₂); add SO₂ to maintain 30 mg/L free SO₂; cork.

**Q15. What is the difference between white wine and red wine production procedures?**

The fundamental difference is skin contact timing and temperature: *White wine:* Grapes are crushed and immediately pressed to separate juice from skins, seeds, and stems before fermentation (or with minimal skin contact, 1–4 hours, for "skin contact" white wines). Fermentation of juice only at low temperature (12–18°C for aromatic whites; 18–22°C for richer styles). Color comes only from trace phenolics in pulp; pale straw to golden color. *Red wine:* After crushing and de-stemming (optional), skins, seeds, and pulp (pomace) remain in contact with juice throughout fermentation (maceration). Color pigments (anthocyanins) and tannins from skins and seeds are extracted into the fermenting must by the action of alcohol (which disrupts vacuolar membranes releasing pigment) and the heat of fermentation. Fermentation temperature: 25–32°C for red wine (higher temperature extracts more color and tannin). Cap management: the CO₂ produced pushes skins to the surface forming a solid cap (chapeau); cap is managed by: punch-down (pigeage — punching the cap back into the liquid by hand or punch tool, 2–3 times daily); pump-over (remontage — pumping wine from the bottom of the tank over the cap); rotor tank (automated continuous mixing). Maceration duration: 7–21 days depending on desired extraction. After fermentation, red wine is pressed (vin de presse) to recover wine from the pomace; press wine contains more tannins and color.

**Q16. How is Brix/sugar concentration measured and how is it used to monitor fermentation progress?**

Brix/sugar monitoring methods: (1) *Refractometer* — measures refractive index of the must/wine; converts to °Brix directly. Advantages: requires only 1–2 drops of sample; immediate result; temperature-compensated models adjust for samples not at 20°C. Limitation: ethanol has a refractive index different from sucrose and interferes with readings during active fermentation; refractometer readings become inaccurate after >3% ethanol accumulates; must use a correction factor or switch to hydrometer. (2) *Hydrometer (saccharometer)* — measures density/specific gravity of must/wine; float hydrometer in cylinder of must; read the graduation at the meniscus. The scale may be directly in °Brix, or in SG from which °Brix is calculated. Advantages: no calibration needed (if properly maintained); measures SG accurately throughout fermentation even at high ethanol. Limitation: requires 100–250 mL of sample; needs temperature correction if not at 20°C (add 0.001 SG per 3°C above calibration temperature). (3) *HPLC or enzymatic glucose/fructose assay* — most accurate for quantifying individual sugars; used in research and commercial QC. Fermentation monitoring protocol: measure Brix at inoculation (Day 0); measure every 24 hours at a fixed time; plot Brix vs. time; fermentation is complete when Brix is stable (±0.1°Brix) at −1 to −2 for 3 days and CO₂ evolution from fermentation lock ceases.

**Q17. How is pH measured and adjusted in must/wine preparation?**

pH measurement: use a calibrated glass electrode pH meter; calibrate with pH 4.0 and 7.0 (or pH 3.0 and 7.0 for more accurate wine measurements at low pH) buffer solutions before each session; rinse with distilled water between measurements; immerse electrode in must/wine sample (minimum 50 mL); allow reading to stabilize (30–60 seconds). For highly colored red musts, wipe electrode carefully as anthocyanins can coat the glass bulb. *pH adjustment — acidification:* Add tartaric acid (the most appropriate acidulant for grape wine, as it is the natural dominant acid and not metabolized by yeast or LAB). Dissolve tartaric acid in small volume of must; add incrementally; re-measure pH; target pH 3.2–3.5. For every g/L tartaric acid added, pH decreases by approximately 0.1–0.2 pH units (depending on buffering capacity). *pH adjustment — deacidification:* Add potassium bicarbonate (KHCO₃; 1 g/L reduces titratable acidity by approximately 1 g/L as tartaric acid; limited by precipitation of potassium bitartrate) or calcium carbonate (CaCO₃; precipitates as CaCO₃ after reacting with malic and tartaric acids — "double salt deacidification" using Acidex). In laboratory wine preparation, acidification is more commonly needed (from sweet juice with naturally low acidity) than deacidification.

**Q18. What is the purpose of racking in winemaking and when is it performed?**

Racking (soutirer in French) is the process of carefully siphoning or pumping wine from one vessel to another, leaving behind the sediment (lees). Purpose: (1) *Separation of wine from gross lees* — gross lees (first racking, 1–2 weeks after fermentation end) consist of dead yeast cells (autolysing), grape solids (skin fragments, pulp cells, tartrate crystals), and various precipitated proteins and polysaccharides. Prolonged contact with gross lees causes off-flavors: H₂S from yeast autolysis; reductive off-notes; excessive fatty acid release. (2) *Yeast autolysis for fine lees contact* — in some wine styles (Muscadet sur lie, premium Chardonnay, Champagne), wine is deliberately left on fine lees (dead yeast cells) for 1–36 months to extract flavor-active compounds from autolysing yeast: mannoproteins (from yeast cell wall; improve mouthfeel, stability, and CO₂ retention in sparkling wine); fatty acids (hexanoic, octanoic — fatty/creamy notes); amino acids; reducing glutathione (antioxidant). (3) *Aeration control* — each racking introduces some oxygen; 2–3 mg/L O₂ per racking; controlled micro-oxygenation at each racking promotes tannin polymerization in red wine (softening) and depletes reductive off-notes. (4) *Free SO₂ maintenance* — each racking consumes free SO₂ (it oxidizes); topping up with KMS after each racking maintains antimicrobial protection. Timing of first racking: white wine — 1–2 weeks post-fermentation; red wine — immediately after pressing (pomace separation).

**Q19. What is bentonite fining and what is its role in wine stabilization?**

Bentonite is a montmorillonite clay (aluminum silicate mineral) with a high negative charge density and enormous surface area (600–800 m²/g) that adsorbs positively charged proteins from wine. Role in wine stabilization: (1) *Protein stabilization* — wine contains grape-derived proteins (primarily thaumatin-like proteins and chitinases from the pathogenesis-related PR protein family) that are heat-unstable and precipitate when wine is warmed (as in a car, restaurant, or during shipping); this creates visible proteinaceous haze or sediment ("protein haze"). Bentonite adsorbs and precipitates these proteins, rendering the wine heat-stable. Bench test to check: heat a wine sample at 80°C for 30 minutes; if haze develops, protein stabilization is needed. (2) *Removal of color compounds* — bentonite adsorbs some anthocyanins and tannins in red wine, which may reduce color; generally avoided in red wine production for this reason; primarily used in white and rosé wine. (3) *Dosage* — bench trial required; prepare dilutions of bentonite slurry (5% w/v; hydrate bentonite in hot water 12–24 hours before use to form a gel); add 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0 g/L to wine samples; heat-test after 24 hours at 80°C; use lowest dose achieving heat stability. Overdosing bentonite: strips wine of desirable aroma compounds, proteins contributing to mouthfeel; generates large volumes of bentonite lees (disposal problem; 1 L lees per 100 L wine treated). Alternative: flash pasteurization, chitosan treatment, Zysil (silica sol + gelatin system).

**Q20. What is chaptalisation and when is it permitted or practiced?**

Chaptalisation (named after French chemist Jean-Antoine Chaptal who formalized the practice in the early 19th century) is the addition of sucrose (or concentrated grape juice, rectified concentrated grape must — RCGM) to grape must before or during fermentation to increase the potential alcohol content of the final wine. It is practiced when grapes are harvested at insufficient sugar ripeness due to cool climates or adverse weather. Calculation: to increase potential alcohol by 1% v/v, add approximately 17 g sucrose per liter of must (since theoretical yield = 0.511 g ethanol per g glucose × density adjustment). Practical formula: **sucrose to add (g/L) = (desired °Brix – actual °Brix) × 18.7** (for concentrating to Brix; alternatively: each 10 g/L sucrose added increases potential alcohol by approximately 0.6% v/v). Regulatory status: chaptalisation is legal and common in cool-climate wine regions (Burgundy, Bordeaux, Germany's table wine category, most of France for AC wines up to +2% v/v alcohol enrichment); strictly prohibited in warm/Mediterranean climates (South of France AOC wines, Italy DOC, Spain DO, California, Australia, South Africa) where grapes naturally reach sufficient ripeness. The addition of water (dilution) is universally prohibited in quality wine production. In laboratory wine preparations, sucrose is routinely added to non-grape fruit juices (apple, pear, tropical fruits) to reach the target fermentable sugar concentration.

**Q21. How is free SO₂ measured in wine (Ripper method)?**

The Ripper method is the standard direct titration for measuring free SO₂ in wine: (1) Pipette 50 mL of wine into a 250 mL conical flask. (2) Add 5 mL of 25% (v/v) H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid; acidification is critical to liberate molecular SO₂ from bisulfite form and prevent iodine from oxidizing phenolics rather than SO₂ at neutral pH). (3) Add 1 mL of starch indicator solution (1% starch w/v in hot water, cooled; forms the blue starch-iodine complex at the endpoint). (4) Titrate with standardized iodine solution (I₂ in KI; 0.02 N or 0.01 N; prepared from 0.1 N stock and standardized against Na₂S₂O₃) from a burette. Iodine oxidizes SO₂: SO₂ + I₂ + 2H₂O → H₂SO₄ + 2HI. The endpoint is the first permanent blue color (starch-iodine complex forms when excess iodine is present after all SO₂ has been oxidized). Record volume of iodine used (V, mL). Calculation: **Free SO₂ (mg/L) = V (mL) × N (normality of I₂) × 32,000 / volume of wine (mL)** where 32 = MW of SO₂/2 (because SO₂ is 2-electron equivalent). *Example:* 2.5 mL of 0.02 N I₂ used for 50 mL wine: Free SO₂ = 2.5 × 0.02 × 32,000 / 50 = **32 mg/L**. Limitations: colored red wines interfere with endpoint detection (use the "color disappearance" endpoint for reds — more difficult; or use automated aspiration-acidification SO₂ analyzer).

**Q22. Describe the process of clarification and stabilization of wine before bottling.**

Clarification and stabilization are sequential processes ensuring wine is visually clear and chemically/biologically stable: *Clarification:* (1) *Natural settling* — store wine at 4–10°C after fermentation; yeast cells, grape solids, and tartrate crystals sediment slowly over weeks; rack off sediment. (2) *Fining* — add clarifying agents that flocculate and precipitate colloidal particles: bentonite (proteins, Section Q19); gelatin (anionic tannins — gelatin has positive charge at wine pH; creates gelatin-tannin flocs that settle; used in red wines to reduce harsh tannins); egg white albumin (similar to gelatin; traditional Bordeaux method; 1–5 egg whites per barrel = 2–5 g/L albumin); isinglass (fish swim bladder collagen; used in sparkling wine and delicate whites; gentle clarifier). (3) *Filtration* — pass wine through diatomaceous earth (DE) filter (removes gross particles), then membrane filter (0.45–0.65 μm, for microbial stabilization). *Stabilization:* (1) *Cold stabilization* — chill wine to −4°C to −6°C for 1–3 weeks; potassium bitartrate (cream of tartar) precipitates and is removed; prevents tartrate crystal formation in bottle after sale. (2) *Protein stabilization* — bentonite fining (see Q19). (3) *Microbial stabilization* — filtration through 0.45 μm membrane removes all yeast; 0.2 μm membrane removes bacteria; sulfur dioxide maintenance (free SO₂ ≥ 25 mg/L at bottling). (4) *Flash pasteurization* — brief heat treatment (72°C, 15–30 seconds in plate heat exchanger) kills all microorganisms; used for cheap/bag-in-box wines; not for premium wines (kills aroma).

---

**Q23. What is tartrate stabilization and why is it performed?**

Tartrate stabilization addresses the precipitation of potassium bitartrate (KHT; cream of tartar; KHC₄H₄O₆) and calcium tartrate crystals in bottled wine. These crystals form when wine is chilled (in refrigerator, during shipping in cold weather) because their solubility decreases dramatically at low temperature. While chemically harmless and natural, consumers perceive crystal sediment as quality defect or glass fragments (particularly white wine). Tartrate stabilization methods: (1) *Cold stabilization (classical method)* — chill wine to just above its freezing point (approximately −4°C for 11–12% ethanol wine; freezing point = −[ethanol % × 0.4 + 0.0]) for 1–3 weeks; KHT crystallizes and is removed by filtration or settling and racking. Requires refrigeration energy; generates KHT for disposal (can be purified to cream of tartar for food use). (2) *Electrodialysis* — ion-selective membranes selectively remove potassium and tartrate ions without changing wine temperature or composition significantly; widely adopted in Europe as "green" alternative. (3) *Contact process* — add fine KHT seed crystals to cold wine; the seed crystals accelerate KHT crystallization dramatically, reducing stabilization time from weeks to hours; combine with cold stabilization. (4) *CMC (carboxymethylcellulose)* — food-grade CMC added 2 g/L inhibits KHT crystal nucleation and growth; approved for white/rosé wines in EU (Regulation EC 606/2009); most economical modern method; not approved for red wines.

**Q24. What analytical tests are performed on finished wine for quality control in a laboratory setting?**

Standard analytical measurements on finished wine: (1) *Alcohol content* — by ebulliometry (measures boiling point elevation due to alcohol, reads directly in % v/v; portable instrument), or by distillation + density measurement of distillate, or by HPLC/GC. (2) *Total acidity (titratable acidity)* — titrate 10 mL wine with 0.1 N NaOH using phenolphthalein indicator; result in g/L as tartaric acid equivalents (multiply mL NaOH × 0.1 × 7.5 for g/L tartaric acid in 10 mL sample). (3) *Volatile acidity (VA)* — acetic acid content by steam distillation (Cash still) + titration; standard ≤0.8 g/L (white), ≤1.2 g/L (red). (4) *Free and total SO₂* — Ripper method (Q21); total SO₂ measured after alkaline hydrolysis releases bound SO₂. (5) *Residual sugar* — Clinitest (semi-quantitative) or HPLC glucose + fructose quantification. (6) *pH* — calibrated pH meter. (7) *Color* — spectrophotometry at 420 nm (yellow/browning), 520 nm (red/anthocyanin), 620 nm (blue/violet); color intensity = A₄₂₀ + A₅₂₀ + A₆₂₀; hue = A₄₂₀/A₅₂₀. (8) *Total polyphenol index (TPI)* — A₂₈₀ nm × dilution factor; measures UV-absorbing polyphenols. (9) *Microbial quality* — plate count on YEPD (yeast and mold) and MRS agar (LAB); <1 CFU/mL is target in finished bottled wine. (10) *Sensory evaluation* — appearance, aroma, taste, finish scored by trained tasters using 20-point or 100-point scale.

**Q25. What is the role of yeast strain selection in determining wine quality and characteristics?**

Yeast strain selection profoundly influences wine aroma, flavor, and fermentation performance because different *S. cerevisiae* strains express different levels of key enzymes: (1) *Ester production* — strains with high alcohol acetyltransferase (ATF1) activity produce high isoamyl acetate (banana) and ethyl acetate (fruity/solvent); strains expressing high ATF2 produce more ethyl hexanoate (apple/anise); selected for fruity young wines (Beaujolais style). Strains with low ATF activity (e.g., EC1118) produce cleaner, less ester-heavy wines. (2) *Glycerol production* — high-glycerol strains (overexpressing GPD1 or GPD2) give fuller mouthfeel, sweetness perception; used for red wine body enhancement. (3) *Beta-glucosidase activity* — releases aromatic terpenes (linalool, geraniol) from non-volatile glycoside precursors in the grape; strains with high β-glucosidase enhance terpene aroma (Muscat-type varieties). (4) *Malic acid degradation* — some *S. cerevisiae* strains (e.g., LALVIN 71B) have elevated malic enzyme and partially deacidify the wine biologically during alcoholic fermentation (20–30% malic acid reduction), reducing the need for MLF. (5) *Fermentation kinetics* — some strains ferment faster (complete in 5–7 days); others slower (10–14 days); some are better at completing stuck fermentations. (6) *Killer toxin* — K2-type killer strains (producing K2 toxin that kills sensitive wild yeasts) are preferred to dominate in inoculated fermentations; EC1118 and many commercial strains are killer.

**Q26. What is the stuck fermentation rescue procedure in laboratory wine production?**

When fermentation stops prematurely (Brix still high, CO₂ evolution ceased), a rescue protocol is followed: (1) *Diagnose the cause* — measure: Brix (confirm sugar remaining), pH (too low?), ethanol (hydrometer on distillate or HPLC), temperature (too cool?), free SO₂ (too high?). Examine microscopically for yeast count (haemocytometer) and viability (methylene blue). (2) *Correct underlying cause first* — if cold: warm to 25–28°C and stir; if SO₂ too high: wait or add ascorbic acid (reacts with SO₂); if nitrogen deficient: add DAP (200–400 mg/L); if pH too low (<2.8): add KHCO₃ to raise to 3.2. (3) *Rehydrate fresh yeast* — use an alcohol-tolerant strain (EC1118 Prise de Mousse; tolerates 18–20% v/v); rehydrate in warm water (40°C) with GoFerm (rehydration nutrient); after 15–20 minutes, acclimatize by adding small amounts of stuck wine to the yeast slurry gradually (10% of stuck wine every 30 minutes for 2 hours) to prevent osmotic/ethanol shock when adding the yeast to the wine. (4) *Inoculate* — add acclimatized yeast to the stuck wine; ensure temperature is 25–28°C; add fresh DAP (500 mg/L) as nutrient. (5) *Monitor* — check Brix every 24 hours; bubbling should resume within 12–24 hours. (6) *If still stuck* — consider Fermaid O (organic nitrogen source, better tolerated in high-ethanol environment than DAP) addition; or accept residual sugar and produce a semi-sweet wine with high SO₂ to stabilize.

**Q27. How is ethanol content determined in finished wine by distillation method?**

Distillation method (the reference method): (1) Accurately measure 100 mL of degassed wine (degas by stirring for 5 minutes or sonicating for 30 seconds to remove CO₂) into a 500 mL round-bottom distillation flask. (2) Add 50 mL of distilled water to assist distillation (prevents bumping and ensures complete ethanol recovery). (3) Distill into a 100 mL volumetric flask submerged in ice water (cold trap condenser to prevent ethanol evaporation). Collect exactly 100 mL of distillate (which contains all ethanol from the original wine plus water). (4) Bring distillate to 20°C (temperature must be exactly 20°C for density measurement accuracy). (5) Measure the density of the distillate using a calibrated pycnometer (weigh empty pycnometer, fill with distillate, weigh full; density = (mass full − mass empty) / volume of pycnometer) or a digital density meter (Anton Paar DMA 35 or 4500). (6) Convert density to % v/v ethanol using International Oenological Codex tables (OIV official tables of density/ethanol at 20°C) or the formula: **% v/v ethanol ≈ (0.79600 − density distillate) / (0.79600 − 0.99820) × 100** (approximate; OIV tables give precise values). This method is the legal reference method for alcohol in wine; precision ±0.1% v/v. Alternatives: ebulliometer (quick, ±0.2% v/v), HPLC, GC, and NIR spectroscopy for routine QC.

**Q28. What safety and hygiene considerations apply to laboratory wine preparation?**

Key safety and hygiene requirements: (1) *Sanitation* — all equipment (fermentation vessels, airlocks, siphon tubing, hydrometer cylinders, funnels) must be sanitized before use with 2% KMS solution or citric acid-based sanitizer; rinse with boiled then cooled water if KMS concentration is high; or use Star San (phosphoric acid-based no-rinse sanitizer; safe at 1 oz/5 gal). (2) *Chemical safety* — potassium metabisulfite releases SO₂ gas when dissolved; work in a well-ventilated area or fume cupboard; avoid inhaling SO₂ (OSHA PEL: 5 ppm; ceiling: 15 ppm); persons with asthma or sulfite sensitivity should avoid direct exposure. Sulfuric acid used in Ripper method is corrosive; use appropriate PPE. (3) *Biological safety* — wine fermentation involves BSL-1 organisms (*S. cerevisiae*); minimal containment required; standard microbiological lab practices. (4) *Alcohol safety* — ethanol is flammable; avoid open flames near fermentation vessels or ethanol concentrates; store wine away from heat sources. (5) *Allergen awareness* — some fining agents (egg albumin, casein, isinglass) are common food allergens; must be declared on wine labels in many jurisdictions; use alternative fining agents (bentonite, plant proteins) where allergen-free wine is needed. (6) *Waste disposal* — wine lees, fining agent precipitates, and excess wine should be disposed of via laboratory drain (with dilution); alcoholic waste at high concentration requires appropriate disposal. (7) *Labeling* — all fermentation vessels must be clearly labeled with content, date, and student/researcher name.

**Q29. Calculate the amount of sucrose needed to adjust 5 liters of apple juice from 12°Brix to 22°Brix for wine production.**

Target: increase from 12°Brix to 22°Brix in 5 L of apple juice.

°Brix represents grams of sucrose per 100 g of solution (approximately g per 100 mL for dilute solutions).

Sucrose to add (g/L) = (target °Brix − initial °Brix) × 10 g/L per °Brix = (22 − 12) × 10 = **100 g/L**

For 5 L: Sucrose needed = 100 g/L × 5 L = **500 g sucrose**

*Note on volume correction:* Adding 500 g sucrose increases the total volume slightly (sucrose contributes volume); 500 g sucrose in ~5 L gives approximately 5.3 L final volume. For precise Brix adjustment, an iterative calculation accounting for volume change is used:
Corrected sucrose = (target − initial) × (volume × density / 100) / (1 − target/100)
For laboratory purposes, the simplified formula above is acceptable with the note that final volume will be slightly higher than 5 L.

*Potential alcohol* after fermentation (theoretical): 22°Brix × 0.576 = **12.7% v/v** potential alcohol.

**Q30. Calculate the free SO₂ in a wine sample from a Ripper titration: 3.2 mL of 0.02 N iodine solution was used to titrate 50 mL of wine sample.**

**Free SO₂ (mg/L) = V_iodine (mL) × N_iodine × 32,000 / V_wine (mL)**

Free SO₂ = 3.2 × 0.02 × 32,000 / 50
= 3.2 × 0.02 × 640
= **40.96 mg/L ≈ 41 mg/L**

*Interpretation:* 41 mg/L free SO₂ is within the legally permitted range (EU maximum for dry red wine: 150 mg/L total SO₂; for dry white: 200 mg/L total; free SO₂ target for microbial stability: 25–35 mg/L). However, the optimal free SO₂ depends on pH: at pH 3.2, 41 mg/L free SO₂ provides approximately 41 × 0.08 = 3.3 mg/L molecular SO₂ (protective level for wine stability ≥ 0.5 mg/L molecular SO₂). This wine is well-protected. At pH 3.8, the same 41 mg/L free SO₂ provides only 41 × 0.03 = 1.2 mg/L molecular SO₂ — still protective but less margin. This shows why both free SO₂ and pH must be considered together when assessing antimicrobial protection.

**Q31. A fermentation starts at 22°Brix and ends at −1.5°Brix. Calculate the sugar consumed and theoretical ethanol produced.**

Initial Brix = 22°Brix → approximately **220 g/L** total sugar (sucrose equivalent at 10 g/L per °Brix).

Final Brix = −1.5°Brix → a reading below 0 reflects that alcohol (less dense than water) has lowered the hydrometer reading below the water reference. Actual residual sugar is approximately **2–5 g/L** (below the threshold for dry wine detection). For calculation, assume residual sugar = 3 g/L (typical for a complete fermentation reading −1 to −2°Brix).

Sugar consumed = 220 − 3 = **217 g/L**

**Theoretical ethanol** = sugar consumed × 0.511 (Gay-Lussac factor)
= 217 × 0.511 = **110.9 g/L**

Convert to % v/v: 110.9 g/L ÷ 7.89 (density correction: 1 mL ethanol = 0.789 g) = **14.1% v/v ethanol**

Actual ethanol is typically 90–95% of theoretical (some carbon to biomass, glycerol, CO₂): Practical ethanol ≈ 110.9 × 0.92 = 102 g/L ≈ **12.9% v/v** — consistent with typical table wine.

**Q32. How do you calculate total acidity (TA) of a wine sample?**

*Given:* 10 mL wine titrated with 0.1 N NaOH; volume used = 9.8 mL.

**Total Acidity (g/L as tartaric acid) = V_NaOH (mL) × N_NaOH × MW_tartaric/2 × 1000 / V_wine (mL)**

MW tartaric acid = 150.09 g/mol; it is diprotic (2 COOH groups), so equivalent weight = 150.09/2 = 75.045.

TA = 9.8 × 0.1 × 75.045 × (1/10) = 9.8 × 0.1 × 7.5045 = **7.35 g/L** as tartaric acid

*Alternatively:* TA (g/L tartaric) = V_NaOH (mL) × N_NaOH × 7.5 / V_wine (mL) [simplified factor for 10 mL sample]
= 9.8 × 0.1 × 7.5 / 10 = **7.35 g/L**

*Interpretation:* TA of 7.35 g/L as tartaric acid is within the normal range for white wine (6–8 g/L); typical red wine TA is slightly lower (5–7 g/L). Very low TA (<4 g/L) indicates flat, flabby wine susceptible to microbial spoilage; very high TA (>9 g/L) gives excessively sour wine. Combined with the measured pH (should be 3.2–3.4 for this TA), this wine appears well-balanced.

**Q33. What is the potential alcohol calculation from Brix and how does it compare to actual measured alcohol?**

**Potential alcohol (% v/v) = °Brix × conversion factor**

Common conversion factors: 0.55 (conservative; accounts for real fermentation losses); 0.576 (theoretical, Gay-Lussac-based); 0.59 (used in some Australian/US practices for high-Brix musts).

*Example:* Grape juice at 24°Brix:
- Conservative: 24 × 0.55 = **13.2% v/v**
- Theoretical: 24 × 0.576 = **13.8% v/v**

The actual alcohol measured (by distillation or GC) is typically 90–95% of the theoretical potential because: ~5–8% of sugar carbon goes to biomass (yeast cell mass); ~3–5% to glycerol; ~1–2% to acetic acid, succinic acid, and other by-products; ~0.5% to CO₂ from biosynthetic reactions not accounted for in simple stoichiometry. The difference between potential alcohol (from Brix) and actual alcohol (from distillation) is called the **fermentation efficiency** or **conversion factor**: **Fermentation efficiency = Actual alcohol/Potential alcohol × 100%**. A value of 92–95% indicates an excellent, complete fermentation; <85% suggests fermentation problems (stuck, stuck partial, major by-product formation).

**Q34. Calculate the amount of KMS to add to achieve 50 mg/L free SO₂ in 3 liters of wine, knowing that 1 g KMS releases 570 mg SO₂.**

Target: 50 mg/L free SO₂ in 3 L wine.

Total SO₂ needed = 50 mg/L × 3 L = **150 mg SO₂**

KMS required = 150 mg SO₂ ÷ 570 mg SO₂ per g KMS = 0.263 g KMS

= **0.263 g = 263 mg KMS** to add to 3 liters of wine.

Practical note: Weigh accurately on an analytical balance (±0.001 g). Dissolve KMS in a small amount (5–10 mL) of wine first, then add the dissolved KMS solution to the bulk wine and mix thoroughly. This ensures even distribution. Because current free SO₂ in the wine is likely >0 (some residual from previous additions), the target is the increment needed: if current free SO₂ = 15 mg/L and target = 40 mg/L: SO₂ to add = (40 − 15) × 3 L = 75 mg; KMS = 75/570 = 0.132 g. Always measure free SO₂ before and 24 hours after addition to confirm the achieved level.

**Q35. How do you interpret a wine color measurement at 420, 520, and 620 nm?**

Wine color analysis by spectrophotometry (Glories method or OIV method for red wine): prepare wine sample (1:1 or 1:4 dilution in tartaric acid buffer pH 3.2 if necessary to bring absorbance to measurable range); read absorbance at 420 nm (A₄₂₀; yellow/brown tones), 520 nm (A₅₂₀; red tones from anthocyanins), and 620 nm (A₆₂₀; blue/violet tones).

**Color Intensity (CI)** = A₄₂₀ + A₅₂₀ + A₆₂₀
**Hue (Tint)** = A₄₂₀ / A₅₂₀

*Interpretation:*
- Young red wine: CI = 10–20 (undiluted); high A₅₂₀ (high anthocyanin); Hue = 0.5–0.7 (predominantly red)
- Aged red wine: CI may increase (anthocyanin-tannin polymers) but A₅₂₀ decreases as monomeric anthocyanins polymerize; A₄₂₀ increases (tawny/brown shift); Hue increases to 0.8–1.2 or higher (more orange/tawny)
- Browning/oxidation: A₄₂₀ disproportionately high relative to A₅₂₀; hue > 1.0 in young wine suggests oxidative spoilage
- For white wine: color measured at 420 nm only (no significant A₅₂₀/A₆₂₀); high A₄₂₀ indicates browning/oxidation

*Example:* A₄₂₀ = 0.45, A₅₂₀ = 0.85, A₆₂₀ = 0.12 (1:4 dilution, multiply by 4 for undiluted):
CI = (0.45 + 0.85 + 0.12) × 4 = **5.68** (undiluted values)
Hue = 0.45/0.85 = **0.53** — this is a young, deeply red wine with good anthocyanin content.

**Q36. A wine has a titratable acidity of 6.5 g/L as tartaric acid and pH 3.6. After malolactic fermentation, TA drops to 5.0 g/L and pH rises to 3.8. Explain the biochemical changes and calculate the malic acid converted.**

Biochemical explanation: MLF converts L-malic acid (MW 134.09 g/mol; diprotic: pKa₁ 3.46, pKa₂ 5.10) to L-lactic acid (MW 90.08 g/mol; monoprotic: pKa 3.86) + CO₂. The conversion reduces total titratable acidity because lactic acid has only one ionizable proton at wine pH, versus malic acid contributing approximately 1.5 protons per molecule at pH 3.6 (between first and second pKa). The pH rise (3.6 → 3.8) reflects the loss of the stronger buffering effect of diprotic malic acid.

TA reduction = 6.5 − 5.0 = **1.5 g/L** as tartaric acid equivalents

To convert to malic acid: TA is expressed as tartaric acid (MW 150.09, diprotic; equivalent weight = 75.05). Malic acid contribution to TA (expressed as tartaric acid) = malic acid (g/L) × (75.05/67.05) where 67.05 is malic acid equivalent weight (134.09/2). Therefore: malic acid converted (g/L) = TA reduction × 67.05/75.05 = 1.5 × 0.893 = **1.34 g/L malic acid** consumed in MLF. Lactic acid produced = 1.34 × (90.08/134.09) = **0.90 g/L** lactic acid produced. CO₂ released = 1.34 × (44.01/134.09) = **0.44 g/L** CO₂ evolved (explains the light spritz observed during MLF).

### PART D: Troubleshooting (Q37–Q44)

---

**Q37. Fermentation does not start after 48 hours of yeast inoculation. What are the possible causes and remedies?**

No fermentation after 48 hours (no CO₂, no Brix drop, no yeast growth) suggests: (1) *SO₂ toxicity* — if too much KMS was added at must preparation (>100 mg/L free SO₂), even the commercial *S. cerevisiae* starter may be inhibited. Wait 24–48 hours more for SO₂ to dissipate (evaporation from open top during settling); or inoculate a fresh yeast culture. (2) *Dead/non-viable starter yeast* — active dry yeast may be old, stored incorrectly (high temperature), or rehydrated in water that was too hot (>45°C kills yeast) or too cold (<30°C does not activate properly). Re-rehydrate fresh yeast at 38–40°C. (3) *Temperature too low* — at <10°C, yeast fermentation is negligible; ensure fermentation vessel is at 18–22°C; verify thermometer. (4) *Temperature too high* — above 42°C kills most yeast; check for heat source proximity. (5) *Missing nutrients* — very low-nutrient juice (some commercial filtered juices) may lack nitrogen and vitamins; add DAP 200 mg/L and thiamine 0.5 mg/L. (6) *Preservatives in juice* — commercial juice with potassium sorbate (E202) or benzoate (E211) inhibits yeast; these are often on the label; use preservative-free juice. (7) *High osmotic stress* — if sugar was over-adjusted above 28°Brix, osmotic stress inhibits yeast germination; dilute to 22–24°Brix. (8) *Low initial pH* — below pH 2.8, even *S. cerevisiae* struggles to initiate growth; adjust to pH 3.2 with KHCO₃.

---

**Q38. Wine smells like rotten eggs (H₂S). What causes this and how is it treated?**

H₂S (hydrogen sulfide, rotten egg odor, threshold: 1–10 μg/L) is one of the most common wine off-odors: Causes: (1) *Nitrogen deficiency* — when YAN is insufficient (<150 mg N/L), yeast cannot synthesize sufficient sulfur amino acids (cysteine, methionine) through the normal pathway and instead produce H₂S from sulfate reduction as an alternative sulfur source for cysteine/methionine synthesis; the sulfide cannot be fully incorporated and is released as H₂S. (2) *Elemental sulfur* — residual sulfur from vineyard fungicide sprays (sulfur is widely used for powdery mildew control); yeast reduces elemental sulfur (S⁰) → H₂S directly. (3) *High SO₂ concentration* — excess SO₂ can be reduced to H₂S by some yeast strains (particularly those with high sulfite reductase activity). (4) *Reductive conditions in lees* — prolonged contact with dead yeast lees generates H₂S from amino acid catabolism (L-cysteine → pyruvate + NH₃ + H₂S). Treatment: (1) *Splash rack* — vigorously splash wine during racking to aerate; O₂ oxidizes H₂S → elemental sulfur (precipitates) or thiol polymers; most effective if done immediately when H₂S first detected. (2) *Copper treatment* — add 0.1–0.5 mg/L CuSO₄ solution; Cu²⁺ + H₂S → Cu₂S (insoluble, black precipitate) → settles out; re-rack after 24 hours; legal maximum: 0.5 mg/L residual copper in finished wine (EU Reg. 2019/934). (3) *Prevention* — adequate YAN before fermentation; wash grapes to remove sulfur spray residues; avoid excessive SO₂ addition.

---

**Q39. The finished wine is hazy despite racking and settling. What are the possible causes and how do you clarify it?**

Wine haze has multiple possible causes: (1) *Protein haze* — most common in white wine; grape proteins (PR proteins: thaumatin-like, chitinases) precipitate when warmed; detected by heat test (80°C, 30 min → haze forms). Treatment: bentonite fining (Q19). (2) *Pectin haze* — did not use pectinase; pectin forms colloidal gel haze. Treatment: add commercial pectinase; allow 24–48 hours; settle and rack. (3) *Tartrate crystals* — white crystalline particles of potassium bitartrate; cold-stabilize at −4°C for 1–2 weeks; filter. (4) *Yeast haze* — active yeast cells still in suspension; centrifuge or membrane filter (0.65 μm) removes yeast cells; alternatively, add bentonite (1 g/L) which flocculates yeast; chill to 4°C to improve settling. (5) *Microbial haze (re-fermentation or bacterial growth)* — if sweet wine re-ferments (residual sugar + live yeast) or if LAB grow: check Brix (rising = re-fermentation); add 50 mg/L free SO₂; centrifuge and sterile filter (0.45 μm). (6) *Copper haze* — excess CuSO₄ addition forms copper protein precipitates; treat with bentonite. (7) *Iron/phosphate haze* — iron in wine >10 mg/L forms iron-phosphate (Fe₃(PO₄)₂, white haze) or iron-tannate (Fe-tannin complex, blue-black haze "casse bleue"); treat by adding citric acid (chelates iron) or by blue fining (potassium ferrocyanide — legal in some countries only; precipitates all excess iron as Prussian blue complex, filtered off).

**Q40. The wine has a vinegar-like smell (excessive acetic acid/volatile acidity). What organisms are responsible and how is this prevented or treated?**

Volatile acidity (VA) exceeding 0.8 g/L (white) or 1.2 g/L (red) expressed as acetic acid indicates spoilage: Responsible organisms: (1) *Acetobacter aceti, A. pasteurianus* — the primary acetic acid bacteria (AAB); obligate aerobes; oxidize ethanol → acetic acid + water via ethanol dehydrogenase (ADH) and acetaldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) when oxygen is present; optimal pH 5.4–6.3, tolerate wine pH. (2) *Gluconobacter oxydans* — oxidizes glucose → gluconic acid in must before fermentation; also contributes acetic acid. (3) *Hanseniaspora uvarum* and other non-*Saccharomyces* yeasts — produce acetic acid and ethyl acetate during early fermentation (especially under aerobic or warm conditions). (4) *Brettanomyces bruxellensis* — can also produce small amounts of acetic acid alongside phenolic off-compounds. Prevention: (a) Exclude oxygen — maintain wine under inert gas (CO₂ blanket); minimize headspace; use appropriate vessel size; sulfite at all racking stages; (b) SO₂ at adequate levels (molecular SO₂ > 0.5 mg/L); (c) Low fermentation temperature during early (most-aerobic) stages; (d) Good sanitation to prevent AAB contamination. Treatment: once acetic acid is present, it cannot be removed without specialized treatment (reverse osmosis with selective membrane; vacuum evaporation — VA lost faster than ethanol at low pressure; both are industrial procedures not feasible in laboratory setting). Prevention is the only practical strategy in laboratory wine production.

**Q41. The red wine color is brown/orange rather than deep red. What could cause this?**

Premature browning in red wine indicates: (1) *Oxidative browning* — most common cause; anthocyanins are oxidized by O₂ (directly or via enzymatic polyphenol oxidase — PPO in must, or via iron-catalyzed Fenton reaction in wine); monomeric anthocyanins (red at wine pH) are converted to oxidized forms (yellow/brown). Prevention: SO₂ addition at crushing (PPO inhibitor); oxygen exclusion throughout. (2) *Insufficient maceration* — if grape skin contact was too short, insufficient anthocyanins were extracted; dilute color is more easily browned. (3) *High pH fermentation* — at pH > 3.8, anthocyanins exist predominantly in the colorless/bluish carbinol or quinoidal forms rather than the red flavylium cation form; wine appears pale or brown-red. Adjust pH to 3.2–3.5. (4) *Low SO₂* — SO₂ both directly bleaches (reversibly) some anthocyanin forms and prevents oxidation; without SO₂, anthocyanins degrade rapidly. Add KMS to maintain 25–35 mg/L free SO₂. (5) *High temperature fermentation* — above 35°C, anthocyanins degrade thermally; ferment red wine at 25–30°C maximum. (6) *Wrong grape variety* — some red grape varieties naturally produce low-colored wine; choose varieties with high anthocyanin content (Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, Merlot) for deep red laboratory wines. (7) *Old or oxidized grapes* — moldy or oxidized grapes have reduced anthocyanin; use fresh, sound grapes.

**Q42. Bubbles continue to form in bottled wine after 3 weeks. What has happened and what risk does this pose?**

Continued CO₂ production in a sealed bottle indicates re-fermentation (secondary fermentation in the bottle, unintended in still wine): Causes: (1) *Residual sugar* — if wine was bottled before fermentation was complete (Brix not at dry level), residual glucose and fructose are substrates for any surviving yeast. (2) *Viable yeast in bottle* — if sterile filtration (0.45 μm) was not performed or a filter was compromised, viable *S. cerevisiae* or *Zygosaccharomyces bailii* (a contaminating osmotolerant wild yeast) survived bottling. (3) *MLF in bottle* — *Oenococcus oeni* surviving in bottle initiates MLF; CO₂ produced from malic acid conversion; less volume of gas but still creates bubbling. Risk assessment: (a) *Bottle overpressure* — CO₂ produced (up to 6 atmospheres in Champagne-style re-fermentation) can cause corks to be blown or, in worst case, glass bottles to rupture (particularly if bottles are heated); risk is limited for limited re-fermentation. (b) *Spoilage* — off-flavors, turbidity, CO₂-induced spritz in a still wine; commercial failure. (c) *Sorbate off-flavor* — if potassium sorbate was incorrectly used instead of sterile filtration; LAB can convert sorbate to 2-ethoxyhexa-3,5-diene ("geranium off-flavor"). Prevention: ensure complete fermentation before bottling; sterile filter (0.45 μm) to remove all yeast; maintain adequate SO₂ (35 mg/L free SO₂).

**Q43. The wine smells and tastes "mousy." What is this defect, what causes it, and how is it detected?**

Mousy taint is one of the most difficult wine defects to detect and remediate: Cause: produced by lactic acid bacteria (*Lactobacillus hilgardii*, *L. brevis*, *Pediococcus* spp.) or *Brettanomyces/Dekkera* through the conversion of amino acids (lysine, ornithine) in wine to N-heterocyclic compounds: 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline (2-APy), 2-acetyltetrahydropyridine (2-ATHP), and 2-ethyltetrahydropyridine (2-ETHP). The threshold is very low (≈1.6 μg/L for 2-APy). Detection: the unique feature of mousy taint is that it is not perceived in the mouth during normal sipping because at wine pH (3.0–3.5), the compounds are protonated and non-volatile. However, when wine is held in the mouth and mixed with saliva (raising pH to 6.5–7.0 in the palate), the compounds are deprotonated, become volatile, and the mousy odor is perceived as an aftertaste. Simple detection: rub a small amount of wine between the palms or on the back of the hand; the alkaline skin surface deprotonates the compounds; sniff immediately — mousy odor confirms the defect. Prevention: maintain adequate SO₂; avoid excessive LAB activity (especially post-MLF without SO₂ replenishment); use sanitary practices; 0.45 μm filtration post-MLF. Treatment: no effective remediation once present in wine; prevention is the only strategy.

**Q44. You observe a white film forming on the surface of wine in an incompletely filled vessel. What is this and what action should you take?**

A white or cream-colored film on the wine surface (flor, voile, or kahm yeast) indicates: (1) *Film-forming yeasts (flor yeasts)* — *Candida stellata*, *Pichia membranifaciens*, *Debaryomyces hansenii*, and specialized film *S. cerevisiae* strains (used intentionally in Sherry/Fino production) form a thick, wrinkled film on the wine surface under aerobic conditions. They aerobically oxidize ethanol → acetaldehyde + water (acetaldehyde gives the nutty/flor character in Sherry), and can consume glycerol and other compounds, making the wine more dilute and oxidized. This is serious spoilage in still table wine (excessive acetaldehyde formation gives "sherry-like" or "flat/nutty" off-character). (2) *Acetic acid bacteria film* — a thin, silky film may be early *Acetobacter* growth; will progress to vinegar. Immediate actions: (a) Check free SO₂ (likely depleted below 20 mg/L — film yeasts and AAB are more SO₂ tolerant but still inhibited at adequate SO₂ levels); add KMS to raise free SO₂ to 50 mg/L. (b) Remove film by carefully skimming surface with a sterile spoon or by careful siphoning from below the film. (c) Top up the vessel with wine of the same type (topping up) to eliminate headspace — the primary enabling factor for film formation. Maintain wine under inert gas (CO₂ or N₂ blanket) in subsequent storage. (d) Sulfite sanitize the vessel top. (e) Monitor taste and VA every 2 weeks.

### PART E: Applications & Interpretation (Q45–Q50)

**Q45. What are the different types of wine (red, white, rosé, sparkling, fortified, dessert) and how do their production processes differ?**

(1) *Still red wine* — skin-contact fermentation of red/black grapes; phenolic extraction during maceration; aged in oak and/or bottle; 11–15% ethanol. (2) *Still white wine* — fermentation of pressed white (or carefully handled red) grape juice without skin contact; clean, aromatic style; 10–14% ethanol. (3) *Rosé wine* — brief skin contact (2–24 hours) with red grape skins before pressing and fermenting as white wine; or blending small amount of red wine into white wine (only permitted in Champagne AOP for rosé sparkling). (4) *Sparkling wine (Champagne method — méthode champenoise)* — base wine fermented dry; then undergoes secondary fermentation in sealed bottle with addition of liqueur de tirage (wine + sugar + yeast + fining agents); CO₂ produced is trapped (6 atm); yeast cells riddled (remuage) to neck; disgorged (dégorgement — freezing of neck, removing frozen yeast plug); dosage (liqueur d'expédition: wine + sugar) added to adjust sweetness; recorked. (5) *Sparkling wine (Charmat/tank method)* — secondary fermentation in sealed pressurized tank rather than individual bottles; less complex, more aromatic (Prosecco, Cava, most commercial sparkling). (6) *Fortified wine (Port, Sherry, Madeira)* — grape spirit (brandy; ~77% ethanol) added during (Port) or after (dry Sherry) fermentation to halt fermentation and raise alcohol to 15–22% v/v. (7) *Dessert wine* — naturally high residual sugar from late-harvest, botrytized (Sauternes, Tokaj), dried (Amarone), or ice wine (Eiswein) grapes; fermentation stops naturally when yeast are inhibited by combination of ethanol and osmotic stress; RS 45–400 g/L.

**Q46. What is the role of oak in wine production and what compounds does oak contribute?**

Oak maturation (in barrels or via staves/chips) is a multi-functional process that transforms wine through: (1) *Micro-oxygenation* — oak wood is permeable to O₂ (diffuses through barrel staves at 20–45 mg O₂/L/year); controlled oxygen exposure promotes tannin polymerization (harsh monomeric tannins → softer polymeric tannins), stabilizes color (anthocyanin-tannin co-pigmentation), and reduces reductive off-flavors. (2) *Oak-derived flavor compounds extracted from wood:* Vanillin (vanilla note; from lignin degradation during toasting); Eugenol (clove/spice; from phenolic acid in wood); Guaiacol (smoky; from lignin pyrolysis during toasting); Oak lactones (cis and trans β-methyl-γ-octalactone; "coconut" and "oak" notes; major oak volatile); Furfural and 5-methylfurfural (from hemicellulose toasting; caramel/toasted notes); Ellagitannins (wood tannins — castalagin, vescalagin; antioxidant; contribute to wine longevity and mouthfeel). (3) *Tannin addition* — ellagitannins from oak precipitate with wine proteins (fining effect) and polymerize with grape tannins, modifying texture. (4) *Evaporation* — 2–5% of wine volume evaporates per year through barrel (the "angel's share"); concentrates wine. Oak character depends on: species (Quercus robur — French limousin, wider grain, more tannin; Q. petraea — French/Allier, tighter grain, more aroma; American Q. alba — intense vanilla, coconut); seasoning (2–3 year air-drying reduces harsh tannins); toast level (light: fruity; medium: vanilla/spice; heavy: smoke/char/coffee).

**Q47. What is the health significance of wine, particularly resveratrol and the "French Paradox"?**

The "French Paradox" refers to the epidemiological observation (first described by Renaud and de Lorgeril, 1992) that despite high saturated fat consumption in France, cardiovascular disease mortality was lower than predicted, correlating with moderate red wine consumption (2–3 glasses per day). The proposed explanation centered on polyphenolic compounds in red wine, particularly: (1) *Resveratrol (trans-3,5,4'-trihydroxystilbene)* — a stilbene phytoalexin produced by grape skins in response to fungal infection or UV stress; present at 0.1–15 mg/L in red wine (higher in wines from grapes under pathogen pressure; Pinot Noir > Cabernet Sauvignon). In vitro and animal studies show: SIRT1 activation (deacetylase; mimics caloric restriction; extends lifespan in yeast and mice); inhibition of platelet aggregation; anti-inflammatory (NF-κB pathway inhibition); antioxidant; potential anti-cancer (apoptosis induction in cancer cells). Clinical human evidence for resveratrol is weaker and concentrations achievable from wine are likely sub-therapeutic for most proposed mechanisms. (2) *Quercetin, catechin, epicatechin, anthocyanins* — cardioprotective through antioxidant, anti-platelet, and anti-inflammatory mechanisms. Current scientific consensus: moderate red wine consumption (1–2 glasses per day; 10–14 g ethanol/day) is associated with reduced CVD risk in observational studies, but ethanol itself accounts for some benefit (HDL increase; antiplatelet effect); confounders (diet, lifestyle) complicate interpretation. No cardiovascular regulatory body recommends starting drinking wine for health reasons.

**Q48. How does the winemaking process differ for country wines (non-grape fruit wines) compared to grape wine?**

Country wines (fruit wines, herb wines, honey wines) follow the same biochemical principles as grape wine but require specific adjustments: (1) *Sugar adjustment (chaptalisation)* — most fruits have insufficient sugar for adequate alcohol (apple juice ~11-12°Brix; mango ~12-15°Brix; strawberry ~8-10°Brix); sucrose is added to reach 22–24°Brix for 12–13% v/v target alcohol. (2) *Acid adjustment* — non-grape fruits often have different acid profiles: apple juice — malic acid (like grape, but much higher concentration; may need deacidification or partial malic acid retention); tropical fruits — citric acid dominant; berry fruits — citric + malic; target pH 3.0–3.5 for microbial stability. Tartaric acid not appropriate for non-grape wines (not natural); use citric acid (for raising acidity) or KHCO₃ (for lowering acidity). (3) *Nutrient supplementation* — non-grape fruit juices typically have lower YAN than grape must; liberal DAP addition (300–500 mg/L) is routinely needed. (4) *Enzyme treatment* — pectinase is even more critical for soft fruits (strawberry, raspberry, peach) which are more pectin-rich than grapes. (5) *Water addition* — for very concentrated fruits (cherry, elderberry), must is diluted with water to achieve desired acid and sugar balance. (6) *Fermentation yeast* — wine yeast strains (EC1118, Lalvin 71B) work well for most fruit wines; some specialized fruit wine yeasts are available. (7) *Malolactic fermentation* — generally avoided in fruit wines (already lower acidity after adjustment).

**Q49. What is the principle and significance of Botrytis cinerea (noble rot) in premium dessert wine production?**

*Botrytis cinerea* is a gray mold fungus (Ascomycota; Leotiomycetes) that infects grape berries in two modes: (1) *Gray rot (undesirable)* — under wet conditions, *B. cinerea* invades damaged berries rapidly and destructively; produces laccase enzyme that oxidizes wine polyphenols (browning); reduces wine quality dramatically. (2) *Noble rot (Botrytis pourriture noble; Edelfäule)* — under specific alternating conditions of humid morning mist (allowing *B. cinerea* spore germination and early hyphal penetration through berry skin without rupturing) followed by warm, dry afternoons (desiccating the berry as the fungus draws water through its hyphae), the berry shrivels and concentrates sugar, acids, and flavor compounds while *Botrytis* enzymatically transforms the berry's chemistry. Biochemical effects of noble rot: (a) Sugar concentration — water loss concentrates glucose + fructose to 250–400 g/L; (b) Acid modification — malic acid is partially consumed by *Botrytis* (malic enzyme), reducing TA; tartaric acid is converted to glucaric acid (a characterful acid in Sauternes); (c) Glycerol production — *Botrytis* produces large amounts of glycerol (10–25 g/L in finished wine) contributing the famous "oily" mouthfeel of Sauternes; (d) Flavor compound production — botrytized wines have distinctive character from: sotolon (4,5-dimethyl-3-hydroxy-2(5H)-furanone; curry/fenugreek/caramel aroma threshold 7 μg/L), phenylacetaldehyde, and other Maillard-type compounds; (e) Laccase must be inactivated by SO₂ immediately after pressing to prevent oxidative browning. Premium botrytized wines: Sauternes-Barsac (France), Tokaji Aszú (Hungary), Trockenbeerenauslese (Germany), Beerenauslese (Germany).

**Q50. Design a complete laboratory experiment for preparing a fruit wine from mango juice, including all stages from must preparation to bottling. Outline the procedure, expected results, and quality parameters.**

**Experimental design: Mango country wine preparation (1 liter scale)**

**Stage 1 — Must preparation:**
Obtain 1 liter fresh mango juice (100% pure, no preservatives; check label). Measure: Brix (target 22°Brix; adjust with sucrose: if 14°Brix, add (22−14)×10 = 80 g sucrose per liter; dissolve thoroughly); pH (target 3.2–3.4; mango juice is typically pH 3.5–4.0; add citric acid monohydrate to reduce pH: 1 g/L reduces pH by approximately 0.15–0.2 units); Titratable acidity (target 6–7 g/L as tartaric acid equivalent). SO₂ treatment: add 0.088 g KMS per liter (50 mg SO₂/L); stir; cover and leave 12 hours.

**Stage 2 — Inoculation and fermentation:**
Rehydrate 0.2 g wine yeast (e.g., EC1118 or Lalvin 71B) in 2 mL warm water (38°C) with 2 mg GoFerm for 15 min. Add DAP (0.2 g/L) and thiamine (0.5 mg/L) to mango must. Transfer must to 2 L glass fermentation jar. Inoculate rehydrated yeast. Fit fermentation lock. Incubate at 22–25°C. Monitor daily: Brix (refractometer); CO₂ evolution rate (bubbles/min from lock). Record Brix and time data in table for fermentation kinetics graph.

**Stage 3 — Fermentation monitoring and completion:**
Fermentation expected: Day 1–2: vigorous CO₂ evolution; Brix drops 2–3°Brix/day. Day 3–5: Brix continues to fall. Day 7–10: CO₂ slows; Brix approaches −1 to −2. Completion confirmed by: Brix stable ≤ −1 for 3 days; CO₂ evolution ceased; Clinitest or DNS showing <2 g/L residual sugar.

**Stage 4 — First racking and SO₂ adjustment:**
Siphon wine off lees into clean, sanitized 1.5 L glass vessel; add KMS (0.044 g/L = 25 mg SO₂/L). Seal with fermentation lock.

**Stage 5 — Clarification:**
Chill at 4°C for 5–7 days for cold settling. If still hazy: add pectinase (0.3 mL/L commercial pectinase) and incubate 24 hours at room temperature; then chill again. Rack off sediment. Optional: add bentonite (1 g/L hydrated slurry; stir; allow 48 hours; rack off bentonite lees).

**Stage 6 — Analytical quality control:**
Measure final wine: Ethanol (% v/v by hydrometer distillation method); pH; TA (NaOH titration); Free SO₂ (Ripper method; target 30–35 mg/L); Residual sugar (DNS or Clinitest); Color (A₄₂₀ for browning); Organoleptic assessment (color, aroma, taste, finish).

**Stage 7 — Bottling:**
Add KMS to reach 30 mg/L free SO₂ in final wine. Filter through 0.45 μm membrane into clean bottles. Cork or cap. Label with: wine type, fruit, production date, student name, % alcohol.

**Expected outcomes:**
- Ethanol: 11.5–13% v/v (from 22°Brix starting material)
- Residual sugar: < 4 g/L (dry wine)
- TA: 5.5–7.0 g/L as tartaric acid equivalent
- pH: 3.2–3.5
- Free SO₂: 28–35 mg/L
- Color: golden yellow, clear
- Aroma: tropical mango esters, floral notes, slight yeast character
- Taste: dry to off-dry, fruity, moderate acidity, clean finish
- Total fermentation time: 10–14 days; clarification: 5–7 days; total: 15–21 days

**Quality criteria for pass/fail:**
- Ethanol ≥ 10% v/v ✓; Residual sugar <10 g/L ✓; No vinegar odor ✓; No turbidity ✓; pH 3.0–3.8 ✓; Free SO₂ 20–50 mg/L ✓.
