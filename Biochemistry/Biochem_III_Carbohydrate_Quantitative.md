# Biochemistry — III. Quantitative Estimation of Carbohydrates
## 50 Viva Questions with Detailed Answers (Master's Level)

---

**1. What is the principle behind the DNS (Dinitrosalicylic acid) method for estimating reducing sugars?**
The DNS method is based on the reduction of 3,5-dinitrosalicylic acid (yellow, in alkaline solution) by the free aldehyde/ketone groups of reducing sugars under boiling conditions, forming 3-amino-5-nitrosalicylic acid, which is reddish-brown in color. The intensity of this color, measured spectrophotometrically (typically at 540 nm), is directly proportional to the concentration of reducing sugar in the sample, allowing quantification against a standard curve.

**2. Why is a standard curve necessary for quantitative carbohydrate estimation, and how is it constructed?**
A standard curve establishes the relationship between known concentrations of a standard sugar (e.g., glucose) and their corresponding absorbance values, which is needed because absorbance alone is not an absolute measure of concentration — it depends on the specific molar absorptivity of the colored product, path length, and reaction conditions. It is constructed by preparing a dilution series of the standard (e.g., 0, 20, 40, 60, 80, 100 µg/mL glucose), subjecting each to the same colorimetric reaction as the test sample, measuring absorbance, and plotting absorbance (y-axis) versus concentration (x-axis) — the resulting linear regression equation is then used to calculate unknown concentrations from their absorbance readings.

**3. State Beer-Lambert's law and explain each term.**
Beer-Lambert's law states A = εcl, where A is absorbance (unitless), ε is the molar absorptivity or extinction coefficient (L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹), c is the concentration of the absorbing species (mol/L), and l is the path length of light through the sample (cm, typically 1 cm for standard cuvettes). The law states that absorbance is directly proportional to concentration (at constant path length and wavelength), forming the theoretical basis for using absorbance to determine unknown concentrations via a standard curve.

**4. What are the limitations of Beer-Lambert's law, and how do they affect carbohydrate estimation?**
Beer-Lambert's law is only linear within a certain concentration range; at high concentrations, deviations occur due to chemical interactions between absorbing molecules (e.g., aggregation, altered molar absorptivity), stray light effects in the spectrophotometer, and changes in refractive index. For carbohydrate estimation, this means samples must be diluted to fall within the linear range of the standard curve — readings outside this range (too high absorbance, typically >1.0-1.2) should be diluted and re-measured rather than extrapolated.

**5. Explain the principle of the phenol-sulfuric acid (Dubois) method for total carbohydrate estimation.**
The phenol-sulfuric acid method involves treating the carbohydrate sample with concentrated sulfuric acid, which hydrolyzes polysaccharides/oligosaccharides to monosaccharides and dehydrates them to furfural (from pentoses) or hydroxymethylfurfural (from hexoses); these furan derivatives then condense with phenol to form a stable yellow-orange colored complex measured at 490 nm (for hexoses) or 480 nm (for pentoses). This method measures total carbohydrate content (both reducing and non-reducing sugars) because the acid hydrolysis converts all sugars to a reactive form.

**6. How does the phenol-sulfuric acid method differ from the DNS method in terms of what it measures?**
The DNS method specifically measures free reducing sugars (those with available aldehyde/ketone groups in their native form), as the alkaline DNS reagent reacts directly with these groups without prior hydrolysis. The phenol-sulfuric acid method measures total carbohydrate content, including non-reducing sugars and polysaccharides, because the strong acid hydrolyzes glycosidic bonds first, converting everything to reactive monosaccharide-derived furan compounds before color development.

**7. What is the role of concentrated sulfuric acid in the phenol-sulfuric acid method, beyond hydrolysis?**
Beyond hydrolyzing glycosidic bonds, concentrated sulfuric acid acts as a dehydrating agent, removing water molecules from the monosaccharide units to form furfural/hydroxymethylfurfural (the chromogenic precursors), and also generates significant heat upon addition to the aqueous sample, which (combined with the acid) drives these dehydration reactions to completion rapidly.

**8. Describe the Anthrone method for carbohydrate estimation and its principle.**
The Anthrone method involves treating the carbohydrate sample with anthrone reagent dissolved in concentrated sulfuric acid; the acid hydrolyzes and dehydrates carbohydrates to furfural/hydroxymethylfurfural derivatives, which then condense with anthrone to form a blue-green colored complex measured at 620 nm (or 630 nm depending on protocol). Like the phenol-sulfuric acid method, it measures total carbohydrates (after acid hydrolysis), making it widely used for estimating total carbohydrate content in plant tissues, food samples, and microbial biomass.

**9. Why must the Anthrone reagent be prepared fresh and protected from light?**
Anthrone reagent (anthrone dissolved in concentrated sulfuric acid, sometimes with thiourea) is unstable and prone to oxidation and decomposition over time, particularly when exposed to light, which can cause the reagent to develop a background color or lose reactivity, leading to inconsistent or inaccurate color development and reduced sensitivity in the assay.

**10. Explain why the reaction tubes in the Anthrone method must be cooled in an ice bath immediately after adding the reagent.**
Adding concentrated sulfuric acid (in the Anthrone reagent) to the aqueous carbohydrate sample generates substantial heat due to the exothermic dilution of concentrated acid; rapid cooling in an ice bath controls this exotherm, prevents excessive or premature charring/side reactions that could produce inconsistent color development, and protects the glassware and the person handling it from heat-related hazards.

**11. What is the difference between the linear range and the dynamic range of an assay?**
The linear range is the concentration range within which absorbance is directly proportional to concentration (following Beer-Lambert's law), allowing simple linear regression for calculations. The dynamic range is the broader range over which the assay can detect a meaningful signal (from the lower limit of detection to the upper limit before saturation), which may extend beyond the linear range if non-linear calibration curves are used, though linear ranges are preferred for simplicity and accuracy in routine quantification.

**12. How would you handle a sample whose absorbance reading falls outside the linear range of your standard curve?**
If the absorbance is too high (above the linear range), the sample should be diluted with an appropriate diluent (water or buffer, matching what was used for standards) by a known dilution factor and re-assayed; the resulting concentration is then multiplied by the dilution factor to obtain the original concentration. If absorbance is too low (below the detection limit), a more concentrated sample or a more sensitive method may be needed, or the result may simply be reported as "below detection limit."

**13. Why is glucose typically used as the standard for carbohydrate estimation, even when estimating total carbohydrates in a complex sample containing multiple sugar types?**
Glucose is widely available in pure form, stable, inexpensive, and serves as a universal reference because most carbohydrate estimation methods give a response (color intensity) that is broadly similar across hexoses; expressing results "as glucose equivalents" provides a standardized, comparable unit even though the actual sample may contain a mixture of different sugars with slightly different molar responses.

**14. What sources of error could lead to an inaccurate standard curve, and how would you identify them?**
Sources of error include inaccurate pipetting of standards (leading to incorrect actual concentrations), degraded standard stock solution (e.g., microbial contamination consuming glucose over time), inconsistent reaction timing/temperature across tubes, reagent degradation, and spectrophotometer calibration drift. These can be identified by checking the R² value of the linear regression (should be ≥0.98-0.99 for good linearity), checking that the curve passes near the origin (zero concentration should give near-zero absorbance after blank subtraction), and repeating the standard curve if results seem inconsistent with expected values.

**15. What is the purpose of running a "blank" in spectrophotometric carbohydrate assays, and how is it used?**
The blank contains all reagents except the carbohydrate sample (replaced with the diluent/water), and is used to "zero" the spectrophotometer, accounting for any inherent absorbance of the reagents themselves at the measurement wavelength. The absorbance of all standards and samples is read against this blank (or the blank's absorbance is subtracted from all readings) to isolate the absorbance specifically due to the carbohydrate-reagent reaction product.

**16. Explain how you would calculate the concentration of glucose in an unknown sample given the standard curve equation y = mx + c.**
Given the standard curve equation (absorbance = m × concentration + c, where m is the slope and c is the y-intercept), measure the absorbance (y) of the unknown sample, then rearrange to solve for concentration: x = (y - c) / m. If the sample was diluted before measurement, multiply this calculated concentration by the dilution factor to obtain the concentration in the original sample.

**17. Why is it important that the unknown sample and the standards undergo identical processing (same reagent volumes, incubation time, temperature)?**
Identical processing ensures that any differences in absorbance between standards and unknowns are due solely to differences in carbohydrate concentration, not due to procedural variables. Since the colorimetric reactions are time- and temperature-dependent (reaction kinetics), even small deviations (e.g., longer incubation for some tubes) could result in different degrees of color development independent of actual concentration, introducing systematic error.

**18. What wavelength would you use to measure absorbance in the DNS method, and why is this specific wavelength chosen?**
The DNS method is typically read at 540 nm (sometimes reported as 500-575 nm depending on protocol variations), because this wavelength corresponds to the absorption maximum (λmax) of the reddish-brown 3-amino-5-nitrosalicylic acid product formed during the reaction — measuring at or near λmax maximizes sensitivity and signal-to-noise ratio for the assay.

**19. How would the presence of proteins in a sample interfere with carbohydrate estimation methods, and how can this be addressed?**
Proteins can interfere by contributing to turbidity (light scattering, causing artificially high absorbance readings), by reacting with certain reagents (e.g., phenol can react with aromatic amino acids), or by being co-precipitated/degraded during acid treatment, potentially contributing additional chromogenic compounds. This can be addressed by deproteinizing the sample first (e.g., using trichloroacetic acid or perchloric acid precipitation, followed by centrifugation to remove the protein pellet before assaying the supernatant).

**20. What is meant by "reducing sugar equivalents" in the context of polysaccharide estimation?**
"Reducing sugar equivalents" refers to expressing the total reducing capacity of a sample (which may contain a mixture of mono- and oligosaccharides of varying sizes) in terms of an equivalent concentration of a standard reducing sugar (typically glucose), based on the color/reduction response — this allows comparison of samples with potentially different molecular compositions on a common basis, though it does not represent the actual molar concentration of any single specific sugar.

**21. Describe the somogyi-nelson method for reducing sugar estimation and how it differs from DNS.**
The Somogyi-Nelson method involves a two-step reaction: first, reducing sugars reduce Cu²⁺ to Cu⁺ (cuprous oxide) in an alkaline copper tartrate reagent (similar to Benedict's reaction); second, the Cu⁺ reduces arsenomolybdate reagent to form a blue-colored molybdenum complex, measured at 500-750 nm. This is more sensitive than DNS (suitable for lower sugar concentrations) but involves more steps and reagents (including toxic arsenic-containing compounds in the traditional protocol), which has led to it being less commonly used in modern labs in favor of DNS or enzymatic methods.

**22. Why might you choose an enzymatic method (e.g., glucose oxidase-peroxidase, GOD-POD) over a chemical method (e.g., DNS or Anthrone) for glucose estimation?**
Enzymatic methods are highly specific — glucose oxidase reacts almost exclusively with glucose (or beta-D-glucose specifically), avoiding interference from other reducing sugars, proteins, or reducing agents that would give false signals in non-specific chemical methods. This specificity is crucial in clinical settings (e.g., blood glucose measurement) where the sample is a complex biological matrix containing many potentially interfering substances.

**23. What is the chemical basis of the GOD-POD method for glucose estimation?**
Glucose oxidase (GOD) catalyzes the oxidation of beta-D-glucose to gluconic acid, producing hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) as a byproduct; peroxidase (POD) then uses this H₂O₂ to oxidize a chromogenic substrate (commonly o-dianisidine or 4-aminoantipyrine with phenol), producing a colored product whose absorbance (typically at 505 nm for the Trinder reaction with 4-AAP/phenol) is proportional to the original glucose concentration.

**24. How would you prepare a series of dilutions for a standard curve ranging from 20 to 100 µg/mL glucose using a 1 mg/mL stock solution?**
From a 1 mg/mL (1000 µg/mL) stock, calculate the volume needed for each target concentration using C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ for a chosen final volume (e.g., 1 mL): for 100 µg/mL, V₁ = (100×1)/1000 = 0.1 mL stock + 0.9 mL diluent; for 80 µg/mL, V₁ = 0.08 mL stock + 0.92 mL diluent; and similarly for 60, 40, and 20 µg/mL (0.06, 0.04, 0.02 mL stock respectively, with corresponding diluent volumes to reach 1 mL total).

**25. What does the slope of the standard curve represent, physically/chemically?**
The slope represents the sensitivity of the assay — specifically, the change in absorbance per unit change in concentration (e.g., absorbance units per µg/mL), which is related to the molar absorptivity of the colored product, the path length of the cuvette, and any dilution/reaction stoichiometry factors. A steeper slope indicates a more sensitive assay (greater absorbance change for a given concentration change), while a shallower slope indicates lower sensitivity.

**26. Why is it important to vortex or mix samples thoroughly during these colorimetric assays?**
Thorough mixing ensures uniform distribution of the sample and reagents throughout the reaction mixture, preventing localized concentration gradients that could lead to incomplete or uneven reactions. Without proper mixing, the colored product might not form uniformly, resulting in inconsistent absorbance readings even from a homogeneous starting sample.

**27. What is meant by "intra-assay" and "inter-assay" variation, and why are they relevant to quantitative carbohydrate estimation?**
Intra-assay variation refers to the variability observed when the same sample is measured multiple times within a single assay run (reflecting pipetting precision, mixing consistency, etc.), while inter-assay variation refers to variability observed when the same sample is measured in different assay runs (on different days, with different reagent batches, etc., reflecting reagent stability and procedural reproducibility). Both are important quality control metrics — low variation indicates a reliable, reproducible assay suitable for quantitative work.

**28. How would you correct for the dilution factor in your final calculation if the sample was diluted 1:5 before the assay?**
After determining the concentration from the standard curve (which gives the concentration of the diluted sample as measured), multiply this value by the dilution factor (5, in this case) to back-calculate the concentration in the original undiluted sample: Original concentration = Measured concentration × Dilution factor.

**29. Why might a researcher choose to express carbohydrate content as "mg/g dry weight" rather than "mg/mL" for a tissue or biomass sample?**
Expressing results as mg/g dry weight normalizes the carbohydrate content to the amount of actual biological material present, accounting for variations in water content between samples (which would affect mg/mL or mg/g fresh weight measurements). This allows meaningful comparison between samples with different moisture contents (e.g., comparing carbohydrate content across plant samples harvested at different times or microbial biomass with different growth-stage water contents).

**30. What is the significance of running the assay in duplicate or triplicate?**
Running samples and standards in duplicate or triplicate allows assessment of measurement precision (by calculating the standard deviation or coefficient of variation between replicates), helps identify outliers due to technical errors (e.g., a pipetting mistake in one replicate), and provides a more reliable average value than a single measurement, increasing confidence in the final reported result.

**31. Explain why heating time and temperature must be precisely controlled in the DNS and Anthrone methods.**
Both methods rely on chemical reactions (reduction/condensation reactions forming colored products) whose rates and equilibrium positions are temperature- and time-dependent; insufficient heating may result in incomplete reaction (underestimating concentration), while excessive heating could cause side reactions, degradation of the colored product, or over-development of color (potentially non-linear with concentration), all of which would compromise the accuracy and reproducibility of the assay if not standardized across all tubes (standards, blanks, and samples).

**32. What is the molar extinction coefficient, and how would you determine it experimentally for a colored product in carbohydrate estimation?**
The molar extinction coefficient (ε) is the absorbance of a 1 M solution of the colored compound at a specific wavelength with a 1 cm path length. It can be determined experimentally by measuring the absorbance of solutions of known concentration of the pure colored compound (or by using the slope of a standard curve where the x-axis is in molar units rather than mass/volume units) and applying A = εcl to solve for ε.

**33. Why is it generally recommended to read absorbance values within a specific time window after the color development reaction, rather than letting samples sit indefinitely?**
The colored products formed in these reactions are often not perfectly stable and may undergo further reactions, oxidation, or fading over time, causing the absorbance to drift (increase or decrease) if measurement is delayed. Reading within a standardized time window (e.g., within 10-15 minutes of cooling) ensures that all samples (standards and unknowns) are measured at a comparable, stable point in the color development/decay curve.

**34. How would you troubleshoot if your sample's calculated concentration came out as a negative value?**
A negative concentration suggests the sample's absorbance was lower than the blank or lower than the y-intercept of the standard curve, which could occur due to: actual carbohydrate concentration being effectively zero or below detection (with the negative value being within the noise/error range around zero), an error in blank subtraction (e.g., using the wrong blank), pipetting errors leading to under-addition of sample, or the standard curve's y-intercept being unusually high due to reagent blank issues — re-running the blank and sample, and checking the y-intercept value, would help identify the cause.

**35. What is the difference between "accuracy" and "precision" in the context of a quantitative carbohydrate assay?**
Accuracy refers to how close a measured value is to the true (actual) concentration of carbohydrate in the sample, often assessed using reference standards or spike-recovery experiments. Precision refers to the reproducibility of repeated measurements of the same sample (how close replicate values are to each other), regardless of whether they are close to the true value — a method can be precise but inaccurate (consistently biased) or accurate on average but imprecise (highly variable).

**36. Why might total carbohydrate estimation (e.g., by Anthrone) give a higher value than reducing sugar estimation (e.g., by DNS) for the same sample?**
Total carbohydrate methods (Anthrone) measure all carbohydrates after acid hydrolysis, including non-reducing sugars (e.g., sucrose) and polysaccharides (e.g., starch, glycogen) that contribute negligibly to reducing sugar assays in their native form. Therefore, if the sample contains significant non-reducing or polymeric carbohydrates, the total carbohydrate value (Anthrone) will be substantially higher than the reducing sugar value (DNS), and the difference represents the non-reducing/polymeric carbohydrate fraction.

**37. How would you estimate the percentage of starch in a flour sample using the Anthrone method?**
Extract the starch from the flour sample (often via solubilization with hot dilute acid or specific starch-extraction protocols), hydrolyze the extracted starch completely to glucose using acid hydrolysis, then estimate the glucose content using the Anthrone method against a glucose standard curve. The total glucose released, multiplied by a conversion factor (0.9, accounting for the loss of water during glycosidic bond formation in the polymer, since starch's molecular weight per glucose unit is approximately 162 versus 180 for free glucose), gives the starch content; this is then expressed as a percentage of the original sample weight.

**38. Why is the conversion factor 0.9 used when converting glucose equivalents to polysaccharide (e.g., starch) content?**
When glucose units polymerize into starch via glycosidic bonds, each linkage formed releases one water molecule (condensation reaction); the molecular weight of a glucose residue within the polymer is therefore 162 (180 - 18 for water) instead of 180 (free glucose). The ratio 162/180 = 0.9 is the conversion factor applied to the measured glucose equivalent to estimate the actual mass of polysaccharide, since the polymer contains less mass per glucose unit than free glucose due to water loss during polymerization.

**39. What quality control measures would you implement in a laboratory routinely performing quantitative carbohydrate assays?**
Quality control measures include: running a standard curve with every batch of assays (not relying on old curves, since reagent activity may change), including a quality control sample of known concentration in each run to verify the assay is performing within expected accuracy, running blanks and replicates for every sample, monitoring spectrophotometer calibration regularly, documenting reagent lot numbers and preparation dates, and establishing acceptance criteria (e.g., R² ≥ 0.99 for standard curves, CV < 5-10% for replicates) before accepting results.

**40. How would interference from colored compounds already present in the sample (e.g., plant pigments) affect carbohydrate estimation, and how can this be corrected?**
Inherent color in the sample (e.g., chlorophyll, anthocyanins in plant extracts) would contribute additional absorbance at the measurement wavelength independent of the carbohydrate-reagent reaction, leading to overestimation of carbohydrate content. This can be corrected by including a sample blank (sample + all reagents except the color-developing reagent, or sample + water instead of the active reagent) to measure the inherent sample color, and subtracting this value from the total absorbance of the fully reacted sample.

**41. Explain the importance of using the same type and brand of cuvettes (or matched cuvettes) when reading absorbance.**
Cuvettes can have slight variations in path length, optical clarity, and surface scratches that affect light transmission; using mismatched cuvettes (e.g., glass for some readings, plastic for others, or cuvettes with different wall thicknesses) introduces systematic errors in absorbance readings that are independent of sample concentration. Matched cuvettes (or consistently using the same cuvette for blank and samples, after rinsing between uses) minimize this source of error.

**42. What would be the effect on your results if the spectrophotometer was not properly zeroed/calibrated before use?**
An improperly zeroed spectrophotometer would introduce a constant offset (systematic error) to all absorbance readings — if it reads a baseline absorbance with no sample/blank in place (or an incorrect blank), this offset would be carried through to all standards and samples, shifting the entire standard curve (changing the y-intercept) and potentially affecting the accuracy of calculated concentrations, though the relative differences (slope) might remain less affected if the offset is constant and additive.

**43. Why is it recommended to prepare fresh standard solutions periodically rather than using a stock standard solution stored for months?**
Standard solutions, especially of reducing sugars like glucose, can degrade over time due to microbial contamination (microorganisms metabolizing the sugar, reducing its concentration), or chemical changes (e.g., mutarotation equilibrium shifts, though this doesn't change total reducing capacity; more relevant is potential oxidation or hydrolysis of other components). Using a degraded standard would shift the standard curve, leading to systematic underestimation of unknown sample concentrations if the standard's true concentration is lower than assumed.

**44. How does sample matrix (e.g., a complex biological extract versus a pure sugar solution) potentially affect the validity of using a simple glucose standard curve?**
The "matrix effect" refers to how other components in a complex sample (proteins, salts, pigments, other organic molecules) can interact with reagents differently than a pure standard solution would, potentially enhancing or suppressing color development independent of the actual carbohydrate concentration. This can be assessed using "spike-recovery" experiments (adding a known amount of standard to the sample matrix and checking if the expected increase in measured concentration is recovered) to validate that the standard curve (prepared in a simple matrix) is applicable to the complex sample matrix.

**45. What is a "recovery experiment," and how is it used to validate a quantitative method?**
A recovery experiment involves adding a known amount of the analyte (e.g., glucose) to a sample (a "spike"), then measuring the total concentration and subtracting the original (unspiked) sample concentration to determine how much of the added analyte was actually recovered/detected. Percent recovery = (measured spiked value - measured unspiked value) / amount added × 100; recovery close to 100% (typically 90-110% is acceptable) indicates the method accurately measures the analyte even in the presence of the sample matrix.

**46. Why might polysaccharide samples require an additional hydrolysis step before quantitative estimation by methods like DNS, while monosaccharide samples do not?**
DNS measures free reducing groups; polysaccharides have a very low proportion of free reducing ends relative to total sugar content (one reducing end per long chain), so directly assaying polysaccharide solutions with DNS would grossly underestimate the total sugar content. An acid or enzymatic hydrolysis step breaks the polysaccharide into its constituent monosaccharide units, each now bearing a free reducing group, making the total sugar content accurately measurable by DNS after hydrolysis.

**47. What is the difference between "total reducing sugars" and "total sugars" in the context of food/biological sample analysis?**
"Total reducing sugars" refers to the sum of all sugars present in their free, reducing form (e.g., free glucose, fructose, maltose, lactose) as measured directly without hydrolysis. "Total sugars" includes both reducing sugars and non-reducing sugars (e.g., sucrose) and is typically determined either by summing separately measured reducing sugar content and (hydrolyzed) non-reducing sugar content, or by direct measurement after complete acid/enzymatic hydrolysis of the entire sample (converting all sugars to reducing form).

**48. How would you determine the concentration of sucrose specifically in a sample that also contains glucose and fructose?**
One approach: measure "reducing sugars before hydrolysis" (gives glucose + fructose, since sucrose doesn't react) using DNS; then perform acid hydrolysis of an aliquot (converting sucrose to glucose + fructose) and measure "reducing sugars after hydrolysis" (gives total of original glucose + fructose + sugars derived from sucrose). The difference between these two values, adjusted by appropriate stoichiometric factors (since hydrolysis of 1 mole sucrose yields 1 mole glucose + 1 mole fructose, with a slight mass increase due to water addition), gives the sucrose concentration.

**49. What practical and statistical considerations determine how many points/concentrations should be included in a standard curve?**
A minimum of 5-6 points (plus a zero/blank) is generally recommended to adequately define a linear relationship and calculate a reliable R² value; points should span the expected concentration range of the unknown samples (ideally with unknowns falling near the middle of the curve, not at the extremes), and points should be evenly spaced (or at least cover low, medium, and high concentrations) to detect any non-linearity across the range.

**50. If you were asked to estimate the carbohydrate content of a microbial culture supernatant during a fermentation study, which method would you choose and why?**
The choice depends on the research question: if monitoring substrate (e.g., glucose) consumption over time, an enzymatic GOD-POD method or DNS (for reducing sugars) would be appropriate due to specificity/simplicity and suitability for repeated time-point sampling. If estimating total carbohydrate utilization (including any polysaccharide substrates or non-reducing sugars), the phenol-sulfuric acid or Anthrone method (which captures total carbohydrates after hydrolysis) would be more comprehensive. Practical considerations (reagent availability, throughput needs, required sensitivity, presence of interfering substances like proteins from the culture medium) would also guide the final choice, often necessitating a deproteinization step regardless of method chosen.
