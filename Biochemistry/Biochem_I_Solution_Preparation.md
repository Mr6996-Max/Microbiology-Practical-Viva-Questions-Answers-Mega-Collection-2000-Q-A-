# Biochemistry — I. Laboratory Solution Preparation
## 50 Viva Questions with Detailed Answers (Master's Level)

---

**1. What is the difference between molarity, molality, normality, and percentage concentration?**
Molarity (M) is moles of solute per liter of solution (mol/L), temperature-dependent because volume changes with temperature. Molality (m) is moles of solute per kilogram of solvent, temperature-independent because mass doesn't change with temperature, making it preferred for precise thermodynamic work. Normality (N) is the gram equivalent weight of solute per liter of solution, accounting for the reactive capacity (valence) of the solute — important for acids, bases, and redox reagents. Percentage concentration can be w/v (g solute per 100 mL solution), w/w (g solute per 100 g solution), or v/v (mL solute per 100 mL solution), and is commonly used for stock reagents like ethanol or formalin.

**2. How do you prepare a 1 M solution of NaCl? Show the calculation.**
Molecular weight of NaCl = 58.5 g/mol. For 1 M solution, 1 mole (58.5 g) is dissolved in enough distilled water to make 1 liter total volume. Practically: weigh 58.5 g NaCl, dissolve in a small volume (~700 mL) of distilled water in a volumetric flask, allow to dissolve completely with stirring, then add water up to the 1000 mL mark, mixing thoroughly. The key principle is "weight in, then make up to volume" — never add water to a fixed weight without using a volumetric flask for accuracy.

**3. Define normality and explain how it differs for H₂SO₄ versus NaOH.**
Normality = (Molarity × number of equivalents per mole). For NaOH, which provides one OH⁻ ion per molecule, 1 M = 1 N. For H₂SO₄, which can donate two H⁺ ions, 1 M H₂SO₄ = 2 N. This matters in acid-base titrations because normality directly reflects reactive capacity — equal volumes of equal normality solutions will exactly neutralize each other regardless of the underlying molarity.

**4. What is a stock solution, and why is it used in the laboratory?**
A stock solution is a concentrated solution prepared in advance that is diluted to working concentrations as needed. It is used to save time and minimize repeated weighing of small quantities of reagent (which introduces weighing error), to ensure consistency across experiments, and to economize on storage space and reagent stability — many reagents are more stable concentrated than dilute.

**5. Explain the dilution formula C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ and its assumptions.**
C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ states that the amount of solute (concentration × volume) remains constant before and after dilution, since dilution only adds solvent, not solute. C₁ and V₁ are the concentration and volume of the stock solution, while C₂ and V₂ are those of the desired diluted solution. The assumption is that volumes are additive (no significant volume change on mixing) and that no solute is lost or gained during dilution — this can break down for solutions where mixing causes contraction/expansion (e.g., concentrated acids with water).

**6. How would you prepare 500 mL of 0.1 M solution from a 1 M stock solution?**
Using C₁V₁ = C₂V₂: (1 M)(V₁) = (0.1 M)(500 mL), so V₁ = 50 mL. Measure 50 mL of the 1 M stock using a pipette or graduated cylinder, transfer to a 500 mL volumetric flask, and add distilled water up to the 500 mL mark, mixing thoroughly to homogenize.

**7. What precautions must be taken while preparing solutions of strong acids like concentrated H₂SO₄?**
Always add acid to water, never water to acid — because the dilution of concentrated H₂SO₄ is highly exothermic, and adding water to acid can cause the water to boil and splash concentrated acid violently (the "exploding acid" hazard). Work should be done slowly, in a fume hood, with appropriate PPE (goggles, acid-resistant gloves, lab coat), using a glass rod to direct the flow along the container wall, and the container should be placed in an ice bath if large volumes are involved to dissipate heat.

**8. What is the significance of using a volumetric flask versus a beaker or graduated cylinder?**
Volumetric flasks are calibrated to a single, highly precise volume (often ±0.05–0.1 mL tolerance) and are used for final dilution to exact volume — essential for molarity-based calculations. Beakers and graduated cylinders have much larger tolerances and are used only for approximate measurements (e.g., measuring solvent to dissolve solute before transfer). Using a beaker for final volume adjustment would introduce significant error into the final concentration.

**9. Define "equivalent weight" and give an example for sulfuric acid.**
Equivalent weight is the molecular weight divided by the valence (number of replaceable H⁺ or OH⁻ ions, or electrons transferred in redox reactions). For H₂SO₄ (molecular weight 98 g/mol, valence 2 since it can donate 2 H⁺), the equivalent weight is 98/2 = 49 g. This means 49 g of H₂SO₄ dissolved in 1 L gives a 1 N solution.

**10. What is meant by a "buffer solution" and why is buffering capacity important?**
A buffer solution resists changes in pH upon addition of small amounts of acid or base, typically composed of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base and conjugate acid) in comparable concentrations. Buffering capacity refers to the amount of acid or base a buffer can neutralize before significant pH change occurs, and depends on both the concentration of the buffer components and how close the solution pH is to the pKa of the weak acid — capacity is maximal when pH = pKa.

**11. Explain the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation and its use in buffer preparation.**
The equation is pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]), relating the pH of a buffer to the pKa of the weak acid and the ratio of conjugate base to acid concentrations. It is used to calculate the precise ratio of the two buffer components needed to achieve a target pH, allowing a microbiologist to prepare, for example, a phosphate buffer at pH 7.4 by calculating the correct ratio of Na₂HPO₄ to NaH₂PO₄.

**12. Why is distilled or deionized water preferred over tap water for solution preparation?**
Tap water contains dissolved ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Cl⁻, etc.), microorganisms, and sometimes chlorine or organic matter that can interfere with reactions, precipitate with reagents (e.g., forming insoluble salts), support unwanted microbial growth in media, or alter pH and ionic strength of the prepared solution. Distilled or deionized water removes these contaminants, ensuring reproducibility and avoiding artifacts in sensitive biochemical assays.

**13. What is the purpose of pH adjustment after preparing a buffer, and what instruments are used?**
Even when calculated amounts of buffer components are weighed correctly, the actual pH may deviate due to purity of reagents, hydration state of salts, or temperature effects, so pH must be measured and fine-adjusted using a calibrated pH meter, adding small amounts of strong acid (HCl) or base (NaOH) dropwise while stirring until the target pH is reached. The pH meter must be calibrated with standard buffers (e.g., pH 4, 7, 10) before use.

**14. How do you calculate the amount of reagent needed to prepare a solution of a given percentage (w/v)?**
For a w/v percentage, the formula is: mass (g) = (% concentration × final volume in mL) / 100. For example, to prepare 250 mL of 10% (w/v) glucose solution: mass = (10 × 250)/100 = 25 g. Dissolve 25 g of glucose in distilled water and make up to 250 mL in a volumetric flask.

**15. What is the difference between "make up to" and "add" when preparing solutions?**
"Make up to" a volume means adjusting the total final volume to the specified amount (e.g., adding water until the total reaches 100 mL), which is essential for accurate concentration calculations. "Add" a volume means simply adding that quantity of liquid without regard to the final total volume, which is appropriate for combining reagents in reactions but not for preparing solutions of defined concentration.

**16. Why must hygroscopic chemicals be handled with special care during weighing?**
Hygroscopic chemicals absorb moisture from the air, which increases their apparent weight and decreases the actual purity/concentration of the active compound, leading to inaccurate solution concentrations. They should be weighed quickly in a desiccated environment, stored in airtight containers with desiccants, and ideally weighed last if multiple reagents are being prepared in sequence.

**17. Explain the principle of serial dilution and its applications in microbiology/biochemistry.**
Serial dilution involves successive dilution of a solution or suspension by a constant factor (e.g., 1:10), each dilution being made from the previous one, producing a logarithmic series of concentrations. It is used to bring concentrated samples into a measurable/countable range (e.g., for bacterial counts via plating, or for constructing standard curves in spectrophotometric assays where the linear range is limited).

**18. How would you prepare a 10⁻⁶ dilution of a bacterial culture using serial dilution?**
Take 1 mL of the original culture and add it to 9 mL of diluent (1:10, giving 10⁻¹), mix thoroughly; transfer 1 mL of this to another 9 mL diluent (10⁻²); continue this process. To reach 10⁻⁶, this 1:10 dilution step must be repeated six times in sequence, using a fresh sterile pipette tip for each transfer to avoid carryover contamination.

**19. What is meant by "molar absorptivity" and why is it relevant when preparing standard solutions for spectrophotometry?**
Molar absorptivity (extinction coefficient, ε) is a constant describing how strongly a chemical species absorbs light at a particular wavelength, expressed in L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹. It is relevant because the concentration of standard solutions used to construct calibration curves must fall within the linear range of Beer-Lambert's law (A = εcl) — too concentrated a standard gives absorbance readings outside the linear range, causing curve distortion.

**20. Describe how you would prepare a phosphate buffer of pH 7.4.**
A phosphate buffer at pH 7.4 is typically prepared by mixing appropriate ratios of monobasic (NaH₂PO₄) and dibasic (Na₂HPO₄) sodium phosphate, since the pKa2 of phosphoric acid (~7.2) is close to 7.4, giving good buffering capacity. The required masses are calculated using the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation for the desired molarity and ratio, both salts are dissolved in distilled water, combined, and the pH is verified and fine-tuned with a calibrated pH meter using dilute HCl or NaOH.

**21. What is the significance of "Analytical Reagent (AR) grade" versus "Laboratory Reagent (LR) grade" chemicals?**
AR (Analytical Reagent) grade chemicals have very high purity (often >99.5%) with documented impurity limits, suitable for precise quantitative analyses, calibration standards, and reference solutions where trace impurities would affect results significantly. LR (Laboratory Reagent) grade chemicals have lower purity specifications and are suitable for general laboratory work, qualitative tests, or where high precision is not critical — using LR grade for quantitative standard solutions could introduce systematic error.

**22. Why is it important to use the correct glassware (Class A vs Class B) for quantitative work?**
Class A glassware has tighter tolerance specifications (smaller permissible error in calibrated volume) and is certified for precise quantitative analytical work, such as preparing standard solutions or performing titrations. Class B glassware has roughly double the tolerance of Class A and is acceptable for general laboratory use where minor volume inaccuracies are tolerable, but using Class B for critical dilutions could introduce cumulative errors affecting final results.

**23. What is meant by "molecular weight" and how is it used in solution preparation calculations?**
Molecular weight (molar mass) is the sum of the atomic weights of all atoms in a molecule, expressed in g/mol, and serves as the conversion factor between mass (grams, which can be weighed) and moles (the unit in which concentration is typically expressed). All molarity-based calculations begin with converting the desired molar concentration to a mass using: mass = molarity × volume (L) × molecular weight.

**24. Explain why temperature affects molarity but not molality.**
Molarity is defined per unit volume of solution, and the volume of a liquid changes with temperature due to thermal expansion or contraction, so the same mass of solute in the same container will yield a slightly different molarity at different temperatures. Molality is defined per unit mass of solvent, and mass does not change with temperature, making molality the preferred unit for precise physical chemistry measurements across temperature ranges.

**25. How do you prepare a saturated solution, and what factors affect saturation point?**
A saturated solution is prepared by adding solute to a solvent until no more dissolves at a given temperature, with excess solid remaining in equilibrium with the dissolved solute. The saturation point (solubility) is affected by temperature (generally increases with temperature for solids), the nature of solute-solvent interactions (polarity, hydrogen bonding), pressure (mainly relevant for gases), and the presence of other dissolved substances that may compete for solvation.

**26. What is the difference between a "primary standard" and a "secondary standard" solution?**
A primary standard is a highly pure, stable compound that can be weighed directly to prepare a solution of precisely known concentration without further calibration (e.g., potassium dichromate, oxalic acid). A secondary standard is a solution whose exact concentration must be determined by titration against a primary standard because the compound itself is not stable enough or pure enough for direct weighing (e.g., NaOH solutions, which absorb CO₂ and moisture from air).

**27. Why must NaOH solutions be standardized before use in titrations?**
NaOH is highly hygroscopic and readily absorbs atmospheric CO₂ to form Na₂CO₃, so a freshly weighed sample of NaOH does not accurately reflect the true amount of active NaOH. Therefore, NaOH solutions must be standardized by titrating against a primary standard acid (e.g., oxalic acid or potassium hydrogen phthalate) to determine their exact normality before use in quantitative titrations.

**28. What is the role of indicators in acid-base titrations used during solution standardization?**
Indicators are substances that change color over a specific pH range, signaling the endpoint of a titration when the moles of acid and base are stoichiometrically equivalent (or close to it). The choice of indicator depends on matching the indicator's color-change pH range to the pH at the equivalence point of the specific acid-base pair being titrated (e.g., phenolphthalein for strong acid-strong base, methyl orange for titrations involving weak bases).

**29. How would you calculate the amount of Na₂CO₃ needed to neutralize a given volume of HCl of known normality?**
Using the equivalence principle (equivalents of acid = equivalents of base), and knowing that the equivalent weight of Na₂CO₃ is half its molecular weight (106/2 = 53 g, since it can neutralize 2 H⁺), the moles of HCl (N × V in liters) are equated to the equivalents of Na₂CO₃ needed, then converted to mass using the equivalent weight.

**30. What is the significance of "specific gravity" when preparing solutions from concentrated liquid reagents (e.g., concentrated HCl)?**
Specific gravity (density relative to water) is needed to convert a percentage concentration (w/w) of a liquid reagent into a usable volume measurement, since concentrated liquid reagents are often labeled by percentage purity and specific gravity rather than molarity. The formula Molarity = (% purity × specific gravity × 1000) / molecular weight allows calculation of the stock molarity, from which the volume needed for a desired dilution can be derived using C₁V₁ = C₂V₂.

**31. Calculate the molarity of concentrated HCl with 37% purity (w/w) and specific gravity 1.19.**
Molarity = (% purity × specific gravity × 1000) / molecular weight = (37 × 1.19 × 1000) / 36.5 ≈ 12.06 M. This stock concentration is then used in C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ to calculate the volume of concentrated HCl needed for any desired dilute HCl solution.

**32. What are common sources of error in solution preparation, and how can they be minimized?**
Common errors include inaccurate weighing (due to uncalibrated balances, hygroscopic absorption, or parallax in reading), incomplete dissolution before making up to volume (causing volume errors when the solid finally dissolves and changes volume), using wrong glassware tolerances, temperature fluctuations affecting volumetric accuracy, and air bubbles in pipettes/burettes. These can be minimized through proper calibration, using appropriate glassware, dissolving completely before final volume adjustment, controlling temperature, and careful technique.

**33. Why is it recommended to dissolve the solute in a smaller volume of solvent first before making up to the final volume in a volumetric flask?**
Dissolving in a smaller volume first ensures complete dissolution can be verified and any heat of dissolution (exothermic or endothermic) can dissipate before final volume adjustment, since volume changes with temperature. If solute were added directly at the final volume mark, incomplete dissolution or temperature-dependent volume changes would result in an inaccurate final concentration.

**34. How do you prepare a 1:1000 dilution, and what does this notation mean?**
A 1:1000 dilution means 1 part of the original solution/substance combined with 999 parts of diluent, giving a total of 1000 parts (i.e., a 1000-fold dilution). Practically, this is prepared by adding 1 mL of stock to 999 mL of diluent, or more commonly through serial dilution (e.g., 1:10 three times, since 10×10×10 = 1000) for ease of accurate measurement with standard pipettes.

**35. What is the difference between "ratio" dilutions (e.g., 1:10) and "fold" dilutions (e.g., 10-fold)?**
A 1:10 ratio dilution typically means 1 part solute/sample in a total of 10 parts (i.e., 1 part sample + 9 parts diluent), resulting in a 10-fold dilution. The terms are often used interchangeably in casual lab speech, but careful interpretation is needed, especially in published protocols, since some conventions express ratio as sample:diluent (1 part sample to 10 parts diluent = 11-fold dilution) rather than sample:total.

**36. Explain the importance of using calibrated micropipettes and balances in solution preparation.**
Calibrated instruments ensure that the actual delivered volume or measured mass matches the indicated value within an acceptable tolerance, which is critical because even small systematic errors compound across serial dilutions or multi-step preparations, leading to significant deviations in final concentrations. Regular calibration (gravimetric calibration for pipettes, calibration weights for balances) and proper technique (e.g., consistent pipetting speed, avoiding parallax in reading) maintain accuracy and reproducibility.

**37. What is meant by "ionic strength" of a solution, and why might it matter in biochemical experiments?**
Ionic strength is a measure of the concentration of ions in a solution, calculated as I = ½Σ(ciZi²), where ci is the molar concentration and Zi is the charge of each ion species. It matters because high ionic strength can affect protein solubility and folding (salting in/out effects), enzyme activity, electrophoretic mobility, and binding interactions, so buffers for sensitive assays are often formulated to a specific ionic strength.

**38. How would you prepare 100 mL of 5% (v/v) ethanol solution from absolute ethanol?**
Using the v/v formula: volume of ethanol needed = (5/100) × 100 mL = 5 mL. Measure 5 mL of absolute ethanol using a graduated pipette, transfer to a 100 mL volumetric flask, and add distilled water up to the 100 mL mark, mixing gently (note: mixing ethanol and water can cause slight volume contraction, so final adjustment after mixing may be needed for high precision).

**39. What is the purpose of using a desiccator when storing certain prepared reagents or weighed chemicals?**
A desiccator maintains a low-humidity environment using a desiccant (e.g., silica gel, anhydrous CaCl₂), preventing hygroscopic chemicals from absorbing atmospheric moisture during storage, which would otherwise alter their effective concentration/purity over time and compromise the accuracy of solutions prepared from them.

**40. Explain how pH changes with dilution for a buffer solution versus a strong acid/base solution.**
For a well-formulated buffer, pH remains relatively stable upon dilution because the ratio of conjugate base to acid (which determines pH via Henderson-Hasselbalch) is largely preserved during dilution (both components are diluted proportionally). For a strong acid or base, dilution directly reduces the concentration of H⁺ or OH⁻ ions, causing a significant pH shift toward neutrality (each 10-fold dilution shifts pH by approximately 1 unit, until approaching the pH of water itself).

**41. Why is sterilization (e.g., autoclaving) sometimes required for prepared solutions, and what precautions are needed?**
Sterilization is required for solutions used in microbiological media, cell culture, or any application where microbial contamination would compromise results. Precautions include checking that the solution components are heat-stable (some sugars, vitamins, and antibiotics degrade or caramelize under autoclaving and must be filter-sterilized and added aseptically after autoclaving the bulk solution), and ensuring containers are not sealed airtight (to avoid pressure buildup/breakage).

**42. What is the difference between filter sterilization and autoclaving for solution preparation, and when is each used?**
Autoclaving uses high-pressure steam (typically 121°C, 15 psi, 15-20 minutes) to achieve sterilization and is suitable for heat-stable solutions and media. Filter sterilization passes the solution through a membrane filter (commonly 0.22 µm pore size) to physically remove microorganisms without heat, and is used for heat-labile components such as certain vitamins, antibiotics, serum, and some carbohydrates that would degrade under autoclaving conditions.

**43. How do you determine the amount of water of hydration to account for when weighing hydrated salts (e.g., CuSO₄·5H₂O)?**
The molecular weight used in calculations must include the water of hydration, since the hydrated form is the actual physical compound being weighed. For CuSO₄·5H₂O, the molecular weight is 249.7 g/mol (CuSO₄ = 159.6 + 5×18 = 90 = 249.6), not 159.6 g/mol (anhydrous CuSO₄), so using the wrong molecular weight would result in a significant concentration error.

**44. What is meant by "isotonic," "hypotonic," and "hypertonic" solutions, and why is this relevant in biological work?**
Isotonic solutions have the same osmotic pressure (solute concentration) as the reference (e.g., cell cytoplasm or blood plasma — 0.9% NaCl for mammalian cells), causing no net water movement across membranes. Hypotonic solutions have lower solute concentration, causing water to move into cells (potential lysis), while hypertonic solutions have higher solute concentration, causing water to move out of cells (crenation/shrinkage). This is relevant when preparing diluents for blood cells, bacterial suspensions, or any live cell-based assay.

**45. How would you verify the accuracy of a freshly prepared solution?**
Accuracy can be verified through titration against a known standard (for acids/bases), measuring pH with a calibrated pH meter (for buffers), measuring absorbance/refractive index and comparing to expected values, or by gravimetric checks (weighing a known aliquot after evaporation, where applicable). Cross-checking with a reference standard solution run alongside is also good practice.

**46. What is the role of magnetic stirrers and stirring time in solution preparation?**
Magnetic stirrers provide consistent, gentle agitation that promotes complete and uniform dissolution of solutes without introducing contamination (unlike manual stirring with a rod) and without the vigorous aeration that could occur with shaking (which may cause foaming for proteins/surfactants or oxidation of sensitive compounds). Adequate stirring time ensures homogeneity, which is critical before any volume adjustment or pH measurement.

**47. Explain the concept of "working solution" versus "stock solution" with an example relevant to biochemistry labs.**
A stock solution is a concentrated, stable form prepared and stored for extended periods (e.g., 10X Tris-EDTA buffer, or 1 M Tris-HCl stock). A working solution is the diluted, ready-to-use concentration prepared fresh (often immediately before use) from the stock by appropriate dilution (e.g., 1X TE buffer diluted from 10X stock), often because the working concentration is less stable or because preparing fresh working solutions ensures activity/accuracy for sensitive assays.

**48. Why might you adjust the pH of a buffer at the temperature at which it will actually be used, rather than at room temperature?**
The pKa of weak acids/bases is temperature-dependent (e.g., Tris buffer pKa changes by approximately -0.03 pH units per °C increase), so a buffer adjusted to pH 7.4 at room temperature (25°C) may have a different pH at 4°C (cold room) or 37°C (incubation temperature). For temperature-sensitive applications (e.g., enzyme assays at 37°C), the pH should be adjusted at the temperature of intended use for accuracy.

**49. What labeling information is essential on a prepared reagent bottle, and why?**
Essential information includes the name and concentration of the reagent, date of preparation, preparer's initials, expiry/use-by date, storage conditions (if special, e.g., 4°C, light-sensitive), and any hazard symbols if applicable. This ensures traceability, prevents use of degraded or expired reagents, allows troubleshooting if results are unexpected, and ensures safety for all lab personnel handling the reagent.

**50. If your prepared solution's pH or concentration doesn't match the expected value, what troubleshooting steps would you take?**
First, verify the calculation (molecular weight, dilution formula, units) for arithmetic errors. Second, check the balance and pH meter calibration status. Third, confirm the purity/grade and hydration state of the chemical used (e.g., anhydrous vs hydrated form). Fourth, check the volumetric glassware used and whether the solute fully dissolved before volume adjustment. Finally, check for contamination of stock reagents (e.g., CO₂ absorption in NaOH, evaporation changing concentration of stored solutions) and re-standardize against a known reference if discrepancies persist.
